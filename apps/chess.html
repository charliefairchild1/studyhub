The Method — how to think, every move

A full routine you run in your head, no engine — like the Doomsday method, but for chess. Six phases. The core is the 3 Sweeps (4–6); phases 0–3 set them up. Do it in order every move; most blunders die in the first two phases.

0
READ orient ~3s
Whose king is safer? Material count? Where is the play — which wing, which weak squares/files? Name the imbalance before touching a piece.
e.g. "Both castled kingside, material equal, the d-file is open and my knight eyes d5 — play is in the centre."
1
THREATS what changed ~2–4s
What did their last move actually do — attack, pin, or open something? Never reply before you know the new threat.
e.g. they play Bg4 — it pins my Nf3 to the queen. That's the threat; deal with it before my own plans.
2
CANDIDATES — forcing first generate ~5–10s
List moves in order: Checks → Captures → Threats (CCT), then quiet improving moves. Forcing moves narrow the tree, so check them first.
e.g. check Qxf7+? capture Nxe5? threat Rd1 hitting the queen? — then the quiet Be3. Four candidates, ranked.
3
FUTURE — point pieces at the king trajectory ~3–5s
A piece is worth what it will do next, and the best future is attacking the enemy king. Simple test for a candidate move: does it put a piece (or aim one) at the squares around their king? Count how many of your pieces already point there — more is better. (Central squares reach the king fastest, which is why the centre matters early.)
e.g. a bishop on c4 already eyes f7 by the black king — it has a future. A bishop on b2 staring at its own pawn does not. Aim it where the king is.
4
SWEEP — Leaks stop dissipation ~3–5s
Every piece, yours and theirs: attacked more than defended? It will drop. Fix your worst leak or take theirs. Loose pieces drop off.
e.g. my bishop on b5 is hit by …a6 and undefended — trade or retreat it now, don't lose it for free.
5
SWEEP — Lifts add transformation ~3–5s
Find your least-active piece and lift it — more reach, toward the centre or their king. The biggest gain is usually improving your worst piece, not your best.
e.g. my rook on a1 does nothing; Re1 onto the open e-file triples its reach.
6
SWEEP — Lanes paths, the Hex idea 2s … 60s+
How many decent moves do you have? Many (wide, quiet) → play the best improving move, fast. Few (sharp, forced) → STOP and calculate the forcing lines to a quiet end, then judge by the by-hand values below. Win by keeping enough winning paths open, not by finding one perfect move.
e.g. 8 sane moves → just play Re1 in 2 seconds. Only Nxe5 holds → spend a full minute calculating it.
7
CHECK before you move last gate ~2–3s
Pretend you've played it: does it hang anything? allow a check, capture, or fork that wins for them? If yes, back to phase 2. This one habit removes most one-move blunders.
e.g. after my Nxe5 — wait, …Qa5+ forks my king and the knight. Back to candidates.

Total ~20–35s on a normal move; phase 6 is where your clock actually goes when it's sharp. In fast games, run 0–1–7 only (read, threats, blunder-check) — that alone stops most losses.

Sharpen the estimate

Piece value by hand — click an example to see it on the board

Forget the fixed 1/3/3/5/9. Rate a piece right now: start from book, then —

+1 controls 2+ centre squares, or reaches the enemy king's zone · +½ a safe outpost (no pawn can kick it)
−1 stuck behind its own pawns / on the rim with no target · −most of it if it's hanging
Trade rule: give a piece up only for one whose by-hand value is ≥ yours — so you trade your bad piece for their good one even at "equal" book material.

Maxims, judged by the lens

The words

Influence = every piece radiates control along the squares it hits (the heatmap). Transformation = how much your army is actually doing (material + reach + centre + king-attack). Dissipation = energy leaking (exposed king, hanging pieces). PTO = Transformation − Dissipation, judged over the whole game, not the next bite.

Process > outcome — the ethics of a move

A bad move that wins is still bad — it was just lucky it ended well. A move's quality is whether it kept your winning paths open given what you could know, not whether the game happened to go your way. Judging a decision by its result is called resulting, and it's how people fool themselves — in chess and in life. The mirror holds too: a sound move that loses to a fluke was still right (that's moral luck). This whole trainer grades you by the move, never by who won — so in Play, finish a game and you'll see your decision quality reported separately from the result: you can win and still be told you got away with it.

What every piece wants

Through the PP lens, a piece "wants" to maximise its transformation (reach, influence, good squares) and minimise its dissipation (exposure, being a target). What that means changes with the phase of the game — and there are always exceptions.
