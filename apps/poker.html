Poker ♠ through the lens

Hold'em is the purest PTO game there is: win the most over the long run, not this hand. Every decision is expected transformation (EV) versus dissipation (chips risked, variance, tilt), made blind to the opponent's cards. Learn it as equity, ranges, and residuals.

What you're actually trying to do

Poker is a betting game. You each get 2 private cards (your hole cards), and over the hand 5 shared cards (the board) are dealt face-up in the middle. You make your best 5-card hand using any of your 7 cards.

You win a pot two ways: everyone else folds (gives up), or you have the best hand at showdown. Here's the mind-bender that makes poker deep — you don't win by winning hands, you win by winning chips over time. A great player folds most hands and still profits, because they put money in only when the math is in their favor. Everything else here builds toward knowing when it is.

Blinds & position — turn order matters

To force action, two players post forced bets each hand — the small blind and big blind — so there's always something to fight over. A dealer button rotates each hand and sets who acts when.

The key idea: acting last is power. If you decide after seeing what everyone else did, you have information they didn't. That's why the player "on the button" (last to act) can profitably play far more hands than the player who acts first. Position is opacity working for you — you see their move before revealing yours. 5

Your five options

On your turn you can only ever do a few things:
• Fold — give up the hand (and any chips already in). Costs nothing more.
• Check — do nothing, pass the action, stay in — only allowed if no one has bet.
• Bet — put chips in when no one else has yet.
• Call — match someone's bet to stay in.
• Raise — match and increase; now they must respond.

Most of poker is one question repeated: is putting these chips in worth it? The next three lessons are how you answer it.

Equity — how often you'd win

Equity is the single most important word here. It's the share of the pot that's "yours" — the % of the time you'd win if all remaining cards were dealt out right now. A hand that wins 70% of the time has 70% equity.

Pot odds & EV — the one idea that wins

Here's where it clicks. If the pot is 100 and someone bets so you must call 50, you're risking 50 to win 150. You only need to win 50 ÷ 150 = 33% of the time to break even. That threshold is your pot odds.

The rule that makes poker profitable: call when your equity beats your pot odds. If you have 45% equity and only need 33%, calling makes money on average — even though you'll lose the hand 55% of the time. That gap between "I lost this one" and "it was still right" is the whole game, and it's pure PTO: judge the trajectory, not the result.

Call or raise? That's a separate question — pot odds only decide whether to continue. You raise for value when you're ahead of the hands that would call you (get more money in while you're the favorite); you raise as a semi-bluff when you're behind but hold a draw plus fold equity; and you just call when you're good enough to continue but raising would only fold out worse hands and get called by better. So: pot odds → fold vs continue; value & fold equity → call vs raise. The Decide, Practice, and Read trainers all label which reason applies.

Draws & sizing — the everyday math

Two things you'll use on almost every hand:
• Outs & the rule of 2 & 4. When you're behind but can improve, count the cards that win it for you (your outs), then estimate equity instantly — flop outs × 4, turn outs × 2. A flush draw is 9 outs ≈ 36% on the flop. Drill it in the Draws tab.
• Bet sizing. Betting isn't one button: the more of their range you beat, the bigger you bet; check or go small with marginal hands to control the pot. Drill it in the Sizing tab.

9

Ranges & bluffing — why you can't just wait for aces

You never know an opponent's exact two cards — only the range of hands they'd play this way (a whole set, a distribution, never one hand). Good players think "what would they do this with all of the time?" not "do they have it?"

This is also why bluffing works: because your betting could mean many hands, a bet with nothing can win if the story is believable. And why you can't only play premium hands — everyone folds to you, and you never get paid. The Read the range tab trains exactly this: guess the right play, then see the answer. The gap is your residual — the only thing that teaches you.
People have ranges, not hands
10

Variance — why you can play perfectly and lose

Short-term, cards are random: you can make every correct decision and lose for days. That's variance, and it's why poker rewards patience. You bet your edge in small enough pieces that a bad run can't bust you, and let the +EV compound. Tilt — playing worse because you're losing — is the real leak; it's dissipation, chips lost to a bruised ego, not to the cards.

That's the mindset the whole app is built around: win the trajectory, not the hand.

When you're behind but can improve, count your outs — the cards that make you the winner. Then estimate your equity instantly: on the flop (2 cards to come) outs × 4; on the turn (1 to come) outs × 2. This is the fastest math in poker, and it's how you know whether a draw is worth a call.

Implied odds: sometimes a draw is worth calling even when the immediate pot odds say no — because when you hit, you win extra chips on later streets. Bet sizing — how much, not just whether

Betting isn't one button. The core rule: the more of their range you beat, the bigger you bet. Nut hands want big bets and overbets (they still get called and you deny equity); marginal made hands want small bets or a check (keep the pot small, don't get raised off it). Position (why the button opens wide)

Acting last is information — you see everyone before you decide, so more hands become profitable. That’s why the button’s opening range dwarfs UTG’s. Position is opacity working for you: you have theirs, they don’t have yours.

Bankroll & variance = the trajectory (PTO)

You can play every hand perfectly and lose for a week. PTO is exactly the discipline of judging the trajectory, not the local result: bet your edge in sizes small enough that variance can’t end the run, and let the +EV compound. Tilt is dissipation — chips lost to a bruised predictor, not to the cards. The fold is inhibition: suppressing the hand you want to play so the bankroll survives.

Glossary

equity — your share of the pot if all cards ran out now. outs — cards that make your hand. range — the set of hands someone could have (never one hand — a distribution). EV — average chips a decision wins over infinite repeats. GTO — an unexploitable baseline; deviate only to punish a specific leak.
