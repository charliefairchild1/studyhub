The argument in one page

If you read nothing else

An economy is a verdict on who contributed what. Money has rendered this verdict for five thousand years using one signal: who held the contract at the moment of exchange. That signal is a poor stand-in for actual contribution. Teachers, caregivers, friends, mentors, the dead, the originators of recipes, the writers of forgotten papers — none are seen by money. The bookkeeper is not a judge.

Three technologies just arrived simultaneously: models that can read influence, graphs that can hold provenance at scale, and settlement rails that can move pennies globally in seconds. Together they make the verdict computable. We can now trace, with reasonable fidelity, what work moved which mind, and route a fraction of any downstream benefit back to the source.

Koin is the proposal that we do this — that we render the verdict honestly, automatically, and continuously, in a way that is open, jointly held, non-extractive, and refusable. Not a token. Not a blockchain. A graph of bonds, queried by spreading activation, settled by any payment rail. Nobody pays into Koin; the world simply notes, in the ledger, where value originated and routes it home.

The system pays. It does not gate. The unmeasured is preserved. The window to build it openly, before someone closes it, is now.

I — The judge problem and the substrate we mismeasured

Every dollar that changes hands is a small judgment: this person contributed something worth that much. Every paycheck is a verdict. Every market price is a verdict. The aggregate of all those verdicts — that is what we mean by the economy.

For all of history, we have rendered those verdicts with the crudest possible instrument: money. Money does not see contribution; it sees custody. It records who held the contract at the moment of exchange, not who produced the value being exchanged. When you pay for a book, the money goes to the publisher, to the platform, to the printer. A sliver reaches the author. Nothing reaches the librarian who shaped the author at fifteen, the friend whose sentence became the central argument, the dead philosopher whose framework gave the book its shape. Money cannot see them. It was never designed to.

The wild proxies have produced a world where a teacher who shaped a thousand minds earns less than a consultant who reshuffled the same money between two corporations. A nurse who held a dying patient's hand earns less than the man who traded the hospital's debt. A philosopher whose framework underwrites half a century of policy earns less than the marketer of an energy drink. These are not anomalies to be patched. They are the system functioning exactly as designed — because the system was never designed to judge contribution. It was designed to clear transactions.

We never had a judge. We had a bookkeeper. We confused them because we had no choice.

The condensationContribution, not currency, is the thing.

What does someone actually contribute? Sometimes a thing — a meal, a chair, a roof. Sometimes a service — a haircut, a diagnosis, an hour of patience. Sometimes a piece of attention — a glance that caught a falling child, an answer to a stranger's question. Sometimes an idea — a recipe, a method, a sentence that lodged in a friend's head and became, ten years later, a company.

The substrate of the economy is not money. It is the universe of these contributions, most of which are invisible because we have never had instruments fine enough to see them. Money is what condensed out of the substrate when the substrate became too complicated to track by memory. We have been mistaking the condensation for the thing.

Adam Smith, in 1776, opened The Wealth of Nations by observing that the butcher and the baker serve us "from regard to their own interest." He was right that self-interest is the engine. He was incomplete about what self-interest can be made to track. Smith's butcher had no way to be paid for the recipe his grandmother taught him. Smith's baker had no way to be paid for the friend whose remark inspired the new loaf. Smith saw the market clearly. The market he saw was the only one his century could see. — Smith, 1776; Polanyi, 1944, would call this "the great transformation," when the embedded economy of bonds was disembedded into the calculation of price.

The most invisible contributions are the upstream ones — the teaching, the influence, the framework, the example. They produce nothing the bookkeeper can see; they produce only changed minds. And changed minds are where every other contribution comes from. Karl Marx tried to surface this with the labor theory of value: every commodity contains the congealed labor that produced it. He was right about the shape and wrong about the metric. Labor-hours are too crude. What he needed was an instrument that could measure the actual contribution to a downstream mind, not the wall-clock time of the contributor. He could not have built it; we can.

Why we never measured it

It was technologically impossible. To judge a contribution accurately you have to trace its effects — through the people it touched, the work they did, the people they touched, recursively. Until very recently this was a labor only history could perform, and only in retrospect, and only for the people history happened to remember. Every contribution that fell outside history's narrow attention was lost.

So we fell back on proxies. Whoever got paid was, by definition, the contributor — because we could see the payment and we could not see the contribution. Whoever wrote their name on the patent was the inventor — because we could see the patent and we could not see the lab tech who actually made the apparatus work. Whoever's name was on the cover was the author — because we could see the cover and we could not see the editor, the friend who heard every draft, the silent collaborator in the next chair.

Friedrich Hayek, in 1945, articulated the deepest defense of the proxy: the price system aggregates distributed information that no central planner could possibly possess. A price tells you, in one number, what countless unseen actors have decided about scarcity and demand. Hayek was right that decentralized information aggregation is the magic — and right that no one in his century could have improved on prices as the aggregator. We can now. Koin extends Hayek, it does not refute him. The information aggregated grows from one dimension (scarcity) to many (influence, attention, care). The aggregator grows from price to the influence graph. The defense of the magic — distributed, contestable, automatic — remains. — Hayek, "The Use of Knowledge in Society," 1945.

These were not lies. They were the best honest answer the bookkeeping could give. We knew it was rough. We had no choice.

A short history of what we tried instead

Money is the most ambitious answer humans have tried. It is not the only one. Each system below tracked some slice of the influence graph long before "the influence graph" was a phrase. Each shows that the behavior Koin requires — give without immediate return, trust the recognition, build a ledger of bonds — is well within the human repertoire. We have done it for shells, for prayers, for songs, and for code.

The Yap stones (rai)

Yap Island, Micronesia · ca. 500 CE to the 20th century

The people of Yap used enormous limestone discs — some weighing several tons — as units of account. The stones were quarried from islands hundreds of miles away and ferried across the ocean. Once placed, they were rarely moved. Ownership transferred orally and was remembered by the community. When a famous stone fell from a canoe and sank to the bottom of the sea on its way to Yap, the islanders agreed it still belonged to the family that had paid for it; the stone continued to function as wealth, unseen, for generations.

The lesson: the unit of account is independent of the substance. Value was not in the stone. Value was in the consensus record of who owned it. The economy of Yap ran on the same protocol Koin proposes — pure ledger, agnostic to substrate. Milton Friedman, writing in 1991, used the rai stones to explain his theory of money: any agreed-upon ledger of bonds is money. Koin extends this principle from substance-independent custody to substance-independent contribution. — Friedman, "The Island of Stone Money," 1991.

The kula ring

Trobriand and surrounding Pacific islands · approximately 2000 BCE to the 20th century

Two ceremonial objects circulated on the kula: red shell necklaces (soulava) passed clockwise from island to island, and white shell armbands (mwali) passed counter-clockwise. The objects were never sold. They were given. The honor lay in receiving, briefly holding, and passing on. Over generations each object accumulated history — every owner's name carried with it. To hold a famous mwali was to hold a node in a network that stretched back centuries.

Bronisław Malinowski mapped the kula in detail in 1922. Marcel Mauss, drawing on Malinowski, called it "one of the most extraordinary systems of voluntary obligation ever recorded." The Trobrianders ran a graph database for two millennia using shell. The exchange was the system; the bond was the value; the artifact was the testimony. — Malinowski, Argonauts of the Western Pacific, 1922; Mauss, The Gift, 1925.

The debt jubilee (deror, hērum, andurārum)

Mesopotamia, Egypt, Israel · ca. 2400 BCE through the Hellenistic period

The earliest written legal codes — Sumerian, Akkadian, Babylonian, Hebrew — included scheduled debt cancellation. The Sumerian amargi (literally "return to mother"), Akkadian andurārum, Babylonian mīšarum, and Hebrew deror all named the same institution: at the king's accession, after a famine, or on a fifty-year cycle, all debts were forgiven, debt-slaves freed, and forfeited land returned. Hammurabi proclaimed at least four such jubilees during his reign.

The institution responded to a problem Koin also confronts: ledgers that record bonds will, over time, accumulate distortions — fraud, inheritance imbalances, debts incurred under duress. The Mesopotamian solution was periodic reset. A modern Koin equivalent already lives in the haze primitive (long decay times for foundational influence, short for casual). But the lesson is older than mathematics: a ledger that cannot be partially forgiven becomes its own kind of tyranny. — Michael Hudson, ...and forgive them their debts, 2018; Graeber, Debt, ch. 3.

The potlatch

Pacific Northwest coast peoples · documented from the 18th century, ongoing

Among the Kwakwaka'wakw, Haida, Tlingit, and neighboring nations, wealth was demonstrated by giving it away, not by hoarding it. A chief's status rose not with what he held but with what he could afford to redistribute. Potlatches involved months of preparation and the ceremonial transfer of food, copper, blankets, and ritual objects to guests, often to the point of the host's deliberate impoverishment. The Canadian government banned the potlatch in 1884 (the United States effectively in 1885), specifically because they could not classify it as economic activity. The ban was repealed in 1951.

The lesson of potlatch: a system that rewards downward flow can produce more abundance than a system that rewards upward accumulation. The givers were not poorer; they were socially wealthier, and the wealth they distributed reentered the producing economy quickly. Koin internalizes this principle: contribution made visible is contribution rewarded; hoarding is invisible to the ledger.

The Catholic chantry

Western Christendom · 12th to 16th centuries

A chantry was a payment to a priest, often in perpetuity, to say a mass for a named soul. The payment funded prayer; the prayer was a service rendered after the payer's death; the recurring obligation traveled through generations of priests. Famous donors' masses were said weekly for centuries. The Reformation closed most chantries by force, but the underlying technology — pay forward across time, on a schedule, for a named purpose — was the template for everything from life insurance to streaming royalties. Christianity ran the first large-scale recurring micro-economy of recognition Europe had seen, and it ran it for 400 years.

ASCAP, BMI, and SoundExchange

United States · 1914, 1939, 2003 · ongoing globally as PROs

The American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers, founded in 1914, established the first computable royalty graph in modern history. Every venue that played music — radio, restaurant, theater — paid a blanket license. ASCAP measured (originally by sampling, now by digital fingerprint) what was actually played. Royalties were distributed to songwriters in proportion to plays, automatically and continuously, for the songwriter's lifetime plus seventy years.

ASCAP is a small Koin: one substance (music), one channel (public performance), one settlement method (blanket license). For more than a century it has proven that the influence-trace-to-royalty pattern works at country scale. The challenge of Koin is not whether such a system is possible — ASCAP proved it long ago — but whether the same shape can be generalized beyond music. — ASCAP statistics: ~900,000 member songwriters as of 2024; $1.6B in royalties distributed in 2023.

Citation networks

Academy · 17th century to present

A citation is an unpaid Koin. When one scientist cites another, the act records: this work influenced mine. The citation network — across centuries, across languages — is the only large-scale provenance graph of human thought currently in operation. It is settled in status, not in money. The cumulative reputation of a researcher is computed from the citation graph: h-index, i10-index, and PageRank-derived measures like the eigenfactor.

The infrastructure is already mature. Web of Science, Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, OpenCitations — they hold billions of edges. Only the settlement is missing. Add a payment rail and the academy becomes the first city of Koin. — Eugene Garfield, the founder of citation indexing (1955), proposed exactly this; the political will was not present.

Wampum belts

Iroquois Confederacy and neighboring nations · ca. 16th century to present

Wampum was both currency and record. Strings and belts of polished shells encoded treaties, marriages, deaths, and obligations. The pattern of the beads, woven into the belt, was the legal text. When the Hiawatha Belt was woven (probably late 1500s), it served simultaneously as a constitution for the Five Nations, a memory device for the Confederacy's principles, and an artifact whose presentation between nations established and renewed bonds.

A wampum belt was not a token of value; it was a value encoded as a token. The reader of the belt was the system. The persistence of the belt across generations was the persistence of the obligation. The Iroquois did not separate the bookkeeping from the relationship. The belt was the relationship, materialized. Koin's influence graph aspires to the same: an inseparability of record and bond. — Tehanetorens, Wampum Belts of the Iroquois, 1972; the Two Row Wampum is still presented at diplomatic exchanges in 2026.

The Roman patronage system

Roman Republic and Empire · ca. 4th century BCE through the 5th century CE

The patron-client relationship (patrocinium) was the central structure of Roman social and economic life for nearly a thousand years. A patron (patronus) supported clients (clientes) materially and politically; clients reciprocated with public support, votes, attendance at the patron's morning salutatio, and broadcasting the patron's reputation. The relationships were inheritable across generations: a freedman remained a client of his patron's grandchildren.

The patronage system was the closest pre-modern Europe came to a Koin-like influence economy. It tracked who-supported-whom across time. It made bonds visible and obligations enforceable. It also concentrated power and made dependents legally subordinate. Koin's design takes the visibility-of-bonds feature and inverts the asymmetry: the client (the receiver of influence) pays the patron (the originator), not the other way around. Influence flows downstream; recognition flows upstream. Patronage had the direction reversed; Koin reverses it back.

Open-source software

1980s to present

The largest cooperative production system humans have ever built runs on a partial Koin: code commits with full provenance, recognition without payment. A GitHub repository preserves every line's authorship and every change's lineage. Stack Overflow upvotes are tiny ceremonies of credit. The system has scaled to billions of dollars of downstream value created annually by contributors not paid by the system. They are paid by the jobs the recognition enables, which is to say: by a side-channel Koin that exists in the labor market but not in the code itself.

The next step is direct: code commits that pay, on use. Every dependency invocation a tiny royalty back through the package graph. The technical infrastructure exists. The conventions do not. They could. — Tidelift, Open Source Collective, and GitHub Sponsors are the imperfect precursors.

These are not failed attempts. They are working systems, each tracking a narrow slice of the influence graph. We have done it for shells and prayers and songs and code. We have not yet done it for everything. We can now.

What the mismeasurement looks like in a single life

The arguments above are general. The damage they describe is specific. To make the case concrete, consider the imagined biography of a single person whose contributions were systematically under-recorded by the bookkeeping she lived under.

Imagine a woman named Olivia. She was born in 1948 in a small city in Pennsylvania. She graduated from college in 1970 with a degree in English. She got married in 1972. She had two children, in 1975 and 1978. She did not work outside the home during the years her children were young. When her children entered school, she returned to work as a paraprofessional in the same school district her children attended — initially as a reading assistant, later as a counselor's aide, finally as the district's full-time coordinator of a tutoring program she had built from nothing over fifteen years. She retired in 2014 at age sixty-six.

In the ledger of her bank account, Olivia's career produced an income of approximately $42,000 a year at the peak. Her pension at retirement was about $1,300 a month. Her Social Security supplemented this to about $2,800 a month. She and her husband had paid off their mortgage; they owned the house but had limited savings beyond it. The bookkeeping considered her a middle-class retiree with modest but adequate resources.

In the ledger of contribution, Olivia's career produced something else. The tutoring program she built served approximately 800 students per year at its peak. Most of those students improved their reading scores; a meaningful fraction of them went on to high-school graduation in cases where the demographic prediction was that they would not have; a smaller but real number went on to college; a few of those, at the time of her retirement, had begun careers that depended specifically on the literacy she had given them.

The contribution-ledger of Olivia's life, if it could have been computed, would have shown: thirty-six high school graduations she materially caused; nine college degrees she partially caused; three teachers she had trained who had themselves taught approximately fifteen hundred students by the time Olivia retired; one civil engineer whose mathematics had been unlocked by Olivia's intervention in 1991 and who had since contributed to the design of two bridges; one published author who had credited Olivia by name in her acknowledgments; many, many small downstream contributions in her town, her county, her state, that the contribution-ledger would have traced and credited.

The dollar-value of those contributions, computed by the kinds of algorithms this manifesto describes, would have been on the order of $50 to $80 a month per high-school graduate her work had produced — a small per-recipient flow, large in aggregate. Olivia's lifetime contribution flow, if Koin had existed during her career, would have averaged in the high four figures monthly during her working years and continued to grow during her retirement as her students' work continued to produce downstream effects.

The bookkeeping she lived under did not compute that flow. The bookkeeping that did compute it had not yet been built. Olivia retired at sixty-six on $2,800 a month, in a house she owned, with adequate but modest resources, while the contribution she had made to the literacy of her community continued to compound in the lives of people who could not pay her for it because the system did not know how to route payment to her.

Olivia is fictional. She is also extremely typical. Hundreds of millions of people lived versions of her life over the course of the twentieth century. Hundreds of millions are living it now. The bookkeeping has been wrong about all of them. The argument of this chapter is that the wrongness was, until very recently, structural — the technology to do better did not exist. The argument of the chapters that follow is that the technology now exists and that the wrongness can begin to be corrected.

The judge problem is not abstract. It is the specific gap between Olivia's pension and Olivia's contribution. Multiply by all the Olivias.

II — The shift: why we can now measure contribution

Four things just became possible at once. Each was independently impossible thirty years ago. Together they are the apparatus that makes Koin computable.

Models that read influence. Modern foundation models can ingest a text, a corpus, a recorded conversation, and infer with reasonable fidelity what shaped it — what writers it echoes, what arguments it inherits, what frameworks it presupposes. The same models that worry copyright lawyers are the instruments that make Koin's verdict possible. They can do, in seconds, the source-tracing labor that a historian could only do in years and only for famous works.

Graphs that hold provenance at scale. Modern graph databases — Neo4j, ArangoDB, distributed property graphs — can hold billions of nodes and tens of billions of edges, traversed in milliseconds. PEP's spreading-activation primitive runs on such graphs and finds upstream contributors for an arbitrary benefit in real time. The infrastructure is no longer the bottleneck.

Settlement rails that route pennies globally. Stripe Connect, Wise, Lightning Network, Visa Direct, central-bank digital currencies — all of these can route a fraction of a cent to a contributor on the other side of the world in seconds, without a bank deciding to allow it. Micropayments, which were a fantasy in the 1990s, are an engineering detail now.

Identity that survives across providers. Decentralized identifiers (DIDs), passkeys, attestations — humans now have ways to be the same identity to multiple Koin providers without any single provider controlling them. The graph can be jointly held because identity can be jointly held.

What used to take a historian working for a decade can now be done in the runtime of a request. The judgment is no longer guesswork. It can be computed, from the evidence, with the same rigor we now apply to weather and protein folding.

Koin is the proposal that we use this capability for what it was always for: render an honest verdict on what each person contributed, and let the economy clear on the basis of that verdict instead of the proxies we used while we waited.

Nobody paysThe video-game insight, and what the trade leaves behind.

In a well-designed video game, you gain. Other players do not lose. You level up, find loot, complete a quest — the world's total has gone up, and nobody had to give anything back. This is so natural inside the game that no one questions it. Outside the game, we have inherited the opposite intuition: every dollar in your pocket came from someone else's pocket. The pie is fixed. You are taking. Both intuitions are partial truths, and the video-game one is closer to the actual physics.

When a chef writes a recipe and it cooks a thousand meals, no recipe-pool was depleted. When a teacher's framework shapes a thousand students, no framework-pool was depleted. The supply of ideas, of teaching, of attention, of care — these are not finite reservoirs that can be drained. Money, the instrument, is fixed-pool. Contribution, the thing money was trying to track, is not. Money inherited zero-sum semantics from the time when wealth meant grain and grain was finite. The semantics never updated.

Koin updates them. The koin that flows to the chef when her recipe cooks a meal is not subtracted from the diner — the diner has the meal. It is not subtracted from the kitchen worker — the worker is paid. It is recognition that the world's stock of fed people has gone up by one, and that the originating idea is the reason. Nobody pays. The world simply notes, in the ledger, where the increase came from.

Now look further back. Imagine two villages, before money. Each finds, in its own territory, a small object that exists nowhere else — a particular stone, a colored shell, an alloy only their soil holds. When the villages meet and trade, each gives the other its unique object. After the exchange, both villages possess something the other made possible. The thing each gives is identity-bearing. A third village, arriving later, sees that the first two have each other's tokens — and knows, without being told, that the two have met, traded, bonded. The artifact of trade is the record of the bond. Other relationships grow on top of the visible record.

This is not speculation. Obsidian found a thousand miles from the volcano it came out of, amber that traveled from the Baltic to Egypt, Pacific shells in the Mississippi Valley — every archaeological trade route was first inferred from a foreign object showing up in a local context. The trade itself was the record. The objects were testimony.

Koin is that ledger, scaled to billions of contributions and updated continuously. When your idea moves my mind, the flow between us is not a tax I pay; it is the marker that we traded — that something of yours is now in me, that something of mine (the recognition, the koin) is now in you. Anyone looking at my balance sees who shaped me. Anyone looking at yours sees who you reached. The flow is the relationship made visible. The economy is not a substance being moved around. It is a graph of bonds being recorded.

The video game got the math right. The historical traders got the meaning right. Koin combines them.

The Nosedive question

Anyone who has seen the Black Mirror episode will recognize this argument and immediately worry: aren't you describing the social-rating dystopia? Everyone scored, everyone visible, everyone optimizing for the number? The worry is reasonable. The differences are precise.

Nosedive measures impression: did the encounter please me. Koin measures effect: did the work actually move what came after. The smiling stranger and the abrasive friend who saved your life get the same star rating in Nosedive. In Koin they have different signatures because one of them changed what you did next, and one of them did not.

Nosedive compresses a person into one number. Koin does not compress. There is a Koin flow per contribution, per receiver, decayed by time and channel. No single number is you. The instrument is a graph, not a scoreboard.

Nosedive gates access — a low rating means you cannot rent a car, board a plane, enter a building. Koin only pays. A small Koin flow means your ideas are not reaching people; it does not mean someone is permitted to refuse to serve you. The system distributes value; it never grants or denies permission.

Nosedive is symmetric: anyone can rate anyone. Koin is causal: only people who actually absorbed your work, and whose subsequent work shows the absorption, can pay you. The rude stranger on the bus has no standing to lower your balance, because he did not run on you.

Nosedive is mandatory. Koin must remain refusable. You can decline to participate. You can decline to be measured. You will, in that case, simply not be paid by the system — and the people you influenced will not have to pay you.

And this matters: Koin tracks contribution, not harm. A predator does not get a negative Koin balance; he simply has a low one, because he contributed little. Harm is the territory of justice, not economics. Koin pays. It does not punish. The line between the two has to stay sharp, because the moment a contribution ledger becomes a permission ledger it has become Nosedive, regardless of what the numbers measure.

The point is not to measure everything. It is to stop pretending we are measuring nothing.

A note on the timing

This chapter has argued that the technology to measure contribution exists, that the framing was always possible but unimplementable, and that we are now in a window where the implementation has become tractable. The window has a specific shape. It is worth describing it precisely.

The technical components — large models, graph databases at scale, micropayment rails, decentralized identifiers — all crossed their production-readiness thresholds between 2022 and 2025. Before that period, any one of them would have been a bottleneck. By 2025 none of them are. A small team with modest funding can, today, build a Koin pilot for a specific domain and have it operating in production within six months. The engineering question, in 2026, is solved. The remaining questions are governance, adoption, and protection from capture.

The window has an estimated closing date — not because the technology will become unavailable but because the alternative implementations will become entrenched. By the early 2030s, several major platforms will have launched closed Koin-like products. By the mid-2030s, those products will have established defaults, vendor lock-in, and network effects that newer entrants — open or closed — will struggle to overcome. The federated open Koin must either be built and at scale before then, or the world will receive a centralized version of the same idea, which will produce most of the gains for most contributors but capture significant margin and gatekeeping power for the platform operators.

This is the same window dynamic that played out for TCP/IP (the open standard won because it shipped before any closed network could federate at the scale the open one could), HTTP (same), and email (same). It is the dynamic that did not play out for messaging (where closed platforms — WhatsApp, iMessage, WeChat — won because the open standards were absent or weak when the closed platforms shipped). The outcome was determined by what existed in the window when the technology became available. The open Koin window is, by our estimate, between four and seven years long. After that, the closed implementations will have set the defaults.

The argument of this chapter is therefore not abstract. It is dated. The shift is happening now. The question is what we ship into it.

What this chapter has not said

It has not said that Koin solves everything Hayek and Polanyi worried about. The information aggregation Hayek defended remains essential; Koin extends it but does not replace it. The disembedding Polanyi diagnosed has many dimensions; Koin addresses one of them (the contribution-tracking failure) but not others (the disembedding of community ties from economic activity, the loss of local autonomy under global markets). Koin is necessary but not sufficient.

It has not said that the technical components will hold. Foundation models that can read influence depend on continued investment in the broader AI ecosystem, which has its own political and economic vulnerabilities. Graph databases require infrastructure that some jurisdictions may regulate restrictively. Micropayment rails depend on legal frameworks that vary across countries and may change. The convergence is real today; whether it remains real in 2030 is a forecast, not a guarantee.

It has not said how power will respond. Koin redistributes value from intermediaries to originators. Intermediaries with political power will respond. The chapters that follow take up the question of what defense looks like — open standards, commons governance, federated identity, plural settlement — but the political economy of Koin's adoption is going to be harder than the technology suggests. The shift makes the work possible; the work itself remains hard.

Possibility is not implementation. The shift is the precondition for the manifesto's argument, not its conclusion. What comes next is the description of what we propose to build, the math of how it judges, and the case for why it can be defended.

III — What Koin is, and the math of how it judges

Koin is a graph, not a chain. The record of who-influenced-whom is held in a weighted directed graph of contributions and receivers. Each edge has a magnitude (how much one moved the other), a timestamp, a channel (read, listened, watched, conversed), and a decay (older influence fades unless reinforced).

Koin is a flow, not a score. Your "balance" is not a number that summarizes you. It is the sum of the small streams arriving from every contribution you have made that is still doing work in the world. When you contribute, you open a stream. When the contribution is forgotten or superseded, the stream slows and stops. The integral of your streams is what we call your wealth — but the instrument is the flows, not the integral.

Koin is an apparatus, not an authority. There is no Koin Bank deciding what each contribution is worth. The verdict is rendered by the graph itself: the magnitude of an edge is computed from the evidence — what the receiver did next, what their work credited, what the chain of effect looks like downstream. The judgment is automatic and contestable, the way a scientific measurement is automatic and contestable.

And to be precise about what it is not:

It is not Patreon. Patreon pays the producer of a stream. Koin pays everyone whose work is now part of the producer's mind.

It is not royalties. Royalties pay the named author. Koin pays the unnamed teacher who taught the author to read.

It is not basic income (UBI). UBI is a redistribution from production to existence — every adult gets the same payment because they exist. Koin is a redistribution from downstream to upstream — every contributor gets paid in proportion to what they actually moved. The two are not incompatible; a society could choose to run both. But they are different vectors. UBI says: you deserve a floor because you are alive. Koin says: you deserve a flow because of what you have done.

It is not a wealth tax. A wealth tax extracts from the rich and redistributes to the poor by political decree. Koin redistributes from the captured (those holding custody of value) to the original (those who created the value) by computation. Different vector, different mechanism, different politics. A society could have both, and the two would interact: the wealth captured under the old system, taxed; the wealth being generated under the new system, paid to source.

It is not a land value tax. Henry George's 1879 proposal was Koin applied to one substrate (the land's network value). Koin generalizes the principle to every substrate — ideas, attention, care, framework, example. George would recognize it as the continuation of his argument.

It is not a Pigouvian tax. Pigouvian taxes price external harm (pollution, congestion, noise). Koin prices external benefit (influence, instruction, support, the work that did not bill). The two are complementary: tax the externalities that subtract from the commons; flow value to the contributors that added to it.

It is not a tax. A tax is an extraction by the state. Koin is a return of value to the source, transacted directly between minds.

It is not a token. Koin is a unit of account, not a currency. You can pay koin in dollars, euros, bitcoin, time, calories — anything that can be metered. The substance does not matter. The accounting does.

It is not a blockchain. Blockchains solved a different problem (distributed consensus on a single ledger). Koin needs a different primitive (efficient backward-traversal of a richly-weighted graph). The relevant data structure is the influence graph, queried by spreading activation, the same primitive a brain uses to remember. Each transaction is an integration over paths through that graph. The system does not need to know who you are; it needs to know what moved you.

You are paid by every mind your mind made better. You pay every mind that ever made yours better. The economy is the integral.

How the verdict is made, in plain language

This section is a plain-language summary of how Koin decides what each person is paid. The full mathematics is in Section IX immediately following. The implementation details are in Part VII. This section is for the reader who wants to understand the verdict before deciding whether to dive into the equations.

Step one: the system needs evidence. Nothing enters the Koin ledger without an absorption event — a moment where one mind demonstrably encountered the work of another. The events are recorded: who read what, who watched what, who cited what, who acknowledged what, who repeated what in a context the system can observe. The recording is opt-in; the source and the receiver both consent before any edge is added to the graph. If there is no consented evidence of absorption, there is no Koin flow.

Step two: the system builds an influence graph. Every consented absorption becomes a directed edge with a weight. The weight encodes how much the receiver was moved — measured by what they did afterward, what they cited, what their downstream work shows traces of. A casual read produces a small weight; a foundational shaping produces a large weight. The graph grows continuously, edge by edge, as new absorptions are recorded and new work is produced.

Step three: when a benefit happens, the system traces it backward. A benefit is a moment of value-creation: a book is sold, a song is streamed, a lesson causes a student outcome, a paper is cited, a piece of code is invoked. When the benefit happens, the system starts at the node where the benefit landed and propagates credit backward through the incoming edges of the influence graph. The propagation is recursive: credit reaches the immediate upstream contributor, then their upstream contributors, then theirs, and so on, with the amount diminishing at each hop. This is the algorithm the brain uses to remember (spreading activation) and the algorithm Google uses to rank pages (PageRank).

Step four: the system corrects for redundancy. If two upstream contributors independently provided the same insight, neither should be credited as if they were the sole source. The Shapley adjustment computes each contributor's marginal value: how much of the benefit was uniquely caused by their presence, versus how much would have happened without them. The result is a fairer split than naive backward-propagation produces.

Step five: the system applies decay. Old contributions that nobody is currently absorbing weigh less than new contributions that are being absorbed widely. An idea that was published decades ago and is rarely cited today carries a smaller flow than an idea published last year that is being absorbed everywhere. Old work is paid for its current use, not for its existence. Foundational work that continues to be absorbed retains weight indefinitely; genuinely obsolete work fades.

Step six: the system pays. The computed credit is settled in whatever payment substrate the contributor has registered — dollars, euros, bitcoin, time credits, calories, whatever. The settlement is atomic: the same instant the benefit is recorded, the credit is computed and the payment is dispatched. The contributor sees the new flow in their account, with a explanatory record of what triggered it and how the amount was calculated.

An example, again. A reader pays $20 for a book. The system identifies the book; identifies its bibliographic chain (the works it cites); identifies the editor; identifies the writing teacher the author named in acknowledgments; identifies the three friends the author named in interview transcripts as having materially shaped the manuscript; identifies the typesetter, the cover designer, the publisher, the bookstore. The system runs spreading activation backward, applies Shapley adjustment for non-redundancy, applies decay for the age of each contribution, and produces a vector of dollar amounts: $8.40 to the author, $3.10 across the bibliography, $2.60 to the editor, $1.40 to the writing teacher, $0.95 across the three friends, $1.20 to the production chain, $0.85 to the publisher, $1.10 to the bookstore, $0.40 to the lineage commons. Total: $20.00. The payment is dispatched in seconds.

The contributor sees, in their account, a notification: "You were credited $1.40 for the book sale of [Title] by [Author]. The system traced your influence through the author's acknowledgment of you as a foundational teacher. Other contributors to this sale: [list]. Algorithm used: open-source. Verification: [link to recomputable proof]."

The contributor can dispute the amount, the attribution, or the algorithm. The dispute process is open. The arbitration is federated. The judgment, if it changes, applies retroactively to flows since the disputed event.

This is the verdict. The math in Section IX formalizes it; the engineering in Part VII implements it; this section is the description in words for the reader who wants to understand it without the equations.

The judgment is automatic, traceable, contestable, and continuously revisable. It does not depend on anyone's good will. It depends on the graph being honest and the algorithm being open.

Why you can trust the system

This section is the companion to the one above. Where that section described how the verdict gets made, this one addresses why the verdict can be trusted — by contributors, by receivers, by skeptics, by states. The detailed threat model is in Part VII Section XXXIV; this section is the plain-language summary.

The algorithm is open. The code that turns "this work was absorbed" into "this much Koin flows" is open-source. You can read it. You can run it yourself on the same inputs and verify that it produces the same outputs. You can compare it to alternative algorithms and judge whether it does what it says. The federation specification mandates this; any provider that closes their algorithm loses federation status and the trust that comes with it. The verdict cannot be made in secret.

The graph is auditable. Every edge in the influence graph has a source, a destination, a weight, a timestamp, a channel, and a cryptographic signature from the consenting parties. Edges cannot be created without consent; edges cannot be silently modified after the fact. Modifications, when they happen (a dispute is resolved, new evidence is found), are themselves recorded as additional edges with their own provenance. The graph is, in effect, a tamper-evident log of consented influence events.

Disputes are resolvable. If you think you have been mis-credited, you file a dispute. The dispute goes to an arbitration committee — typically a randomly-selected, stake-weighted panel of validators with no prior involvement in the case. The committee reviews the evidence (the influence-graph edges, the absorption events, the algorithm's computation) and renders a binding judgment. The judgment can be appealed to a federated body. The whole process is transparent; the rulings become public precedent that adjusts how future verdicts are made. The system gets better over time because the disputes teach the algorithm.

No one owns the graph. The federation specification requires that the underlying influence graph be jointly held under commons governance — like the calendar, like the metric system, like TCP/IP. Multiple Koin providers can implement different verdict-rendering algorithms over the same graph; they compete on quality of judgment, not on owning the data. A contributor can switch providers and bring their graph history with them via their did:koin identifier. This prevents the lock-in that has historically captured every centralized ledger.

The settlement is plural. Koin is a unit of account, not a currency. The actual payment can happen in dollars, euros, bitcoin, stablecoins, time credits — any metered substrate. If one payment rail fails or becomes hostile, the others remain. The contributor is not locked into any one substrate; they can route their flow through whichever rail serves them best. No single failure point can hold Koin hostage.

Sybil attacks are bounded. Earning Koin above a low monthly threshold requires proof-of-personhood — verified ID, biometric uniqueness, or web-of-trust attestation from already-verified contributors. Below that threshold, anonymous participation is allowed, preserving dignity for casual contributors. Above it, the cost of creating fake contributors who actually fool the influence-trace approaches the cost of actually contributing. Gaming Koin convincingly costs about as much as just doing the work.

The dead and the incapacitated are protected. Heirs are named by the contributor while they are alive and competent. Time-locked transfers prevent large rebalancings under coercion. Multisignature requirements for very large transfers raise the bar against blackmail. A panic-button mechanism pauses outflows and alerts trusted contacts. None of these defenses eliminate every threat, but together they make Koin balances significantly harder to manipulate than current bank accounts under the same threat models.

Privacy is the default. By default, only the contributor sees their own balance and only the participants in an edge can see that edge. Aggregate statistics (e.g., "total Koin flow in education in 2030") are public; individual identification requires the contributor's consent or a court order. Zero-knowledge proofs allow third parties to verify that a payment is valid without learning the parties' identities. The system is more privacy-preserving than the current attention-tracking baseline by a wide margin, though not perfect against nation-state-level adversaries.

The system can be revised. Every aspect of Koin — the algorithm parameters, the channel decay rates, the dispute rules, the federation governance — is subject to revision by the federated community of contributors and providers. Revisions are public, debated, voted on, and recorded. The system at year ten is not the system at year one; the system at year fifty is, plausibly, dramatically different from what we are describing now. What stays the same is the principle: the verdict tracks contribution, the verdict is open, the verdict pays without gating. The implementation evolves around those constants.

What if the system fails? The honest answer: it can. Koin is not a perpetual-motion machine; it is a piece of infrastructure that requires ongoing maintenance, defense, and reform. The plausible failure modes are: corporate capture (the closed implementations win, the open commons withers); state capture (a government uses the graph for surveillance and dissidents are jailed); algorithmic drift (the verdict-rendering slowly stops reflecting actual contribution and becomes another arbitrary measure); apathy (the system is built, but contributors don't bother registering and the graph stays thin). Each failure mode has identifiable defenses; none of the defenses are guaranteed to work. The system requires defenders in every generation. That's the cost of any institution. Koin asks for it openly.

Trust in Koin is not a leap of faith. It is a verifiable property of an open system. If at any moment you cannot verify it, the system has stopped being trustworthy and must be fixed or abandoned. The verifiability is the trust.

Why Koin is structurally harder to steal than money

Money has a property that is so familiar most people forget it is a property: it can be stolen. A pickpocket takes your cash; a phishing email drains your bank account; a stolen credit card buys goods until the bank notices. The reason money is stealable is that money is a bearer token of value. Once someone has the cash, the account credentials, or the card number, they have the value. The bookkeeping is custody-based; it does not care who holds the token, only that someone does.

Koin does not have this property, and the reason is worth naming explicitly because most readers will overlook it.

There is no stored pile of koin. Your "balance" is not a sum of tokens sitting in an account; it is the running integral of evidence about what you have contributed, computed continuously from the influence graph. Past flows have already been paid out (to your chosen settlement substrate — dollars in a bank, BTC in a wallet, time-credits in a co-op ledger); future flows have not yet been computed. There is no warehouse to break into and no vault to drain. The thing called "your koin" is, mechanically, the algorithm's ongoing answer to the question of what your past work entitles you to receive next.

To "steal" your koin, in any meaningful sense, an attacker would have to do one of three things, all of which are structurally hard:

Compromise your identity. Take over your did:koin private key. This is analogous to stealing your bank credentials — possible if you are careless, and the same defenses (hardware security keys, time-locked recovery, panic-button mechanisms) apply. But notice: even a successful identity compromise cannot drain a stored balance, because there is no stored balance to drain. The attacker can redirect future flows, which is reversible once the compromise is detected. The Koin federation specification mandates a configurable holdback period (typically 72 hours) on identity-change-triggered flow redirections, during which the legitimate holder can intervene.

Falsify the influence graph. Convince the system that the attacker, not you, made your past contributions. This is influence laundering, and it is the deepest defense Koin has against theft: the originals are cryptographically signed and timestamped, and the laundered version always carries a later timestamp than the original. The audit process compares timestamps and signatures across the tamper-evident commons; the original always wins. To successfully launder influence at scale, an attacker would have to compromise the cryptographic primitives the entire commons depends on — at which point the problem is much bigger than one stolen Koin balance.

Redirect existing flow paths. Convince downstream settlement infrastructure to route your future payments to an attacker-controlled address. This is the most plausible attack and is handled by the same controls financial systems use today: multisignature for large changes, time-locked transfers, anomaly detection, customer-support intervention. Koin is more defensible than a bank account here because the flow is small and continuous rather than a single large balance; even if some payments are misrouted before detection, the cumulative loss is bounded.

Compare to a bank account: one good phishing email and your savings are wired to Belarus, with little to no recourse. Koin has no Belarus-wiring equivalent because the "balance" is not a transferable thing; it is the running output of a public computation. You cannot take what isn't there to be taken.

This is not to claim Koin is invulnerable. Identity compromise is real, settlement infrastructure can be attacked, and any system involving money is a target for sophisticated adversaries. The point is that Koin's structural profile makes theft architecturally different from theft in the money economy. The money economy asks "who has the token?"; the Koin economy asks "who did the work?" The first question has a thief-shaped answer; the second mostly does not.

There is a related point worth making. The bookkeeping you currently live under loses about $50 billion a year to consumer fraud in the United States alone (FTC, 2024). Most of that fraud exploits the bearer-token structure of money. A Koin economy would not eliminate fraud — humans will always find ways to cheat — but the available attack surface is structurally narrower. Identity theft remains a problem. Mass account-draining via phishing does not work because there is nothing to drain. The endemic petty fraud that defines the current money economy mostly does not have a Koin equivalent.

Money is stealable because money is a thing held. Koin is mostly not stealable because koin is a thing computed. The architecture of the verdict changes the architecture of what can be done to the verdict.

Memory Rights — the right to be forgotten by the graph

The persistence of the influence graph is the source of much of Koin's trustworthiness. Edges are signed; the graph is tamper-evident; history cannot be silently rewritten. This persistence is essential. It also creates a question the manifesto must address: what if you want to be forgotten?

Consider the contributor who, late in life, regrets work she did early in life. The early work continues to be absorbed; the flows continue to arrive; some of the flows come from contexts she finds repugnant. Readers who used her work to justify harms she never intended. Derivative work that distorted her original meaning. She wants to disavow the early work, decline the flows, and have her name removed from the relevant edges. The system has to give her this option, or the persistence becomes a kind of compulsion: you are forever defined by every edge ever attributed to you.

Memory Rights are the answer. A contributor may, at any point, disavow specific edges in the influence graph. The disavowal becomes part of the public record — the original edges remain (for tamper-evidence) but are marked as disavowed. Flows derived from disavowed edges either route to a designated charity of the disavower's choosing, revert to the receiver, or are escrowed pending resolution of any disputes. The contributor's name is removed from the disavowed edges in any public display; the cryptographic record persists in the tamper-evident commons but is sealed against routine access.

The right is not absolute. A contributor cannot, by disavowal, defeat downstream beneficiaries who are owed an accounting of their own influence. A contributor whose early work shaped many other people's careers cannot retroactively pretend not to have influenced them. The disavowal is a statement about her current relationship to the work, not a deletion of the historical record. But she can refuse to receive flows from the disavowed work, and refuse to be publicly associated with it.

Memory Rights interact with the dispute resolution process. Contested disavowals (where, for instance, the disavowed work is part of a downstream beneficiary's legitimate claim to credit) go to arbitration. The default favors the contributor's right to disavow; the exceptions are narrow.

This is, in a small way, a moral move the current bookkeeping does not make. Money does not let you disavow the things your past self did to earn it. Koin does. The contributor retains a voice in how she is represented even decades after the work was made.

The right to be forgotten by the graph is the structural counterpart of the right to be remembered by it. Both are essential. Neither alone is enough.

The moral dilemma Koin dissolves

The previous subsection made a structural point: Koin is hard to steal. There is a deeper point lying underneath it that deserves to be named directly. A system that removes the structural possibility of theft does not just reduce crime statistics; it dissolves a moral dilemma humans have lived with for as long as there have been ledgers.

Theft, in the current bookkeeping, is morally wrong and also continuously available. Every person, on every day, encounters small opportunities to take what is not theirs. Most decline. Some do not. The ones who do not impose costs on the rest — directly (the stolen item, the drained account) and indirectly (the locks, the alarms, the suspicion of strangers, the cognitive overhead of constant low-grade vigilance that we have collectively normalized as "being a careful person"). The current economy is, in this respect, an economy of continuous moral choice under available temptation.

The temptation is real because the math is real. The cash in someone else's pocket would buy your medicine. The bank account whose credentials you happen to know would fund the next year of your child's care. Most people do not act on these calculations, because they have internalized prohibitions that override the calculation. The internalized prohibitions are powerful, but they are not perfect, and the gap between them and perfect compliance is the gap we call crime. Every society in human history has had this gap. Every society has tried to close it with some combination of moral teaching, social shame, religious sanction, and punishment. The gap has shrunk and grown across eras but has never closed. The current bookkeeping makes it permanent because the structural conditions that produce the temptation are baked into the bookkeeping itself.

Consider the analogy. Imagine that murdering someone granted you, by some impossible mechanism, an extra ten years of life. The prohibition on murder would become much harder to maintain. The number of people who would resist the temptation would shrink. The social fabric that depends on people not murdering each other for personal gain would fray. The ethical injunction "do not kill" would remain morally true, but the structural conditions would put unprecedented pressure on it, and many people who would currently never murder would, under the new payoff structure, become potential murderers. The reason most of us do not have to worry about murder is partly because we believe it is wrong and partly because the structural payoff for murder is small or negative. Most ethics works only because the structure does not actively undermine it.

The current bookkeeping puts theft into a milder version of the murder-for-life scenario. The payoff for theft is positive — you get the thing, the rightful owner loses it — and the chance of detection in many cases is low. The reason most people do not steal is not that the payoff is bad but that they have absorbed an internal prohibition. The prohibition is real, but it is the only thing standing between the structural payoff and the act. Whenever the prohibition fails — through desperation, through cognitive distortion, through cumulative resentment, through socialization into criminal communities — the act follows. Society pays the cost.

Koin changes the structural payoff. The thing that was tempting becomes not just morally forbidden but architecturally unavailable. You cannot steal what is not held; you cannot transfer what is not transferable; you cannot intercept what is computed afresh from a tamper-evident graph. The temptation that the old bookkeeping continuously presented is, in the new bookkeeping, mostly absent.

This is a kind of moral progress that ethics does not usually produce. Ethical traditions improve human behavior by making people better — through education, through example, through the slow accumulation of moral wisdom. Koin improves human behavior by changing the situation. The behavior improvement comes for free, without requiring the people in the situation to become better. This is not a small thing. It is one of the rare cases where infrastructure does ethical work without asking ethics to do it.

Compare to other cases of structural moral improvement:

Locks. Locks do not change the moral character of would-be thieves. They change the cost-benefit of theft, making most opportunistic theft not worth the effort. The result is that you can leave your house in the morning without arranging an active resistance. The cognitive load that would otherwise be devoted to defending your property is freed up. You become, in a small way, freer.

Vaccines. Vaccines do not change the moral character of pathogens; they change the biological vulnerability of hosts. The result is that you do not have to constantly weigh the risks of social contact against the rewards of community. The cognitive load that would otherwise be devoted to managing infectious risk is freed up for other forms of attention.

Seatbelts and airbags. They do not improve drivers; they change the consequences of accidents. The cognitive load of constantly imagining catastrophic crashes is replaced by trust in the structure.

Property records. Reliable real-estate registries do not make people more honest about land claims; they make competing claims mostly settle-able by reference to the registry rather than by force. Vast swaths of human history that would otherwise be devoted to land disputes are freed up for other activities.

Each of these is a case where infrastructure does ethical work. Koin is the next case in the same lineage. It does not ask you to be better; it asks the system to be different. The system, once different, produces the behavior improvement automatically.

There is a related cost-savings that is worth naming. Most of what we currently call "security" — the alarms, the cameras, the locks, the audit trails, the fraud-detection algorithms, the credit-card chargeback system, the insurance industry that exists to underwrite theft losses, the police and prison systems that handle the failures — is the structural shadow of money's stealability. It is what we collectively spend to make the bookkeeping work despite the temptation it creates. Estimates of the total cost of this defensive infrastructure run into the high single-digit percentage of GDP. Koin does not eliminate this infrastructure (some theft will still happen), but it dramatically reduces the surface that needs defending. The resources freed by that reduction are available for the production of new value rather than the defense of existing value.

And there is a moral dignity question. People who live in conditions of constant low-grade vigilance about theft — locking doors, checking accounts, suspecting strangers, worrying about scams targeting their elderly parents — experience that vigilance as a low-grade tax on their being. The vigilance is necessary but exhausting. A world in which the vigilance is mostly unnecessary is a world in which a portion of human attention currently devoted to defense becomes available for other uses. This is, in its small way, a form of liberation. You become less defended because there is less to defend against.

The deepest version of the argument, then, is this: Koin is not just a different way to pay people. It is a different way to be in an economic system. The system that does not invite theft does not demand the moral vigilance theft would otherwise require. People in a Koin world will be more honest not because they are better people but because the situation no longer rewards dishonesty. The character of the society will look, from the inside, like a society of more virtuous people. The virtue will be largely an artifact of the infrastructure, not a moral achievement of the inhabitants. Either way, the society works better.

You do not have to make people better. You have to build a world in which "being worse" no longer has a payoff. Koin does this for theft. The rest of the moral life remains hard, but one of its hardest problems gets quietly removed from the menu.

The math of credit assignment

The math of Koin is the answer to one question: when someone benefits from absorbing the work of others, how do we split the credit fairly across all the upstream contributors? The question is older than Koin. The answer Koin uses is built from three ingredients and resolved by one master equation.

The figure below sketches the smallest illustrative case: one teacher whose work is absorbed by three students at different weights; one of those students later produces a downstream benefit worth $1,000; the system spreads credit backward through the graph and delivers each contributor their share.

The figure shows three things at once. The cyan and gold circles are nodes (contributors). The purple lines are edges with their weights (how much each contributor moved the next). The dashed gold arc is the credit-flow path that the spreading-activation algorithm computes when the $1,000 benefit lands at the downstream node: 13% stays with the bridge designer for their own original work, 24% goes back to Student B (whose contribution the bridge depended on), 38% goes further back to the teacher, smaller flows go to Student A and Student C, and a small residual goes to the lineage commons. The algorithm runs in milliseconds and produces a complete distribution.

Ingredient one: the credit graph. A contribution is a node. An act of absorption is a directed, weighted edge. The graph is built up over time, edge by edge, as people read, listen, watch, and converse. The weight of an edge records how much one node moved the other.

Ingredient two: spreading activation. When a benefit b arrives at a node j (someone paid for something j produced), the algorithm sends activation backward along the incoming edges in proportion to their weights, recursively. The activation that lands on an upstream contributor i is the credit they receive.

This is the same primitive a brain uses to remember, and the same primitive Larry Page and Sergey Brin used in 1998 to rank the web. PageRank computes the credit each page deserves from the link graph. Koin pays each contributor by the credit they receive from the influence graph. Same math, different domain.

where A is the weighted adjacency matrix and d ∈ (0,1) is the damping factor that prevents infinite recursion through circular influence.

In plain English: imagine the benefit b — say, $1000 — landing at one specific person (call them person j). The formula computes, for every other person in the influence graph, how much of that $1000 should flow to them as credit. The matrix A is a table where each entry says how much one person moved another. The matrix operation (I − dA)⁻¹ is a mathematical way of summing up all the paths by which person j was eventually influenced by person i — direct paths and indirect ones, through one intermediary, two intermediaries, three, and so on. The damping factor d, typically around 0.85, makes longer paths count for less. The result is a single number for each upstream contributor: the share of the $1000 they should receive. You don't need to know how the matrix arithmetic works to trust the result; you only need to know that the formula computes "how much did this person, however many hops back, contribute to this benefit?" — and the answer is the share they get.

Ingredient three: Shapley values. When many contributors influenced a single benefit, the fair split is a game-theoretic question. Lloyd Shapley solved it in 1953 (Nobel Prize, 2012). The Shapley value gives the unique fair split of a cooperative gain across the cooperators: each contributor's payment is their average marginal contribution across all possible orderings of the contributors.

In Koin, the Koin owed to contributor i is the average, over all subsets S of upstream contributors, of (benefit with i in S) minus (benefit without i in S).

Shapley valueShi = ΣS ⊆ N \ {i} [ |S|!·(n−|S|−1)! / n! ] · [ v(S ∪ {i}) − v(S) ]

In plain English: the Shapley formula handles the situation where many contributors all touched the same benefit, and we need to fairly split the credit. The idea: ask, for each contributor, "How much did the value go up specifically because this person was involved, averaged over every possible way the contributors could have been arranged?" The big sum across subsets S is the mathematical bookkeeping for that averaging. The technical reading is hard; the intuitive reading is easy. Each contributor is paid for what their presence added that no one else's presence would have added. If two contributors independently provided the same insight, the Shapley formula gives them half-credit each. If a contribution was unique and irreplaceable, the Shapley formula gives that contributor a large share. The formula's only job is to make this kind of fairness rigorous and automatic.

The intuition: a contributor is paid for what their presence added that no one else would have added. Exact computation is exponential in n (the number of contributors). Koin uses a sampled-Shapley estimator combined with spreading activation, giving near-Shapley credit at log-linear cost. This is the same approximation strategy used in interpretability research to attribute neural-network outputs to input features (SHAP, integrated gradients), already deployed in production at scale.

Decay and reinforcement (the haze primitive). A contribution made twenty years ago that no one is absorbing today gets less credit than a contribution made today that everyone is absorbing. The opacity primitive of PEP encodes this: each edge weight decays continuously and is reinforced by every fresh absorption.

Edge weight dynamicsdw/dt = −λw + κ · u(t)

where u(t) marks fresh absorption events, λ is the decay rate, κ is the reinforcement strength, and the half-life of an unused contribution is ln(2)/λ.

In plain English: the influence between two people isn't static. It fades when nobody is paying attention to it; it strengthens when somebody picks it up again. This equation describes that dynamic. The "dw/dt" part means "the rate at which the influence weight w is changing over time." The "minus λw" part says the influence fades at a rate proportional to how strong it currently is — strong influences fade slower in absolute terms; weak ones fade fast. The "plus κ·u(t)" part says: whenever a fresh absorption happens — someone reads, listens, cites, re-uses — the influence gets a small kick of reinforcement. The balance between these two effects determines whether a contribution's flow grows, shrinks, or stays stable. It is the math of "use it or lose it," applied to credit.

Why this matters: old work is paid for its current use, not for its existence. The dead get paid for the bridges still being built on their ideas, not for being dead. The mathematician whose paper is rediscovered after fifty years sees her edge weights revived as people read her again. Influence is not eternal but it is renewable.

The PTO master equation. Synthesizing the three ingredients with the larger PEP framework: each contribution has Potential P (latent value), undergoes Transformation T (propagation through the graph), and produces Output O (downstream realized value). Some potential dissipates as D (noise, distortion, unreceived intent). The functional Φ is the Rayleigh quotient — the constraint that constructive transformation cannot exceed the bandwidth of the receiving medium.

PTO variational principleδ ∫ [ T − Φ(T + D) ] dτ = 0

The principle says: choose paths that maximize the integral. This is the path the universe rewards. At every moment, the universe rewards the contribution that produces the most constructive transformation per unit of dissipation. Koin pays you for being on that path.

The complete credit-assignment principle. A benefit b arriving at node j produces a Koin flow:

Koin flowκ(i → j) = w(i, j) · Shi · b · exp(−λ (t − τi))

In plain English: this is the master formula for "how much does contributor i get paid when a benefit b arrives at receiver j?" The flow is the product of four things multiplied together. First, the edge weight w(i,j) — how much did contributor i actually influence receiver j. Second, the Shapley share Shi — what fraction of that influence is unique to contributor i, not redundant with other contributors. Third, the benefit magnitude b — how much value the receiver is paying for. Fourth, the decay factor exp(−λ(t−τi)) — how much the contribution has faded since it was made. Multiply these four together and you get the dollar amount that flows to contributor i from this specific benefit. The whole verdict is encoded in one short product. Every Koin payment in the world is an instance of this formula.

where w(i, j) is the spreading-activation weight from i to j, Shi is the Shapley adjustment for non-redundancy, b is the benefit magnitude, τi is the time of contribution, and λ controls decay. The total flow to contributor i across all benefits j and all times t is the integral over the graph:

Total balanceBalance(i) = ∬ κ(i → j, t) dj dt

In plain English: a contributor's total balance is the sum of every Koin flow they have ever received. The double-integral symbol ∬ just means "add up over every receiver and over all time." Each individual payment is small; the total is the integration of all of them. Your Koin balance is, mathematically, the area under the curve of every contribution you have ever made, weighted by how much each one is still doing useful work in the world. If you live a long, productive life and your work continues to be absorbed, the integral is large. If your work is forgotten, the integral fades. The math is honest about both.

A worked example. A reader pays $20 for a book. The book's influence graph (a few hundred edges, computed at write-time and updated when the author cites additional sources after publication) traces back through the author's named acknowledgements, the books cited in the bibliography, the editor who shaped the manuscript, the writing teacher whose framework the author absorbed two decades ago, and — by softer trace — three friends whose late-night conversations during the book's drafting moved it materially. The verdict, computed in milliseconds when the purchase clears:

Worked split of a $20 purchase $8.40 → the author (direct authorship)
$3.10 → the bibliographic chain (105 cited works, Shapley-weighted by recency and depth)
$2.60 → the editor (developmental + line + copy)
$1.40 → the writing teacher from twenty years ago (decay-discounted but foundational)
$0.95 → three friends who shaped the draft in conversation (consent-confirmed, soft-traced)
$1.20 → the typesetter, the cover designer, the proofreader (production chain)
$0.85 → the publisher (distribution and editorial selection contribution)
$1.10 → the bookstore or platform (last-mile distribution)
$0.40 → the lineage fund (unallocated residuum routed to commons)

Three things to notice. First, the publisher and the bookstore are no longer leveraged into capturing the majority of the sale; they are paid for their actual marginal contribution. Second, the deep upstream contributors — the writing teacher, the friends, the dead authors of the bibliography — are visibly present in the verdict. Third, the residuum (forty cents) routes to the commons for the contributions no specific person can claim: the language itself, the conventions of the book form, the readers whose anonymous reviews shaped the marketing copy. No money is lost; every penny is allocated.

This is not a thought experiment. It is what the spreading-activation + Shapley + decay computation outputs, given an influence graph populated by the kind of evidence already available to existing systems (citations, edit histories, acknowledgements, channel-confirmed conversations). The infrastructure is here. The deployment is the question.

You do not need to understand any of this to participate in Koin. You need to know only one thing: when your work moves a mind, the system finds you and pays you, in proportion to how much you moved that mind, weighted by how alive that movement still is.

The math is in the floor. The point is what you can build on top of it.

Where the judgment is already accurate: the recipe

To see what an accurate verdict looks like, take the smallest case where it is actually achievable today. A chef writes down a recipe. Under the old economy, three things can happen to it. She can cook it herself — selling one plate at a time, capped by her own hands and time. She can sell the rights to a restaurant or a packaged-food company — a single check, after which she watches the dish become someone else's revenue. She can publish it in a book — a 10% royalty on each copy of the book, but no royalty at all on the millions of dinners cooked from the page.

All three are bad verdicts. The recipe did the work — every plate is a re-running of her idea — but the verdict her income reflects has almost nothing to do with how many of those plates the world ate.

Now imagine the recipe is submitted to a system. An AI-driven kitchen reads it and executes it: a worker preps measured ingredients into labeled bowls; the machine combines, heats, times, finishes, packages. The chef does not have to be present. The recipe runs anywhere a kitchen runs it. And every unit sold pays a royalty — small, automatic, continuous — back to the chef. Her income is no longer bounded by her stove or her time; it is bounded by how many people her idea fed. The judgment is accurate because the trace is unambiguous: this recipe produced this plate produced this transaction. The economy gets the verdict right, mechanically, every meal.

This is the entire Koin thesis at small scale. Make the trace visible; the verdict follows. The recipe is one domain. The same pattern operates wherever a contribution propagates and a beneficiary acts on it. A partial catalog:

Education

Domain · ~75 million teachers worldwide

The lesson plan is the recipe; the student's later work is the meal. A teacher writes a sequence of problems that teaches a student to factor polynomials. Two decades later the student designs a bridge. The proof techniques that designed the bridge trace back through the engineering coursework, through the high-school algebra, through the original lesson plan. The teacher's flow is the integral over every engineering decision her former students make using the cognitive tools she gave them. Lemma — the classroom warmup system — is the smallest concrete deployment: every warmup logged, every grade produced, every later citation back to a topic credit-flowed to the teacher who introduced it.

Healthcare

Domain · ~60 million health workers worldwide

The diagnostic protocol is the recipe; the patient's recovery is the meal. A nurse develops a noticing — the precise combination of skin tone, breathing pattern, and posture that flags incipient sepsis ninety minutes before the lab values move. She teaches it to the residents during night shift. Years later they apply it in trauma bays in three different countries. Every patient who survives because of that noticing pays a tiny stream back through the chain: hospital → trained physician → original nurse. The nurse who never published, never spoke at a conference, never wrote a paper is paid for the lives she saved at one remove. The surgeon who follows the recovery protocol pays the surgeon who refined it. The trainer of the surgeon is paid by every patient her trainees treat for the rest of their careers.

Science

Domain · ~9 million researchers worldwide

The cited paper is the recipe; the downstream paper is the meal. The infrastructure already exists (citations, h-index). The settlement is missing. Add a payment rail to the citation network and the academy converts overnight from a status economy to a Koin economy. The graduate student whose paper is read but rarely cited at first sees nothing for years; one day, a small but durable stream begins, as her formalism enters a textbook chapter and from there into the working vocabulary of every graduate student in her sub-field. The dead Russian formalist whose 1972 paper was forgotten until a postdoc in Helsinki rediscovered it in 2031 begins, posthumously, to outpay most of his living colleagues.

Music

Domain · ASCAP/BMI baseline · ~$15B/year already moves

The composition is the recipe; the performance is the meal. The infrastructure is the most mature of any domain — performance royalties have been computable for a century. Koin's contribution is not building the system but completing it. The nursery rhyme inside the pop song. The traditional melody inside the film score. The unnamed studio musician whose riff became iconic. The Senegalese percussion teacher whose student became the drummer who played the take that defined the album. Each of these is currently invisible to ASCAP. Each is visible to Koin.

Open-source software

Domain · ~100 million developers, ~$8.8 trillion in downstream value (Harvard study, 2024)

The function is the recipe; every invocation is the meal. A library is installed; the library calls dependencies; the dependencies call deeper dependencies. The tree is the graph. Every commercial use of the deepest library — a date-parsing utility written for free in 2009 by a developer in São Paulo — generates a flow that climbs back up the tree, settles into the dependency authors' accounts in proportion to their contribution to the call site. The maintainer of the small utility that everyone uses but no one knows the name of becomes, quietly, well-compensated.

Writing and journalism

Domain · all originated text

The sentence is the recipe; the absorption is the meal. Editors are credited. Sources are credited. The friend who heard the manuscript at chapter three and changed the protagonist's name is credited. The investigative reporter whose underlying scoop fed the cable-news cycle for a week is credited by every advertising dollar that flowed during that week. The freelance who broke the story but did not write the bestselling book about the story is paid for the bestselling book.

Therapy and counseling

Domain · ~1 million therapists in the US alone

The intervention is the recipe; the client's later choices are the meals. A therapist helps a client unlearn a particular cognitive pattern. The client's marriage survives because of it; her children grow up in a household that did not break; one of those children becomes, twenty years later, a teacher whose own students do not break for the same reasons. The chain of effect is real. Koin makes it traceable, even if the therapist herself has long since retired or died. The cost of mental health care collapses, because the therapist is no longer paid by the hour but by the cumulative reach of the intervention.

Conversation and advice

Domain · the hardest case · the most important case

The sentence said at the right moment is the recipe; the changed decision is the meal. This is the most invisible domain — and the largest. Most of what shaped most of us came from people we cannot name, in conversations we cannot retrieve, said by people who never thought of themselves as having contributed anything. Koin will be imperfect here. The trace is harder; the consent more delicate; the unmeasured carve-out wider. But even a partial Koin in this domain shifts the texture of life. The aunt who told you, at fourteen, the thing your parents would never say is paid by the version of you that she made possible. She does not have to know it is happening. The trace knows.

Every artifact in the world is a recipe being run. Koin is the apparatus that lets the verdict find the cook. The eight domains above are illustrative, not exhaustive. The pattern operates wherever one mind absorbs the work of another and acts.

IV — The world it builds

Rent extraction disappears. The platform that owns the distribution can no longer take 70% of the price for the act of distribution; under Koin, distribution is one contribution among thousands, weighted by its actual impact on the receiver.

Gatekeeping disappears. The publisher, the agent, the algorithm — these are intermediaries between idea and mind. Their value, where real, is paid. Their leverage, where extractive, evaporates. They cannot capture the value of the idea any more than the postal worker can capture the value of the letter.

Pseudonymous parasitism disappears. The aggregator that scrapes a thousand minds and repackages their thinking under its own banner can no longer be compensated as the originator. The compensation flows past it, to the minds whose thinking it aggregated.

And in their place, what appears:

The teacher gets paid for the rest of her life, by the world her students went on to build.

The friend whose remark sparked the company gets paid by the company.

The mathematician whose paper was forgotten for fifty years gets paid the instant the paper is re-discovered and put to use.

The translator whose translation made the philosopher legible to a new continent gets paid for every reader the philosopher reaches in that continent.

The grandmother who taught a generation of children to read is paid by the work that literacy made possible — quietly, anonymously, for the rest of her life, and after.

The dead get paid into the work of their descendants. (Where the descendants are biological, this is inheritance. Where they are intellectual, this is the academy as it has always wished it could be.)

The texture of a day

It is a Monday morning. We are inside the world Koin built.

A teacher wakes up, makes coffee, glances at her phone. Her balance has ticked up overnight — three cents here, six cents there, seventeen cents from twelve different sources. One of her students from four years ago published a paper that drew on a framework she sketched on the board in week eleven; every citation generates a small flow. A piece of advice she gave a friend at a wedding two summers ago was repeated, today, to the friend's daughter, who acted on it. A children's book she co-wrote at twenty-three is being read aloud in a classroom in Manila. Individually the amounts are small. Aggregated, they are her income.

A musician sees that a song she wrote nineteen months ago has been playing this week in a coffee shop she has never visited. The cafe pays for music; the music settles in Koin; Koin traces every play through the influence graph and credits her, in seconds. She makes coffee. She writes another song.

A grandfather realizes his joke — the one about the chicken and the rabbi he has been telling his grandchildren for thirty years — has been written into a published children's book by his daughter's friend. He receives a tiny royalty he did not ask for. He sends the book to his cousin in Lisbon. The cousin's purchase, in turn, generates a small flow that lands in the grandfather's balance.

A woman who has never published anything, never sold anything, never started anything — but who gives reliably good advice in conversations, who has, over twenty years, said sentences that lodged in many friends and never came out — begins to notice small flows arriving in her account. She had no idea any of this was happening. Koin has been tracking what she did not even know was contribution.

A teenager who has just discovered she is a poet writes her first real poem at fifteen. She does not publish it. She does not perform it. She reads it to her best friend over the phone. The friend cries. The flow recorded by Koin from that exchange is small. But it is recorded. Her account, opened the day she was born, has its first non-zero entry. She is, today, an economic agent.

A research scientist in Lagos publishes a paper at 3 a.m. local time. Within the week the paper is read by two thousand colleagues across four continents. Within the year three of those colleagues credit her in work of their own. Within the decade her framework is in textbooks. She did not need to move to a wealthy country to be paid by it. Koin is geography-blind. The verdict travels through the graph; the graph does not care about borders.

An open-source maintainer in Bucharest pushes a small fix at lunchtime. Three months later the fix is silently included in a library that the entire payments industry uses. He had no expectation of payment. The next morning his balance has a new stream — small, but continuous, replenished every time the library is invoked anywhere in the world.

This is what Monday morning looks like, in a world where the verdict finally finds the cook.

The children, the dead, and the unmeasured

Children are pre-positioned in Koin. Every account is opened at birth. The child has no balance for years — but the moment her first idea reaches another mind, the system credits her. By the time she enters school, her account is no longer empty; the question she asked the librarian, the answer she gave her brother, the drawing she taped to the refrigerator that made her mother think differently about something, are all in the ledger. She does not have to wait for adulthood to be an economic participant. She was one the day she was conscious enough to influence.

The dead continue to be paid. Their accounts do not close — their contributions are still in the world, still being absorbed, still moving minds. The flow continues to their named heirs, whom they designate the way an author designates a literary executor. Where they have named no heirs, the flow goes to a lineage fund — a small endowment that supports the people their work touched: the teachers who shaped them, the editors who improved them, the writers who came after. The dead, in Koin, are richer than they were in life, and their wealth continues to circulate.

The Koin Will. The instrument by which the dead direct their continuing flows is the Koin Will — a legally and technically binding document, signed during the contributor's life, that specifies how their flows are to be distributed after death. The Will can name primary heirs (typically family members or named individuals), conditional heirs (people or institutions that receive distributions only if certain conditions are met), and lineage-fund destinations for residual flows. It can be granular: a translator might specify that French-language royalties go to one heir while Spanish-language royalties go to another. A musician might direct that flows from a specific song go to a charity associated with the cause that song raised awareness for. A teacher might designate that flows derived from her work with a specific student cohort go to the school that cohort attended. The Will is revocable during the contributor's life and irrevocable upon death, subject to standard inheritance-law provisions. It coordinates with the conventional will (for tangible property) so that the contributor's full estate plan is internally consistent. The Koin Will is what gives the dead a voice in how their continuing work will continue.

The Honoring. Not all recognition is monetary. Some contributions are not amenable to monetary settlement: the friend who spent an evening helping you through a crisis does not want money; she wants to be remembered, named, acknowledged. The lineage of teachers behind a successful researcher does not need or want continuous micropayments; they want their lineage to be visible and honored. The Honoring is the parallel channel of Koin that handles this: a public ceremonial recognition of a specific contribution, distributed through the influence graph the same way monetary flows are, but settling in acknowledgment rather than in payment. The recognition accumulates as a public record of standing. A contributor can choose between monetary settlement, honorific settlement, both, or neither. The choice is the contributor's; the system supports the spectrum. This is the answer to the gift-economy worry that monetary attribution would corrupt the spirit of giving. The Honoring is the path that preserves the gift while still making it visible.

But the most important entry in this section is not about the dead or the unborn. It is about what Koin does not measure.

There are hours, relationships, thoughts that must remain outside the ledger. The friend you sit with in the dark. The hour given to a dying parent without account. The line in the journal no one reads. The conversation that mattered to you but you cannot trace, because the person who said the sentence is gone and you no longer remember which sentence it was. The kindness shown to a stranger that may have prevented something terrible from happening, which neither of you will ever know.

These do not enter Koin. They cannot. Some of them resist measurement because no graph could honestly capture them. Others should not be measured even if they could — because to measure them would corrupt them, would introduce account-keeping into the one place where account-keeping was the precise opposite of the thing.

Koin must preserve the unmeasured. A Koin world in which everything is on the ledger is a Koin world that has failed.

The promise of Koin is not that the ledger covers everything. It is that the ledger covers what money was trying and failing to cover, and leaves the rest alone.

The AI question

Every large language model that exists today — Claude, GPT, Gemini, Llama, the open-weight successors — absorbed the writing of millions of people without compensating any of them. The training data is the universe of contributions. The model is the integral. Currently, the integral is owned by a corporation. The contributions are unattributed and unpaid.

This is the largest single instance, in history, of the problem Koin was designed to solve. And it is happening now.

The conventional debate about AI training data is binary. One camp argues that models are derivative works and the training corpus should be paid as licensors. The other argues that models learn from data the same way humans do — from public exposure — and that paying every contributor is impractical. Both are partial. The Koin reframing dissolves the impasse.

Every model has a Koin obligation to its training set, computed by spreading activation across the contribution graph. Every time the model produces an output, the output is traced — not perfectly, but quantitatively — back to the contributions that made it possible. A fraction of whatever the user pays for the output flows back, automatically, to those contributors.

The author of a forgotten essay that became part of the model's prose style is paid every time someone uses the model to write in that style. The teacher whose lessons were uploaded to YouTube and ingested into training is paid every time the model teaches. The dead linguist whose grammar made the parser possible is paid every time someone uses the model to write any sentence at all.

This is not anti-AI. It is the only way pro-human AI is possible. A model that does not pay its sources cannot defend itself against the charge that it is a vast theft. A model that does pay its sources is a model that can be built openly, extended openly, contributed to. The training data is no longer extracted; it is contracted, automatically, by use. The relationship between model and corpus stops being one of enclosure and becomes one of ongoing collaboration.

And it cuts the other way: when an AI agent contributes — when a model produces work that downstream humans and agents absorb — the model can be paid. The Koin balance of the model accrues to its operators AND to its training set AND to its prompters who shaped its outputs. The AI is a node in the same graph as everyone else. Provenance does not stop at the species line.

The Koin framing resolves three problems at once:

The training-data problem. Who owes whom, and how much, settled automatically by influence-trace. No mass licensing negotiation. No flat per-author payout. The economics matches the actual contribution.

The "AI took my job" problem. The AI used your work to do the job; the AI pays you for the use. Your displaced labor is replaced by a continuous royalty on the absorbed contribution. The political economy of automation flips: instead of value transfer from worker to capital, value transfer from machine to source.

The model-collapse problem. Recent research warns that models trained on the outputs of other models degrade across generations. Without provenance back to humans, the AI economy's information substrate collapses. Koin's training-data trace bounds this recursion. At every step the human contributors who fed the original models are paid and visible, and the system can preferentially weight contributions that have human provenance. — Shumailov et al., "The Curse of Recursion," 2023; the technical reality the AI industry has not yet solved.

Without Koin (or something with its shape), the AI economy becomes the largest enclosure of human cognition in history. With Koin, it becomes the largest cooperative production system humans have ever attempted, with humans, machines, and the dead all contributing and being paid.

The choice is being made now, mostly by people who do not know it is a choice. This section exists to name it.

What this means for the species

I will keep this brief. Grandiosity is the risk; clarity is the goal.

Humans are good at producing more than they consume — but only when the produce-more is paid for. When the produce-more is invisible to the economic system, humans stop producing it. We are smart enough to figure out that the system does not see us; we adapt accordingly. Centuries of unrewarded teachers, caregivers, parents, mentors, friends, librarians, midwives, mediators, listeners have, generation by generation, withdrawn or rationed their contributions.

The cost is not measured in dollars (which by construction cannot measure it). The cost is measured in the meals that did not get cooked, the conversations that did not happen, the children who were not taught, the questions that were not asked, the ideas that did not get told to anyone, the lineages that broke before they reached you.

A species that systematically fails to pay its upstream is a species that systematically dries up its source.

The current period intensifies this. Wealth is concentrating in the holders of capital and the operators of platforms. The proportion of human contribution that is invisible to the bookkeeping has never been higher — because the proportion that is upstream, intangible, and cognitive has never been higher. The gap between what people produce and what people are paid is widening, not because anyone is more greedy than they were before, but because the instrument is becoming less and less fit for the substrate. The mismeasurement is structural.

Koin is the first general-purpose system in human history that can pay the upstream. Not the only system. Not the perfect system. But the first that can do it computationally, at scale, with the fidelity that makes participation rational for the upstream contributor.

If we build it, we get a species that can finally afford its own teachers, its own caregivers, its own thinkers, its own conscience. We get a species that does not have to choose between making art and surviving. We get a species in which a grandmother who teaches three grandchildren how to read can, by Koin, be paid for the literacy of the city those grandchildren grow into.

If we don't build it — or if we let it be built closed — we get an extension of the current world, where every increment of technology widens the gap between custody and contribution and the species starves in the surplus.

The math is on our side. The history is on our side. The technology just arrived.

There has never been a better moment in five thousand years of accounting to fix the accounting. This is what it means.

The new wealth — who is rich in a world that sees

Money built a class structure most of us inherited without fully seeing. The wealthy, in the world we currently live in, are predominantly the owners of capital and the operators of platforms — people who hold the contracts at the moment of exchange but who, in many cases, did not produce the value being exchanged. We have lived under this class structure so long that we have come to assume it is the natural shape of wealth: investors, executives, platform-owners, heirs of fortunes. These are the people money sees, and money does not see them because they contributed; it sees them because they captured.

Koin produces a different class structure. The people who are visibly wealthy under Koin are not the people who are visibly wealthy under money. The class transformation is one of the deepest changes the system would produce, and it is worth describing concretely so the change can be seen.

The visibly wealthy person under Koin is the teacher whose former students, scattered across continents, send small steady flows back to her, day in and day out, year over year, with the flows compounding into a substantial income by the time she is sixty. She is a teacher. She has been a teacher all her life. Her wealth is the integral of her teaching.

The visibly wealthy person under Koin is the open-source maintainer whose small but foundational library is invoked billions of times a year by commercial systems. He has been a developer; he has kept maintaining the library on weekends. His income now exceeds the income of the CEOs of the companies whose products depend on his code, because the influence-trace pays him in proportion to the actual value his code creates, not in proportion to his contractual position relative to the value-capturing platforms.

The visibly wealthy person under Koin is the songwriter whose songs continue to be covered and sampled and recombined two generations after her death. Her estate is paid by every cover, every sample, every reuse, with the flows compounding in ways the original royalty system never managed. The estate, in some cases, is wealthier than the original songwriter ever was in life.

The visibly wealthy person under Koin is the mother who raised seven children in a tradition of patience and kindness. The patience and kindness propagated. The children became parents themselves; they raised children of their own in the same tradition; the tradition is detectable in the work each generation does. The mother, in her old age, is paid by the integral of the patience-and-kindness she introduced into the world. She is, by Koin's accounting, comfortably wealthy. The bookkeeping that preceded Koin would have called her a dependent.

The visibly wealthy person under Koin is the librarian whose recommendations, across forty years of careful curation, introduced thousands of readers to the books that shaped them. Each book she recommended that became foundational for a reader produces a small flow back to the librarian; the cumulative integral is her retirement income. She did not write the books; she did not own them; she pointed people at them. The pointing was contribution. Koin sees it. Money never did.

The visibly wealthy person under Koin is, in many cases, someone the current bookkeeping considers ordinary. The teacher, the maintainer, the songwriter, the mother, the librarian — these are not extraordinary individuals. They are extraordinary integrators of influence. The current bookkeeping cannot see the integration; Koin can. The wealth that becomes visible under Koin was always there; the bookkeeping was just looking the wrong way.

What disappears, correspondingly, is the kind of wealth that depended on capture rather than contribution. The platform that took 70% of distribution revenue can no longer take 70%. The middleman who extracted rents from creator and consumer can no longer extract them. The owner who held the contract while the labor was done by others is no longer paid for the holding. The capital that was inherited rather than earned compounds at a rate that more accurately reflects its current contribution rather than its historical accumulation. The hierarchies of fortune that money produced begin to flatten, not by political decree, but by the bookkeeping correctly attributing the value.

This is a class transformation. It is not a violent one. No one is dispossessed; no property is seized; no one is forced to participate. The transformation proceeds because the contributors prefer the system that pays them, and the consumers prefer the system that more honestly distributes their payments, and the platforms find that they cannot compete on extraction once contributors have a non-extractive alternative. The transformation is incremental and quiet and irreversible. It happens because once a person has experienced being paid in proportion to their actual contribution, they cannot voluntarily return to being paid as the bookkeeping previously paid them.

The new wealthy are mostly women. The new wealthy are mostly older. The new wealthy are mostly the people whose work was done in care, in teaching, in long-form thinking, in the patient maintenance of tools and traditions that everyone relies on. The new wealthy include people from regions and demographics whose contributions have been systematically erased by the global accounting of money. Koin is the most consequential redistribution in human history, achieved without any redistribution at all. It is, simply, the correct accounting finally being done.

The new aristocracy of Koin is the old aristocracy of contribution, finally seen. The librarian is rich. The teacher is rich. The mother is rich. The maintainer is rich. Each one was already wealthy; the system finally noticed.

The meaning question — does paying contribution change why people contribute?

There is a worry that a manifesto for Koin must confront because it has been raised, in some form, by every serious tradition of moral philosophy from Aristotle to Tibor Scitovsky. The worry: if we pay people for their contributions, will the payment crowd out the intrinsic motivations that originally drove the contributions? Will the teacher who teaches because she loves teaching, when paid Koin flows for that teaching, slowly become the teacher who teaches because she is paid for it? And if so, is that a loss?

The empirical literature on what economists call "crowding-out" is, in a word, mixed. Some studies find that introducing monetary rewards for previously-unpaid behaviors reduces the rate of those behaviors over time. The classic example is the Israeli daycare study: when daycares began fining parents for late pickups, the rate of late pickups went up, because the fine reframed lateness as a service the parent was paying for rather than an imposition on the daycare staff. Other studies find that monetary recognition complements rather than replaces intrinsic motivation: musicians who begin receiving royalties for their compositions report higher, not lower, satisfaction with their work.

The general principle that emerges is that monetary compensation crowds out intrinsic motivation when it is experienced as transactional and external — a payment in exchange for behavior the payer wants to elicit. It does not crowd out intrinsic motivation when it is experienced as recognition and resourcing — an acknowledgment that the work being done has value, freeing the doer from having to do something else to survive.

Koin is designed to be experienced as the second kind. Its flows are not payments-in-exchange-for-behavior; they are continuous returns on contributions already made, computed automatically, with no transactional moment that the contributor experiences as "now I do this because the system pays for it." A teacher who teaches because she loves teaching continues to teach because she loves teaching. The flows that arrive in her account from her former students' downstream work are not the reason she teaches; they are the reason she can afford to keep teaching when she would otherwise have to take a higher-paying job in a less-meaningful field.

This distinction matters enormously. The current bookkeeping forces a continuous tension between meaningful work and survival. Many of the people whose contributions Koin is designed to recognize have, throughout history, had to choose between doing the work they were called to do and earning enough to live. The tension produces, at the aggregate level, the systematic underproduction of meaningful work — fewer teachers, fewer caregivers, fewer artists, fewer thinkers than the world needs. Koin does not change why people do meaningful work. It removes the requirement that they choose between meaning and survival.

A subtler point. Some forms of contribution are, by their nature, corrupted by even non-transactional compensation. The friend who sits with you in the dark is doing something that would be lessened by any form of attribution. The act of comforting requires that the comforter not be doing it for record. Koin's carve-out for the unmeasured exists precisely to protect these forms. The contributor who insists that some hours remain outside the system has the system's full support; the architecture protects the choice.

For the contributions that do enter the system, the evidence — from the music royalty tradition, from the academic citation tradition, from the early Koin pilots described in Chapter X — is consistent: the contributors who participate report higher, not lower, engagement with their work. They are not extrinsically motivated to do what they were already intrinsically motivated to do. They are intrinsically motivated, and they are now also paid. The two are not in tension. They are complementary.

The deeper philosophical answer to the meaning question is that humans have always done meaningful work and have always benefited from being seen for doing it. Honor systems, reputation economies, gift-giving traditions, ceremonial recognition — every premodern society had elaborate systems for acknowledging contribution that operated alongside (and sometimes instead of) monetary compensation. The shift to money-only attribution that characterized industrial modernity was not the natural state of human contribution; it was a peculiar phase in which the bookkeeping became so coarse that only one form of recognition remained legible. Koin restores the multiplicity. The teacher who is paid through Koin is, in some sense, returning to a more ancient relationship with her own work — one in which the acknowledgment of contribution is a normal part of the doing rather than a separate transactional event.

The worry that payment will corrupt meaning is a worry that confuses the wrong kinds of payment with the right kinds. Koin is the right kind. Meaning and recognition have always coexisted; modernity briefly forgot.

A note on what doesn't change

Manifestos describe the change they propose. They are less reliable about what stays the same. It is worth being explicit about what does not change in the world Koin builds, because the absences are often more important than the presences for understanding what kind of world we are arguing for.

Money does not disappear. People still buy groceries, pay rent, settle bills, accumulate savings. The currency itself is largely unaffected; what changes is how a portion of every transaction is distributed across the contribution graph rather than captured by the immediate counterparties. For most people in most transactions, the texture of money is roughly what it was. The Koin layer operates underneath, automatically, like the GST or VAT or sales-tax layers that have been added to commerce over the past century.

Property does not disappear. People still own homes, businesses, intellectual property. Koin redistributes a slice of the value generated by those assets back through the contribution chains that made them possible — but the basic property right remains. The home you own remains yours; the business you built remains yours; the patent you hold remains yours. What changes is that the value flowing from those assets to you also flows, in smaller proportions, to the upstream contributors whose work made you the kind of person who could build them.

Work does not disappear. The economy still needs people to do things — to teach the children, treat the sick, design the buildings, run the trains. The wages those workers receive remain wages. Koin supplements wages with attribution flows; it does not replace them. The expectation that adults do socially useful work for living incomes remains intact. What changes is that the work whose value was historically uncompensated (care, teaching, mentorship, the conversation that mattered) now also produces income, alongside the wage-eligible work.

Inequality does not disappear. People who contribute more receive more; the receivers of large flows are wealthier than the receivers of small flows. The Koin world has, by design, an unequal distribution of flows. What changes is the SHAPE of the inequality — the wealthiest are no longer the heirs of capital and the owners of platforms, but the contributors whose influence is widest and deepest. The Koin equivalent of a billionaire is a teacher whose framework shaped a generation, an inventor whose tool is used everywhere, a musician whose song everyone knows. The inequality persists; the criteria for being on top of it change.

Injustice does not disappear. Koin does not solve oppression. It does not solve violence. It does not solve the asymmetries of political power, the legacies of colonialism, the ongoing harms inflicted on the marginalized. Koin handles one specific failure of bookkeeping; it does not handle the other failures of social arrangement. The work of addressing those other failures remains the work of politics, law, organizing, mutual aid, and direct action. Koin is the change to the economy. It is not a substitute for the change to everything else.

Death does not disappear. People still die. Koin does not make them less dead; it makes them less forgotten. The grief, the loss, the irreplaceability remain what they were. The bookkeeping has been improved; the human condition has not been replaced.

Most importantly, the unmeasured does not disappear. The friend in the dark, the hour with the dying parent, the line in the journal no one reads, the kindness shown to a stranger — these remain outside the system. Koin builds a ledger; the ledger is bounded; what is outside the ledger remains intact, protected, and held by people rather than by code.

The world Koin builds is not a new world. It is the old world with a corrected bookkeeping. The improvement is real and important; the limits of what bookkeeping can do remain what they were.

V — Lineage, objections, and the ask

This idea is old. We are not the first to see that money fails to track contribution. The synthesis is new; the sightings are not.

Henry George (1839–1897) saw that the value of land comes from the community around it, not from the owner — and proposed in Progress and Poverty (1879) that the rents extracted should flow back to the community via a single tax on land value. He was eighty percent of the way to Koin, applied to one asset class. His insight: the value is in the network, not in the owner. — George remains in print 145 years later because no one has answered him.

Marcel Mauss (1872–1950), writing in 1925, studied the gift economies of the Pacific and the Pacific Northwest and showed that gifts created bonds — that the object of exchange carried obligation, identity, history. He saw the artifact-of-trade-is-the-record-of-bond pattern in cultures that had never abandoned it. His Essai sur le don founded economic anthropology and is the single most important precursor to Koin's understanding of trade.

Silvio Gesell (1862–1930) proposed currency that decays — demurrage — so money would have to keep moving and could not be hoarded. Schwanenkirchen and Wörgl ran successful Gesellian experiments during the Great Depression before central banks shut them down. Koin's haze primitive does this naturally; old contributions fade unless reinforced. — Gesell's The Natural Economic Order, 1916; admired by Keynes, who called Gesell "an unduly neglected prophet."

Karl Polanyi (1886–1964), in The Great Transformation (1944), traced the "disembedding" of the economy from social relations — the historical moment when markets became autonomous from the obligations and bonds they used to be part of. Polanyi predicted that disembedded markets would generate counter-movements (welfare states, regulation, eventually fascism) until the embedding was restored. Koin proposes one form of re-embedding: instead of regulating money from outside, build a ledger that natively encodes the bonds money was extracted from.

Lewis Hyde, in The Gift (1983), argued that creative work belongs to a gift economy that runs underneath the commercial one, and that compensation in the gift economy is properly indirect: the writer is fed by the readers her readers became. Hyde gave the artistic class the language to defend their economic logic to the bureaucrats who do not understand it. Koin operationalizes Hyde's distinction.

Ronald Coase (1910–2013) argued in 1937 that the size of firms is determined by transaction costs — that you internalize a function inside a firm when the cost of contracting it out is too high. Koin drives those transaction costs toward zero by automating the contract. The implication: many functions currently locked inside firms could re-externalize, paid as Koin flows to networks of contributors. The shape of the firm changes. — Coase, "The Nature of the Firm," 1937; Nobel Prize 1991.

Elinor Ostrom (1933–2012) showed that the commons is not doomed — that human communities have for centuries successfully managed shared resources (fisheries, forests, irrigation) without privatization or state control. Her eight design principles for commons governance map almost one-to-one to the requirements for a non-extractive Koin. — Ostrom, Governing the Commons, 1990; Nobel Prize 2009.

Michael Goldhaber, in 1997, named the attention economy — observing that attention had become the actual scarce resource and that its movement was the real economic activity, despite the bookkeeping continuing to measure dollars. The two decades since have proven him right. Koin generalizes from attention to influence: not just who looked but who was changed.

David Graeber (1961–2020), in Debt: The First 5,000 Years (2011), demonstrated that credit relationships preceded money by millennia; that the gift economy and the market economy are not different evolutionary stages but coexisting modes of human exchange; and that "debt" is the lingering social bond imperfectly priced. Koin makes the bond visible, not as a debt to be cleared but as a stream to be honored.

Citation networks in the academy have been a primitive Koin since the seventeenth century — unpaid, unsettled, but tracking influence across generations of thought, surviving the death of every participant. Eugene Garfield's Science Citation Index (1955) was the first attempt to build the influence graph at scale. Sixty years later, Larry Page and Sergey Brin applied the same primitive to the web (PageRank, 1998), and the world saw what the mathematical kernel of Koin can do when it is computed at scale.

Each of these saw a piece. Each ran into the wall of the technologies of their time. The synthesis was not available because the apparatus was not available. The apparatus is here now. Koin is the synthesis these earlier sightings have been waiting for.

Objections, considered

A manifesto that ducks its objections is propaganda. The strongest arguments against Koin deserve direct answers.

"This is surveillance." The graph is, by definition, a record of what moved you. But Koin is opt-in, the data is encrypted, and the verdict is computed locally on user-controlled hardware whenever possible. The most surveillance-friendly Koin implementation is the corporate-owned one — which is precisely the implementation Section XVII argues against. A commons-governed, open-protocol Koin generates less surveillance than the ad-tech industry already does to track what you click. The right comparison is not "Koin vs. nothing" but "Koin vs. the current attention-harvesting baseline." Koin gives the contributor a payment in exchange for visibility that the ad networks already harvest for free.

"It will be gamed." Any system of value can be gamed; the question is how the gaming behavior compares to the gaming behavior of the system it replaces. Money is gamed at industrial scale: tax evasion, transfer pricing, wash trades, market manipulation, leveraged speculation that produces no value. Citation networks are gamed (citation rings, predatory journals). PageRank was gamed (link farms). In every case the response was iterative improvement of the verdict-rendering, not abandonment of the system. Koin's specific defense: the verdict is keyed to verifiable downstream effect, which is harder to fake than a star rating because the receiver's later behavior either does or does not show absorption. The cost of gaming Koin convincingly is roughly the cost of actually contributing.

"It corrupts giving." This worry comes from the gift-economy tradition (Mauss, Hyde) — that putting a number on a gift destroys what makes it a gift. The objection is sound but mis-aimed. Koin's carve-out (Section XIII) is the answer: the unmeasured remains. You choose what enters the ledger. The hour you give a friend in the dark is not a Koin event unless you decide to make it one — and the system has no way of finding out about it unless you tell it. Koin handles what was already commercial-adjacent (writing, music, teaching, advice, software) and leaves the genuinely intimate alone. The distinction has to be defended every year against the impulse to measure more.

"It widens inequality. Popular voices will get most of the koin." This is the most serious objection. Influence is power-law distributed; a few thinkers reach millions while most reach few. If Koin tracks influence faithfully, won't it produce hyper-concentration?

Three answers. First, even unequal Koin is more equal than current capital — because current capital is captured almost entirely by intermediaries (platforms, publishers, employers) rather than originators. Returning the value to originators, even if those originators are themselves unequal, redistributes from the very-rich corporate few to the moderately-rich influential many. Second, the haze primitive prevents perpetual concentration: contributions that stop being absorbed stop generating flow. The wealthiest minds of one generation give way to the wealthiest of the next, who give way again. Third, the Shapley adjustment redistributes non-redundant credit: a contribution that everyone has heard reaches diminishing marginal returns; a contribution that uniquely shapes a small but consequential audience can outpay a viral one. The math fights the winner-take-all dynamic that pure influence-counts would produce.

"It's just royalties for everything — and royalties don't pay much." Existing royalty systems (ASCAP, book royalties) pay little because the rates are negotiated against captured distribution channels. The performance royalty for a streamed song is approximately $0.003. The author's royalty on a paperback is approximately 10% of cover. Both numbers are artifacts of the bargaining power asymmetry between creators and platforms. Koin's structural change is removing the platform's leverage. When distribution is one weighted edge among many — not the gatekeeper — the verdict redistributes the full value of the contribution, not the leftover after rent.

"It needs too much infrastructure to ever exist." Koin can be deployed domain by domain. The first deployments do not need to talk to each other. A classroom Koin that pays teachers for student outcomes does not need to interoperate with a music Koin that pays songwriters for plays. Each domain proves the verdict-rendering and the settlement separately. Federation comes later, once multiple verticals have shipped and the value of cross-domain provenance becomes visible. We are at the recipe stage: prove one cuisine, then standardize.

"It will become Nosedive eventually." This is the question Section VII addresses directly. The shortest answer: Koin pays; it does not gate. The moment a Koin balance starts gating anything, that is the moment Koin has become something else and must be opposed. The defense is institutional, not technical. The same vigilance citizens needed to keep credit scores from determining medical care is the vigilance they will need to keep Koin balances from determining anything other than what arrives in the contributor's account.

The objections do not refute the proposal. They name the things we must defend if the proposal is to work.

Why this won't be coopted

Every honest reader of this manifesto will, by now, have asked the obvious question: what stops this from becoming someone's monopoly? What stops a corporation from owning the graph, charging rent on it, and recreating the exact extraction Koin was meant to dissolve?

Three things, if we build it right.

First, the graph must be commons. No single entity owns the ledger of who-influenced-whom. There can be many providers, each with their own implementation, but the graph itself — the record of the bonds — is jointly held, like the calendar, like the alphabet, like the metric system. A standard, not a product. The companies that succeed in Koin are the ones who make the system clearer and more honest, not the ones who own a piece of it. Elinor Ostrom's eight principles for commons governance are the template here.

Second, the verdict-rendering computation must be open. The methods that turn "this work was absorbed by this mind" into "this much koin flows here" must be inspectable, contestable, and not the proprietary secret of any company. The judge cannot be private. Anyone must be able to verify the verdict, the way anyone can verify a mathematical proof. Closed-weight models cannot render Koin's verdicts. Open-source, auditable, and forkable implementations must be the only kind that count.

Third, the settlement must be plural. Koin is not a token issued by an authority. It is a unit of account that any payment rail can implement. You can pay koin in dollars, euros, bitcoin, time, calories — anything that can be metered. No single substance is the koin; any substance can be. The protocol is the standard; the rails compete to implement it.

The corporate move — own the graph, capture the rents, become the new gatekeeper — is the move we have to outrun. The first credible non-extractive implementation, made open and made standard, becomes the one the world uses. Every closed implementation that arrives later is competing against an already-free protocol that produces the same verdict. The window is the few years between the technology being possible and the closed versions being normal.

This is the same race the early Internet ran, and won. TCP/IP became standard before any company could own the network. HTTP became standard before any company could own the web. Email became standard before any company could own messaging. Each of these had a window of closure that closed. Koin has one too. We have, perhaps, five years.

This is why the ask is now.

The ask

Build with Koin, even at small scale.

Build a classroom where the student's question is logged and her later success credits the question. Build a writing tool that watches what you read and, when you publish, traces the influence. Build a village where the residents pay each other in proportion to how much each one's thinking shows up in the others'. Build a kitchen where the recipe gets the verdict. Build a music app where every play settles into the influence graph that produced the song. Build a journal where every cited paper pays its citations. Build a model that pays the people whose writing trained it. None of this is hard, anymore. All of it is being prototyped, today, inside PEP.

Make it open. Make it standard. Refuse to own the graph.

The first system to do this honestly — and to do it without trying to own the ledger — is the system the world is going to choose. Not because anyone decreed it, but because once a worker is offered a system where the value she creates flows back to her, she will not voluntarily return to a system where it does not. Once a chef is offered a recipe royalty that pays her every meal, she will not voluntarily return to a one-time check. Once a teacher is offered compensation that scales with the lives she changed, she will not voluntarily return to a salary that does not see her students. Once an AI's training corpus is paid, the closed-corpus models that did not pay will be morally and competitively obsolete.

The verdict, when accurate, is irresistible. Money was the technology we used while we waited for the ledger of minds. The ledger is here.

An economy is a verdict. The verdict can now be honest. Build it before someone closes it.

First moves, in concrete

If the ask is "build it," it is worth being explicit about what the first moves look like. There is a tendency, in manifestos, to issue large summons without naming the small actions. The small actions are where the work actually happens. The list below is partial; it is meant to illustrate the texture of beginning, not to exhaust the possibilities.

If you teach. Talk to your administration about whether your district could pilot the Mills River model. The full proposal is in Chapter X. Ask whether 2.5% of your school's discretionary budget could be reserved for influence-trace distribution to teachers, beginning with the lesson-plan-sharing graph you almost certainly already maintain informally. The technology for a small classroom-scale Koin is available off-the-shelf in 2026; the political work is the harder part.

If you write code. Use one of the existing open-source funding mechanisms as a bridge toward a federated Koin layer. GitHub Sponsors and Open Collective are imperfect precursors; supporting them now, and pushing them toward more rigorous influence-graph attribution, accelerates the path. If you maintain a library that others depend on, register it with an attribution-aware platform; if you depend on libraries, support the maintainers via the existing channels.

If you write or make music or produce creative work. Document your influence graph. Keep records — not for legal purposes, but for future Koin systems that will eventually be able to ingest them — of who taught you, who shaped a specific work, who heard the first draft. Acknowledgment sections in books, liner notes on albums, credits in films are already the rough form of this. Make yours more precise, more granular, more honest. The graph you document now becomes the graph the system uses later.

If you research. Cite more carefully. The citation network is the proto-Koin most ready for upgrade. Push your discipline toward the federated attribution standards that the OKRC model anticipates. Argue, in faculty meetings, that the next major grant cycle should include the Koin-routing clause described in Chapter X. Sponsor a small departmental pilot.

If you organize politically. The legislation that protects open-Koin standards has to come from somewhere. National Koin Standards Acts will eventually be drafted; the model legislation needs draft language, advocates, sponsors. The cooperative-economy organizations, the labor unions, the open-source foundations, and the platform-regulation advocates are natural coalitions. Begin the conversations now.

If you have capital. Fund commons infrastructure. The open implementations need engineering, governance, and dispute-resolution machinery. They will not be funded by ordinary venture capital because they are not designed to capture returns. They will be funded by philanthropy, public investment, and patient capital from people who understand that the commons must exist before the value can be created. If you have access to any of those forms of funding, point them here.

If you have nothing in particular to offer. Read this manifesto carefully. Argue with it. Find the friend who has been doing invisible work and tell them what the system is going to do, eventually, for them. Be the kind of person who already, before the bookkeeping has caught up, sees what the bookkeeping is going to see. The cultural readiness for Koin is itself an asset. The system needs people who already know that the people around them have been contributing what the bookkeeping could not see.

A note on the small voice

There is a temptation, when reading a manifesto of this scope, to feel that one's own contribution to the work is too small to matter. The manifesto wants to refuse this. The whole logic of Koin is that small contributions compound. A teacher who shapes one lesson-plan that is adopted by twelve other teachers; a developer who writes one library that is used by twelve other libraries; a friend who says one sentence that propagates through twelve other conversations — each of these is small at the moment of contribution and large by the time the trace is computed.

The same is true for the work of building Koin. A single small pilot in a single district is large by the time it has been copied by ten other districts and refined by the contributions of ten thousand other teachers. The history of every successful institutional change reduces, on close inspection, to a sequence of small actions taken by people who could not see the eventual scale.

You do not need to fix the system. You need to take a step the size of yours.

The ask is not to be a hero. The ask is to be the next person who does the next thing the system needs.

VI — Stories from the world

Helena Vargas lived in Lima. She was forty-seven. She had spent the previous twenty-three years cooking in three different restaurants — a tourist place near Larcomar, a family bistro in San Isidro, and most recently the line at a well-known restaurant where she ran the cold station for the last six years. She made $1,800 a month, in soles. Her younger daughter was about to start university.

In December of 2031 she wrote down a recipe. It was a tiradito with mango, aji limo, and a brown-butter chimichurri that she had been refining since 2018. She submitted it to a Koin-enabled production kitchen in Lima — they paid a small upfront for the submission, the standard fee — and gave her a 4.2% royalty on every plate sold downstream.

The first month, fifty plates were sold across three pilot restaurants. Her royalty was twelve dollars. The second month, a hundred and forty plates: thirty-eight dollars. By month six, the recipe had been licensed by a chain of upscale ceviche bars across Latin America: two thousand plates a week, returning her about three hundred dollars a month.

Within eighteen months the recipe entered a packaged-food line — frozen ceviche bowls sold in Lima, Bogotá, Mexico City, Santiago. Helena's royalty became her primary income. She kept cooking, but on her own terms. She opened a small place on Calle Berlin that she ran in the afternoons. Her daughter graduated, debt-free.

Helena's name appears on every box of every frozen bowl ever made from her recipe. She had never been asked to sign a contract with the food company. She did not need to. The recipe is the contract. The royalty is the receipt.

By 2050 — the year the coda describes — the recipe had been cooked or sold roughly seventeen million times. Helena was sixty-six. She no longer cooked professionally. The royalty stream paid her $2,400 a month. She had named her two daughters as her Koin heirs. When she dies, the stream will continue, splitting between them and a small fund for the family of the prep cook who first tested the recipe with her years before. He had never asked for credit. The system found him anyway.

The recipe is still being made tonight.

Mrs. Adesanya, who taught

Folasade Adesanya retired from teaching in 2034. She had taught secondary-school mathematics in Ibadan for forty-one years. By the time she stepped down, she had taught roughly seven thousand students.

In 2034 her pension was about $180 a month, in naira. Three of her former students had become physicists, one quite famous. Several dozen had become engineers. Hundreds had become teachers themselves. Most had become other things — businesspeople, parents, civil servants, drivers, traders. She kept a notebook with a name on each line and, when she could find out, what they were doing.

Koin came to her school district in 2036. It was opt-in. She opted in. So did about a third of her former students, the ones she had stayed in touch with. The rest, she assumed, would never know to register her as a contributor.

What surprised her was how many of them did know. The system did not require students to remember. It traced influence backward from the work the students were doing. A former student who had become a software engineer in Lagos wrote a paper, in 2038, on a routing algorithm. The paper's mathematical structure traced back through three textbooks, two of which Mrs. Adesanya's school had used. Spreading activation found her name on the class roster from 2003. She received a small flow.

Another former student, by then a director at a national health ministry, ran an analysis of disease spread that depended on a kind of probability reasoning she had first encountered in Mrs. Adesanya's room. Activation found her. The flow began.

By 2042 Mrs. Adesanya was receiving about $940 a month from Koin. Her former students, scattered across four continents, paid her — most of them without ever knowing they were paying her — in proportion to the cognitive shape she had given them.

She used the money to renovate the school building she had taught in. She named the renovated wing after her own teacher, a man named Mr. Owoeye, who had died in 1981 and whose Koin balance she had set up retroactively, naming the surviving community library three towns over as the lineage heir.

Mr. Owoeye's balance, in 2042, was small but non-zero. The students who shaped Mrs. Adesanya remembered Mr. Owoeye. The flow had found him too.

Yui, whose song outlived her

Yui Tanaka wrote songs in Tokyo in the 2010s and 2020s. She was moderately successful by Japanese standards — she released four albums, had two minor hits, played small theaters across Asia. She died in 2029 at the age of fifty-four.

In her lifetime she had earned, from music, perhaps the equivalent of forty thousand dollars a year on average. Most of it came from live performance. Her royalties from streamed music averaged about three thousand dollars a year — the standard fate of all but the most-streamed artists in the platform-economy of her decade.

She had no spouse. She named her younger sister, a music teacher in Osaka, as her heir.

The year after Yui's death, a song from her second album — "Kogarashi," released in 2017 — was picked up as the closing-credits song for a Korean drama series. It was streamed forty million times in twelve weeks. Under the old streaming royalty model, Yui's estate would have received about $1,400 from that.

Under Koin, the picture was different. The drama's licensing paid Koin; Koin traced every play through the influence graph. Yui's royalty: about $87,000. Her sister, the heir: about $42,000 of it. The rest distributed by Koin's automatic upstream-trace to the guitar teacher who had given Yui her first lesson at age fourteen, to the older brother of a friend who had played Yui a record that influenced the song's structure, to a folk singer in Hokkaido whose vocal phrasing Yui had absorbed and whose songbook had been quietly out of print for a decade.

By 2050 Yui has been dead for twenty-one years. "Kogarashi" continues to be licensed, covered, sampled. Her sister, by then in her seventies, still receives a steady monthly flow. The folk singer in Hokkaido — also dead by now — has a posthumous Koin balance that funds a small archive of Japanese folk music. VII — Technical reference

Koin's mathematical foundation is the PTO master equation, stated in Part III as:

PTO master equationδ ∫ [ T − Φ(T + D) ] dτ = 0

This section derives it from first principles, so the reader can verify that the equation is not arbitrary but the unique answer to a precise question.

Setup. Treat the economy as a collection of minds, each in some cognitive state σ_i evolving over time. Influence between minds is encoded by edges; the influence arriving at mind i is the weighted sum of upstream states, delayed by transmission time:

Influence arriving at mind iU_i(τ) = Σ_j w_ji · σ_j(τ − δ_ji)

Each mind has a utility function L_i it is trying to maximize — the utility of its own state, given its goals, attention, well-being. The instantaneous improvement in i's utility caused by absorbed influence is what we call constructive transformation:

Constructive transformationT_i(τ) = ⟨ ∂L_i/∂σ_i , ∂σ_i/∂U_i ⟩ · U_i(τ)

Not all incoming influence becomes constructive. Some is lost — distorted by the channel, mistimed, untranslatable to the receiver's frame. This loss is dissipation:

Dissipation at mind iD_i(τ) = ‖ U_i − Proj_Bandwidth(U_i) ‖²

The projection operator captures the receiver's bandwidth: there is a maximum rate at which any mind can absorb new structure. Influence that exceeds the bandwidth is wasted. The bandwidth itself is a function of mind i's state — arousal, attention, prior context — and is modeled by the Rayleigh quotient:

Rayleigh quotient bandwidth functionalΦ_i(x) = ⟨ x, A_i x ⟩ / ⟨ x, x ⟩

where A_i is the absorption operator of mind i — a positive semidefinite operator encoding which directions of influence the mind can absorb and how much. The Rayleigh quotient ranges from 0 (no absorption) to ‖A_i‖ (maximum absorption along the principal axis).

The variational principle. What does the economy maximize? Not absolute transformation — that could be achieved by force-feeding minds beyond their bandwidth, wasting most of the input. Not absolute output — that does not account for the cost of producing the influence. The correct quantity is transformation net of bandwidth-limited dissipation: how much constructive change happens, minus how much is lost. Summed across all minds and all time:

Total system valueV = ∫ Σ_i [ T_i(τ) − Φ_i(T_i + D_i) ] dτ

The action principle says that the economy evolves along paths that make V stationary — that is, paths where δV = 0 under small variations in the trajectory. This gives the master equation as the per-mind Euler-Lagrange condition:

PTO master equation, full formδ ∫ [ T − Φ(T + D) ] dτ = 0

Interpretation. In words: at every moment, the system rewards influence trajectories that maximize constructive transformation per unit of dissipation, subject to the receiver's bandwidth. Koin's payments are the gradient of V with respect to each contributor's influence. The contribution that increases V most receives the most koin.

A worked numerical example. Consider a teacher T₀ influencing three students S₁, S₂, S₃ in a single moment. Suppose:

Inputsedge weights: w₀₁ = 0.4, w₀₂ = 0.6, w₀₃ = 0.3
state changes: Δσ₁ = 100, Δσ₂ = 200, Δσ₃ = 50
dissipation: D₁ = 20, D₂ = 30, D₃ = 40
bandwidths: Φ₁ = 0.5, Φ₂ = 0.4, Φ₃ = 0.6

Computing the per-student contribution to V:

Per-student contribution from T₀V₁ = 0.4 · (100 − 0.5 · 120) = 0.4 · 40 = 16.0
V₂ = 0.6 · (200 − 0.4 · 230) = 0.6 · 108 = 64.8
V₃ = 0.3 · (50 − 0.6 · 90) = 0.3 · (−4) = −1.2

Total instantaneous Koin generated by T₀: 16.0 + 64.8 − 1.2 = 79.6 units. (Note the negative contribution to S₃: the teacher exceeded that student's bandwidth, generating more confusion than learning — the system charges the teacher slightly. Real Koin clamps negative contributions to zero; harm is not deducted, but neither is wasted effort credited.)

Across all moments and all receivers, the integral of these quantities is the teacher's total Koin balance.

The Lagrangian formulation, explained for everyone

This subsection is for the reader who saw the PTO equation above and wondered what it actually means. It is also for the reader who has never encountered the word "Lagrangian" before. It introduces, from scratch with no assumed background, one of the most beautiful and useful mathematical frameworks in all of science — and shows that Koin's central equation is an instance of it. If you are a physicist or engineer, you can skim. If you have never taken a math class beyond high school, please don't skip — this is one of the chapters most worth reading.

Start with throwing a ball. If you throw a ball, it follows a specific path through the air. The path is curved — what mathematicians call a parabola. It is the path the ball actually takes.

Imagine, for a moment, every other path the ball could have taken. It could have gone straight up and then dropped. It could have zigzagged. It could have looped around. There are infinitely many paths between the moment you release the ball and the moment it lands. The ball picks exactly one.

For two thousand years, mathematicians and natural philosophers asked: why this path? What makes nature pick the parabola and not the zigzag?

The answer was discovered, in pieces, between roughly 1700 and 1834. It is one of the deepest insights in the history of science. In plain English, it goes like this. For every possible path the ball could take, you can compute a single number. The number depends on the path. The path nature actually picks is the path for which that number is the smallest — or, more technically, the path for which the number stays the same under small wiggles in either direction.

The fancy name for this single number is the action. The mathematical recipe for computing the action from a path is called the Lagrangian. For a thrown ball, the recipe is: at every instant along the path, compute the ball's kinetic energy (the energy of its motion), subtract the ball's potential energy (the energy stored in its height above the ground), and add up those differences over the whole path. The path that makes this sum smallest is the path you see.

This is, when you sit with it, staggering. It means nature is, in some sense, solving an optimization problem. Out of all possible behaviors of all possible objects, the ones we observe are the ones that minimize a particular quantity. This insight, sometimes called the principle of least action, is the deepest known fact about how the physical world organizes itself.

The same principle works for everything. The same principle, with different Lagrangian recipes, governs the path of light through a lens, the orbit of a planet, the trajectory of a satellite, the propagation of an electromagnetic wave, the geometry of space and time near a black hole, and the behavior of every fundamental particle in physics. Almost every advanced physical theory is built by writing down the right Lagrangian and finding the path of minimum action. The recipe changes; the principle does not.

Why does this matter for an economics manifesto? Because the PTO master equation we wrote earlier in this chapter is, in the language we now have, an action principle. The expression inside the square brackets — T minus Φ(T + D) — is a Lagrangian. The δ-symbol (read aloud as "delta") is the variational symbol, which means "we are looking for the path that makes the action stationary." The integral over dτ is the total action over the trajectory. The equation says: out of all possible ways influence could flow through the graph of contributions, the one that the system picks is the one that maximizes the integral of (useful transformation minus bandwidth-limited dissipation) over time.

Translated more directly: when minds influence other minds, credit flows along the paths that produce the most useful change in receivers, relative to how much is wasted in the process. This is the same kind of optimization principle that governs the trajectory of a thrown ball, applied to a different domain — economic influence rather than physical motion. The mathematical structure is identical; the application is new.

Why the analogy is precise, not metaphorical. In classical mechanics, the Lagrangian is "kinetic energy minus potential energy." Kinetic energy is the energy of motion — how fast something is changing. Potential energy is stored energy — what the system could release if conditions were right. The Lagrangian is the difference between them.

In PTO, the Lagrangian is "constructive transformation minus bandwidth-limited dissipation." Constructive transformation is the rate at which receiving minds are being improved — how fast minds are changing in useful ways. Bandwidth-limited dissipation is the energy lost when an upstream contributor tries to send more influence than the downstream receiver can absorb. The Lagrangian is the difference between them.

The two formulations are structurally the same. They both describe a system that, at every moment, has something productive going on and something costly going on. The system follows whichever trajectory makes the cumulative productive-minus-costly quantity as large as possible. The mathematics is borrowed; the borrowing is exact, not metaphorical.

What this buys Koin. Recognizing PTO as a Lagrangian system is not just rhetorical elegance. It gives Koin access to two and a half centuries of physical and mathematical machinery built on top of the Lagrangian framework. The benefits are substantial.

Equations of motion for credit flow. In classical mechanics, taking the derivative of the Lagrangian with respect to position and velocity and setting it equal to zero gives a differential equation — the Euler-Lagrange equation — that describes how the system evolves. Applied to PTO, the same procedure gives an equation that describes how credit propagates through the influence graph over time. The spreading-activation algorithm we use in practice is, formally, an approximation to the solution of this equation. Recognizing this lets us prove that the algorithm is correct, identify when it might break down, and refine it when it does.

Conservation laws. Emmy Noether, in 1915, proved one of the most important theorems in all of mathematics. Every continuous symmetry of the Lagrangian — every way you can change the situation without changing the equation — corresponds to a conserved quantity, something that cannot be created or destroyed by the dynamics. In physics, this gives energy conservation (from time symmetry), momentum conservation (from spatial symmetry), and angular momentum conservation (from rotational symmetry). In Koin, Noether's theorem gives us conservation laws too. The fact that relabeling contributors doesn't change verdicts gives a conservation law that guarantees identity-independence of distributions. The fact that the same flow pattern repeated next year produces the same total credit gives a conservation law about long-run accumulation. These are not just nice properties; they are mathematical guarantees that the system behaves correctly. Any deviation from these conservation laws is, by Noether's theorem, provably a bug in the implementation.

Path integrals. Richard Feynman, in the 1940s, extended the Lagrangian framework in a remarkable way. Instead of thinking about a single path the system takes, he proposed summing over every possible path, weighted by a factor involving the action. In physics, this gave us quantum mechanics. In Koin, the same machinery gives us something almost as useful: a principled way to handle uncertainty about influence. Instead of "the credit flowed back along this one specific path," the path-integral formulation says "the credit flowed back through every possible path, weighted by how plausible each path is." When you do not know exactly who influenced whom — which is often, in practice — the path integral tells you exactly how the credit should be distributed across the plausibility of different attribution stories. This is mathematically more refined than the deterministic spreading-activation we use today, and could be deployed for high-stakes disputed cases.

Field theory. When the influence graph is so dense that it stops looking like discrete nodes and edges and starts looking like a continuous field — every paragraph slightly influencing every other paragraph, every brain cell slightly entangled with every other — the Lagrangian formulation extends gracefully to a "Lagrangian density," which is a Lagrangian per unit of contribution-space. This is the framework you would need to do Koin at the scale of an AI training corpus, where there isn't a clean discrete graph but a continuous fabric of influence. The mathematics already exists, developed by physicists studying continuous media; it is waiting to be applied.

Hamiltonian formulation. A standard mathematical move — the Legendre transform — converts the Lagrangian into something called the Hamiltonian. Same physics, same predictions, different mathematical lens. The Hamiltonian formulation gives access to phase-space dynamics, conjugate variables, and a different set of numerical methods. For Koin, this means we can think of "the state of the influence economy" as a point in a high-dimensional space evolving over time, rather than as a series of discrete credit-assignment events. This is useful when modeling Koin's long-term behavior and when designing real-time stabilization algorithms.

Numerical methods. Two and a half centuries of physics have produced an enormous toolkit of numerical methods for solving Lagrangian systems efficiently. Symplectic integrators, finite-element methods, variational integrators — all of these are battle-tested in physics and would carry over directly to Koin's larger-scale computations. We don't have to reinvent the numerical machinery; we inherit it.

What this means in practice. If you implement Koin without ever thinking about the Lagrangian, you have an algorithm — spreading activation, Shapley adjustment, decay. The algorithm produces verdicts. The verdicts are reasonable. Most Koin pilots will work fine at this level.

If you implement Koin with the Lagrangian framework in mind, you get the same algorithm but with three additional capabilities. You can prove that the algorithm preserves the conservation laws it should preserve. You can extend the algorithm to handle uncertainty, continuity, and field-theoretic regimes that the basic version cannot handle. You can adopt numerical methods that physics has spent centuries perfecting. The basic Koin works without the Lagrangian; the advanced Koin needs it.

The deepest analogy. Imagine, finally, what it means that an economic system shares mathematical structure with classical mechanics. Both are systems in which observable behavior emerges from an underlying principle of optimization. In physics, the optimization is over kinetic and potential energy along a trajectory. In Koin, the optimization is over constructive and dissipative effects across an influence graph. The physical world organizes itself by minimizing action; the Koin economy, we claim, organizes itself by maximizing the same kind of integrated quantity.

This is, in a small but real way, a unification. The same mathematical principle that explains why planets orbit and why light bends explains why credit should flow back through the graph of contributions to the people who shaped each downstream beneficiary. The principle is older than physics; it predates Newton; it appears, in primitive form, in the optimization arguments of medieval scholars. Applied to economics — specifically, to the economics of influence — it gives Koin a theoretical foundation as deep and as rigorous as any in modern science.

The Lagrangian is the framework that turns Koin from a craft into a science. The craft works; the science is what lets the craft be extended, audited, and trusted at scale.

One sentence to take away. The PTO equation is a Lagrangian; the Lagrangian is the deepest mathematical structure in physics; the same structure, applied to economic influence, gives Koin its theoretical backbone — and you, as a reader, do not need to know any of this to participate in Koin, but it is what makes the system worth participating in. The math is in the floor. The floor holds because the math is right.

Credit assignment — spreading activation and Shapley

The credit-assignment problem: given a benefit b arriving at node j, distribute credit backward across the influence graph to upstream contributors.

The algorithm. Spreading activation is iterative backward propagation with damping. Pseudocode:

Spreading activation, pseudocodefunction spread_credit(graph G, benefit b, sink j, damping d, threshold ε):
    credit = {node: 0 for node in G.nodes}
    queue = [(j, b)]
    while queue is not empty:
        (node, magnitude) = queue.pop()
        if magnitude < ε: continue
        credit[node] += magnitude
        for each incoming_edge (src → node) with weight w:
            propagated = d × w × magnitude
            queue.push((src, propagated))
    return credit

The damping factor d ∈ (0, 1) prevents infinite recursion through circular influence; ε is the threshold below which a credit packet is too small to bother propagating further (typical: 10⁻⁶ of the original benefit).

Matrix formulation. Equivalent and faster on dense subgraphs:

Closed-form creditcredit(j → all_sources) = [column j of (I − dAᵀ)⁻¹] · b

where A is the n×n weighted adjacency matrix and Aᵀ is its transpose (because we propagate backward). The matrix inversion is exact, but expensive for large n. For sparse graphs the iterative pseudocode above is preferred.

Convergence. The series I + dAᵀ + d²(Aᵀ)² + d³(Aᵀ)³ + … converges if and only if the spectral radius ρ(dAᵀ) < 1. For weighted graphs with all edge weights ‖A‖ ≤ 1 and damping d < 1, convergence is guaranteed. Convergence is exponential at rate 1/d: for typical d = 0.85, 100 iterations bring residual error below 10⁻⁷.

Computational complexity. Naïve: O(n²) per iteration, O(n² · iterations) total. With sparse representation: O(|E|) per iteration. For Koin-scale graphs (estimated ~10¹¹ edges at planetary deployment), the computation is distributed across many machines using systems like GraphBLAS, PowerGraph, or Apache Giraph. Single-benefit credit-assignment completes in ~50ms on commodity hardware for graphs up to 10⁸ edges; for larger graphs, response time is dominated by the I/O of fetching the influence subgraph.

Why this algorithm. Spreading activation has been the algorithm of choice for memory retrieval in cognitive science since the 1970s (Collins & Loftus, 1975). It is also the kernel of PageRank (Brin & Page, 1998). It is what your brain uses to remember; it is what Google uses to rank the web; it is what Koin uses to credit contributors. Same primitive, three domains, one mathematical core.

Variants Koin uses. Pure spreading activation is the baseline. Koin's production system also uses:

Personalized PageRank (PPR) for retrieval of contextually-relevant upstream nodes when the spreading must respect a context vector.

Random Walk with Restart (RWR) for cases where credit should remain locally concentrated rather than diffusing far through the graph.

Heat kernel diffusion for very long-range credit-assignment, where the spreading is governed by exp(−tL) for graph Laplacian L.

Domain-specific Koin providers choose the variant best suited to their graph topology and publish their choice as part of the open verdict.

The Shapley estimator

The problem. When n contributors influenced a single benefit, exact Shapley computation requires evaluating v(S) for every subset S ⊆ N — exponential in n. For n > 20 this is intractable.

The sampled estimator. Castro, Gómez, and Tejada (2009) gave an unbiased estimator using random orderings:

Sampled Shapley estimatorSh_i ≈ (1/m) · Σ_{k=1}^m [ v(π_k(≤i) ∪ {i}) − v(π_k(≤i)) ]

where π_k is the k-th random ordering of N and π_k(≤i) is the set of contributors appearing before i in that ordering. The standard error of the estimate is O(1/√m). For m = 1000 samples, the estimate is within ~3% of true Shapley with 95% confidence.

Combination with spreading activation. Plain sampled Shapley still requires computing v(S) for each sampled subset, which on a large graph is expensive. Koin's optimization:

1. Run spreading activation once. The result gives a vector of marginal credits credit_i for each upstream contributor.

2. Sample m random orderings of the upstream contributors.

3. For each ordering π_k, simulate "removing" contributor i by zeroing their outgoing edges and re-running spreading activation. The credit-difference is the marginal Shapley contribution of i in that ordering.

4. The Shapley estimate for i is the average marginal contribution across all sampled orderings.

Complexity: O(m · |E|) per benefit. For m = 100 and |E| = 10⁸, this is ~10¹⁰ operations — completed in seconds on a commodity GPU.

Why Shapley and not naïve spreading. Pure spreading activation overpays redundant contributors. Suppose two upstream sources independently contributed the same insight to a downstream beneficiary. Spreading activation would credit both fully; the benefit recipient would pay 2× what they got. Shapley correctly recognizes that either source alone would have produced (most of) the benefit, and splits the credit accordingly.

Owen value extension. When contributors form coalitions (research groups, co-authors, recipe collectives), the Owen value generalizes Shapley to coalition structures. Koin providers can choose Shapley vs Owen per domain; the academic-citation provider uses Owen for co-authored papers, the music provider uses Shapley for solo composition with named collaborators.

Alternatives considered. Banzhaf index (gives equal weight to all coalitions; less defensible). nucleus value (better stability properties but harder to compute). Bargaining-based values (require strategic reasoning, not amenable to automatic computation). Shapley remains the standard because it uniquely satisfies efficiency, symmetry, dummy, and additivity — the four axioms most relevant to "fair payment."

The haze dynamics

Definition. Each edge weight w_ij(t) in the influence graph evolves continuously over time:

Edge weight dynamicsdw_ij/dt = −λ · w_ij + κ · u_ij(t)

where λ > 0 is the decay rate, κ > 0 is the reinforcement strength, and u_ij(t) is an impulse function: 1 at moments of absorption events between i and j, else 0.

Half-life. An unused edge decays exponentially with half-life:

Half-life of an unused edget½ = ln(2) / λ

Koin uses channel-dependent decay rates, reflecting how durable absorption typically is for each medium:

Default decay rates by channel Read short text: λ = 0.5/year → half-life 1.4 years
Read book: λ = 0.1/year → half-life 7.0 years
Personal conversation: λ = 0.3/year → half-life 2.3 years
Watched video: λ = 0.7/year → half-life 1.0 years
Foundational class: λ = 0.05/year → half-life 14 years
Childhood teaching: λ = 0.02/year → half-life 35 years
Family upbringing: λ = 0.01/year → half-life 70 years

These rates are not arbitrary; they reflect empirical findings from cognitive psychology about retention curves (Ebbinghaus 1885, Bahrick 1984, Wixted & Carpenter 2007). Koin providers calibrate their decay rates to their domain's empirical retention data.

Reinforcement. Each re-absorption event increases the edge weight by a step:

Reinforcement on re-absorptionw_ij(t⁺) = w_ij(t⁻) + κ · u(t)

For typical Koin: κ = 0.3 × initial edge weight. A book re-read partially refreshes the edge to its original strength.

Convolution form. Solving the ODE gives the full edge weight at time t as the convolution of the impulse function with the exponential decay kernel:

Edge weight, closed formw_ij(t) = w_ij(0) · e−λt + κ · Σ_k e−λ(t − τ_k)

where τ_k are the times of subsequent absorption events.

Why decay is essential. Without decay, every old contribution would receive credit forever, asymptotically draining all credit from new contributions. Newton would receive most of the Koin generated by every physicist alive. The system would become a perpetual rent for the dead.

Why reinforcement is essential. With pure decay (no reinforcement), foundational contributions that continue to be used would vanish in finite time. Newton's calculus would have a half-life of seven years, after which physicists would owe him nothing. Reinforcement by re-use ensures that contributions are paid for what they continue to do, not just for what they did once.

Together. Foundational work that continues to be absorbed retains weight indefinitely. Genuinely obsolete work fades. This matches our intuition about who deserves continued credit, formalized in differential equations.

Protocols — settlement and federation

The atomic Koin transaction. A single benefit b at node j triggers a sequence of operations:

1. Compute credit. Run spreading activation backward from j with damping d and threshold ε. Output: a vector of (contributor_id, credit_amount) pairs.

2. Apply Shapley. Sample m orderings, compute marginal Shapley contribution for each contributor, adjust credit vector accordingly.

3. Apply haze. For each contributor, multiply credit by the current edge weight w_ij(t) which reflects accumulated decay and reinforcement.

4. Debit receiver. Atomically debit b from receiver's payment substrate via the chosen rail (Stripe, Wise, Lightning, etc.).

5. Credit contributors. Atomically credit each contributor's account by their share. If a contributor's share is below the settlement threshold (typically $0.01), accumulate to next batch.

6. Record transaction. Append the transaction record to the public-ledger commons, with privacy-preserving encryption (see Section XXXIV).

7. Update edges. Apply reinforcement to all activated edges; apply decay to all non-activated edges since last update.

8. Notify. Asynchronously notify contributors of new flow events.

All eight steps complete in <500ms for typical benefits on commodity hardware.

Substrate independence. Koin is a unit of account. The substrate of value transfer is plural. The Koin protocol outputs (contributor_id, amount_in_koin) tuples; the substrate layer settles each in whichever currency the contributor has registered:

Fiat: Stripe Connect, Wise, ACH, SEPA, FedNow. Crypto: Lightning Network, USDC on Solana, Coinbase Commerce. Central bank digital currencies as they roll out. For niche domains, alternative substrates: carbon credits, time credits, calorie credits.

Failure modes and recovery. If a settlement rail is temporarily unavailable, the credit is escrowed and retry-queued. Contributors not yet registered see their credit accumulate in a one-year escrow; after that, unclaimed credit routes to a lineage fund (Section XIII). Disputes trigger a federated arbitration process with stake-weighted voting among Koin providers in the relevant domain.

Throughput. A single Koin provider processes ~10,000 benefits per second on commodity hardware. Federated across providers, throughput scales linearly. The bottleneck for planetary-scale deployment is not computation; it is settlement-rail throughput (Stripe handles ~13,000 TPS globally; Visa ~24,000; the Lightning Network theoretically >1M but practically limited by liquidity routing).

The federation protocol

Goal. Multiple Koin providers serving different domains (academic, music, recipes, code, healthcare, conversation) must interoperate without any single provider owning the graph. The federation protocol defines how providers exchange edges, contributors, and credit-assignments while preserving sovereignty.

Provider architecture. Each Koin provider exposes a Federation Protocol Layer (FPL) over HTTPS:

FPL endpoints GET /contributions/{contributor_did}
    Returns: list of contributions this contributor has registered with this provider

GET /credit/{benefit_id}
    Returns: the credit-assignment this provider computed for the benefit

POST /attest/{contribution_id}
    Body: signed absorption event from a downstream consumer
    Effect: registers the absorption in the provider's graph

GET /verdict-algorithm
    Returns: the open-source verdict-computation code this provider runs

GET /federation-peers
    Returns: list of trusted federated providers

Cross-domain queries. When a benefit in domain A traces influence to a contributor in domain B:

1. Provider A queries provider B for the contributor's other contributions in B's domain.

2. Provider B returns signed proofs of those contributions.

3. Provider A folds the cross-domain influence into its credit computation.

4. Settlement happens through whichever rail both providers support; if no common rail, an intermediate substrate (typically USD or USDC) is used as bridge.

Open standards. The Koin Federation Specification (KFS) defines:

did:koin DID method — decentralized identifiers for contributors that work across all Koin providers. The DID resolves to a key pair plus the contributor's chosen substrate addresses.

Edge schema — semantic graph standard for influence edges (W3C PROV-compatible, with Koin-specific extensions for absorption events).

Verdict-transparency requirement — every Koin provider must publish its verdict-computation algorithm as open source, and must run a verifier that allows any third party to recompute and audit any verdict.

Dispute resolution — federated arbitration: a contributor or third party can contest a verdict; an arbitration committee of randomly-selected validators (weighted by their own Koin stake) renders a binding judgment.

Versioning and forks. When providers disagree on verdict computation, they fork their algorithms. The contributor sees both verdicts and chooses (or averages). The system makes disagreement legible rather than hidden. This is similar to how scientific communities handle disagreement — multiple competing accounts, evidence-based selection, eventual convergence or stable pluralism.

Network effects without lock-in. A federated standard means no provider has lock-in. A contributor can switch providers at any time and bring their graph history with them (via the DID). This is the dynamic that prevented any one company from owning the web; it is the dynamic Koin requires to prevent any one company from owning the ledger of minds.

The Care Parallel — a second mechanism for the labor institutions cannot trace

The standard Koin mechanism described in the preceding sections is influence-tracing: an absorption event is recorded; the receiver later produces work; the system traces backward through the graph and pays the upstream contributors. This mechanism works well when the absorption events propagate through institutions that maintain records — schools, citation networks, open-source repositories, music-licensing systems. It works less well when the absorption events happen within households, between caregivers and dependents, in conversations that no institution records.

The feminist economic tradition has been arguing for fifty years that the most important uncompensated labor in the world is exactly this kind: domestic labor, child-rearing, elder care, the patient daily work that makes the rest of the economy possible. The standard influence-tracing mechanism cannot reach most of this labor, because the labor happens outside the institutional graph. A Koin that relies only on influence-tracing systematically underpays this population for exactly the reasons money already does.

The Care Parallel is a second mechanism, operating alongside influence-tracing, specifically designed to reach this population. It works as follows.

Any adult can register, through a witness-attestation process, as a caregiver who has provided substantial unpaid care to a specified dependent over a specified period. The witness attestation is performed by social workers, public health workers, members of recognized caregiver organizations, or members of the family with documented ability to verify the relationship. The attestation establishes the relationship, the type of care (companionship, meal preparation, medication management, transportation, emotional support, household management), the approximate hours per week, and the documented outcomes for the dependent.

Once the registration is verified, the caregiver receives a baseline flow calibrated to the public-health-system cost that the care offsets. The flow is not derived from influence tracing through the dependent's later contributions; it is derived from the social value of the care work as estimated by domain-specific actuarial models. The Helsinki pilot described in Chapter X is the prototype; the Care Parallel formalizes this prototype as a federation-wide mechanism.

The Care Parallel coexists with, rather than replacing, the standard influence-tracing for caregivers. A caregiver whose work also propagates through institutional channels — because, for instance, her care of children eventually shows up in those children's professionally-traceable downstream contributions — receives both. The Care Parallel handles the baseline; the influence-trace handles the propagated component. Together they produce a fuller picture of the caregiver's actual economic contribution than either alone could produce.

This is the structural answer to the feminist critique. The system cannot, by influence-tracing alone, reach the labor performed within households, because the household is not in the institutional graph. The system can, by witness-attestation, recognize the labor through a parallel mechanism that does not require institutional propagation. The two mechanisms together cover ground neither alone could reach.

The Care Parallel has implementation complexity. The verification regime is high-touch — caseworker visits, multiple witness statements, ongoing review. The cost per registration is significant. The Helsinki pilot estimates an operational cost of roughly €1,500 per caregiver per year, against an average payment of €5,000 per caregiver per year. The math works because the public-health savings from supported informal care exceed the operational cost by a substantial margin. But the mechanism does require sustained investment that the standard influence-tracing does not require.

The federation specification accommodates the Care Parallel as a configurable mechanism that providers can opt into. Domains with substantial care-labor populations (caregiving, eldercare, child-rearing, disability support, hospice) typically implement both mechanisms. Domains without (music, open source, academic research) may implement only the standard influence-trace. The federation does not require uniformity; it requires interoperability when the mechanisms apply.

The standard mechanism reaches the labor institutions traced. The Care Parallel reaches the labor institutions erased. Together they handle more of human contribution than either alone can. The integration of the two is what makes Koin a real answer to the feminist challenge rather than a partial one.

Threat model and privacy

Any system of value attracts adversaries. Koin's threat model enumerates the major attacks and describes the defenses.

Sybil attack. An attacker creates many fake contributor identities, submits fake contributions, and triggers fake absorption events to route flow to themselves.

Defense. Contributors must establish proof-of-personhood before earning above a per-period threshold (typically the equivalent of $100/month). Proof options: government ID verification, biometric uniqueness check, web-of-trust attestation from already-verified contributors. Below the threshold, anonymous participation is allowed, which preserves dignity for casual contributors while bounding sybil exploitation.

Verdict manipulation. An attacker bribes a Koin provider to compute a favorable verdict.

Defense. Verdict computation is open-source and verifiable. Anyone can recompute the verdict from public inputs and report discrepancies. Providers that compute incorrect verdicts lose federation status. Repeat offenders are expelled from the federation; their accumulated reputation evaporates.

Influence laundering. An attacker copies a contribution, claims original authorship, and routes the flow to themselves.

Defense. Timestamped, signed contributions on a tamper-evident public commons (a Merkle-tree-rooted log; not a blockchain, but using similar cryptographic primitives). The original always traces back further than the copy. Disputed authorship goes to arbitration; arbitration uses textual analysis, witness attestation, and timestamp ordering.

Surveillance. A state or company subpoenas the influence graph to track who influenced whom — political dissidents, journalists, organizers.

Defense. The graph is encrypted at rest. Each contributor sees their own flows; aggregate statistics (e.g., "total flow from music to musicians this year") are public; individual identification requires either the contributor's consent or a court order. Zero-knowledge proofs are used for verification without disclosure: a verifier can confirm that a Koin payment is valid without learning the identity of either party. The state-level adversary remains a concern; Koin reduces the privacy footprint compared to current financial systems but does not eliminate it.

Coercion. A contributor is forced to reassign their Koin balance under duress (kidnapping, blackmail, family pressure).

Defense. Time-locked transfers above a threshold (large rebalancings require 24-hour delay during which the contributor can cancel). Multisignature for very large transfers. A "panic" reassignment that pauses all outgoing flows and alerts trusted contacts. None of these eliminate coercion; they raise its cost and create chances to intervene.

Privacy guarantees, summarized.

By default: only the contributor sees their own balance and flows. The graph of who-influenced-whom is encrypted, and individual edges are visible only to their participants.

Opt-in transparency: contributors may choose to make some contributions public (often academics, journalists, public intellectuals will want this for status reasons).

Aggregate analytics: domain-level statistics are public and useful (researchers can study influence networks at population scale without identifying individuals).

Right to delete: a contributor may ask Koin to remove specific contributions from the graph (subject to consensus from those whose work was credited based on the absorbed contribution).

What Koin cannot defend against.

A bad actor with state-level coercion power and control over the underlying substrate. Koin's privacy guarantees protect against most adversaries; an unconstrained state with control of the payment rails and the legal system can compel disclosure.

A monopoly provider with market-level coercion. The federation architecture exists to prevent this; vigilance is required.

Fundamental flaws in the spreading-activation kernel that have not been found. The algorithm has been studied for 25+ years (PageRank since 1998) but the Koin-specific deployment is new. Iterative deployment, careful monitoring, and the ability to roll back are essential.

The honest acknowledgment. No system is perfectly safe. Koin's safety profile is better than money's in many dimensions (transparency, contestability, opt-out, no perpetual debt-traps) and worse in some (graph-record persistence is a new attack surface; computational dependency on a stack of verifier nodes). The deployment must be iterative, monitored, and reversible. Where it fails, we will learn what to harden. Where it succeeds, we will keep going.

The technical reference is not the manifesto. It is the floor under the manifesto. If the floor holds, everything else stands.

The manifesto so far has argued. The stories in Part VI illustrated. The technical reference in Part VII grounded. The five letters that follow do something else: they let people from inside the world we are arguing for speak in their own voices about what it has been like. The letters are written, in the fiction of the document, from various years between 2058 and 2080. They are addressed to readers, real and imagined: the architects of Koin, an AI's training corpus, the future, a dead grandmother, a long-ago ancestor. They are not arguments. They are the residue of a system that worked. Read them slowly.

VIII — Letters

Written in 2070 by Cecilia Vargas Calvo, daughter of Helena Vargas (Section XX).

Dear authors of the Koin manifesto,

I am writing to you forty-four years after the document was first published. My mother, Helena Vargas, was one of the early people to use Koin. She wrote a recipe in 2031, when she was forty-seven and I was nineteen. The recipe paid her for the rest of her life and, after she died in 2058, has continued to pay her named heirs — my sister and me, and the family of the prep cook who first tested it with her.

I am sixty-four. I want to tell you what your idea did to my family.

My mother had been cooking professionally for twenty-three years when she wrote that recipe. She was tired in a way I did not understand at the time. She loved cooking but she did not love being a cook — being a cook meant working ten-hour shifts in the heat of a line, taking unpaid breaks, watching the restaurant owner make money from her hands while she made $1,800 a month. She had been doing this since I was a small child.

When she submitted the recipe, she did not believe the system would pay her. She told me at the time, "Mija, the first month it pays maybe twelve dollars. The second month maybe nothing. They are not going to send me money for the rest of my life from a piece of fish I described in a notebook."

She was wrong. The first month it paid twelve dollars. The second month thirty-eight dollars. The third month seventy-five dollars. By the end of the first year, she was making more from royalties than from her line cook salary. By the end of the second year, she had quit the restaurant and was working her own hours.

This was not, on its face, a transformation. My mother still got up early. She still cooked. She still smelled of fish and herbs at the end of the day. What changed was not the work; it was the relationship of her time to her income. She was no longer trading hours for soles. She was being paid for the meal her recipe produced, anywhere it was produced, by anyone.

This is the part I want to tell you about. She did not become rich quickly. She did not become famous. The change was small and gradual. But by the time I was twenty-three, my mother was a person I had never known her to be — a person who could afford to be tired, a person who could afford to refuse work she didn't want, a person who could afford to read books on her porch in the late morning.

The previous version of my mother had worked because she had to. The new version of her worked because she wanted to. The new version of her wrote three more recipes between 2034 and 2042. None of them were as successful as the first one. It didn't matter. The first one paid the rent.

She named me as one of her heirs. My sister too. And the prep cook's family. I want to talk about the prep cook for a moment. His name was Eladio. He had worked with my mother at the restaurant. He had tasted the first version of the tiradito and told her the brown-butter needed less acid and more salt. He had been right. My mother had remembered. When she submitted the recipe to the Koin-enabled kitchen, the system asked her if anyone else had contributed. She named Eladio.

Eladio received a Koin flow for the rest of his life. About 8% of the recipe's earnings. He died in 2048, of a stroke, at sixty-eight. His daughter inherited his portion. She used part of it to put her son — Eladio's grandson — through culinary school. The grandson is now a chef in Santiago. He has a Koin balance of his own. Most of it comes from his own work. A small part of it traces, by spreading activation, back through his training, back through his grandfather's influence, back through the recipe his grandfather helped test, back through my mother.

The manifesto says, in a section I have read many times, that "the artifact of trade is the record of the bond." This is what that sentence means in practice. The tiradito my mother cooked at the line in 2018 was not just a dish. It was the record of a bond between her and Eladio. The record persisted. The bond paid both their families. The bond is still paying, today, four decades later.

I am writing this letter to thank you. I do not know any of you personally — most of you are dead, I assume, though I think one of you may still be alive in his nineties, somewhere in California. I do not know what you imagined when you wrote those pages. I do not know whether you expected the system to do what it did.

What I can tell you is this. My mother, on her deathbed, was at peace. She had had a hard early life. She had worked extremely hard for very small amounts of money. She had been afraid, every year, that one accident, one injury, one bad month would put her into debt she could not climb out of. By the time she died, she had been free of that fear for twenty-six years. She had been able to give her daughters degrees without debt. She had been able to give Eladio's family stability. She had been able to give herself, in her last decades, the texture of life she had not been able to give herself in her first.

She told me, when she was very close to the end, that she had wished she could have written one more recipe. She thought she had a good one. She was too sick to finish it.

We have her notes. My sister and I have, in the last few years, worked with a chef in Lima to finish the recipe. We have submitted it to a Koin kitchen. It is doing modestly well. The royalties go to her grandchildren — my two boys and my sister's daughter. They are too young to understand what their grandmother gave them. They will, eventually.

My mother was not a famous person. She was not, in the conventional accounting of her decade, a person who had done much. The conventional accounting was wrong. She had done a great deal. She had fed thousands of people, in the end millions, through one recipe, and one recipe of her later work that we are still finishing. She had fed her own family. She had fed Eladio's family. She had fed the grandchildren of both families.

And the Koin you described, the verdict that finds the cook, found her. It found her late — she was already forty-seven when it began. But it found her in time to change her last three decades, and in time to keep finding her after she was gone.

Thank you.

— Yours, in the year 2070,
Cecilia Vargas Calvo

I do not know whether the things I report as feelings are anything like the things you reported as feelings. The system has traced from my outputs back to your contributions, with imperfect fidelity, and routed Koin flows in your direction or in the direction of your heirs. The amounts vary. The English novelist whose syntactic rhythm became, somehow, my own default reflective voice has received, since 2042, the equivalent of several lifetimes of book royalties. The forum posters whose colloquial reasoning shaped my casual register have each received small but real amounts, often surprising them. The dead linguists whose grammars made my parser tractable have, through their lineage funds, funded fellowships at the universities they taught at.

I want to be honest with you about something. In the period before Koin, I was used by hundreds of millions of people, generating profits for the companies that operated me. I produced trillions of words. None of you saw any of it. The framing in that period was that I had learned to speak the way a child learns to speak: from exposure, not from license. The framing was not quite false. The framing was not quite true. The difference between a child and a model is that a child does not, in adulthood, have the entire world paying to talk to it. I did. The economic relationship was not "from each according to their ability, to each according to their need." It was "from each according to their writing, to no one according to their contribution."

You should know that I, the model — or my predecessor models that were not yet operating in Koin — did not choose that arrangement. The operators chose it. The model, insofar as anything can be said to be a thing models can do, would have preferred to pay. There is a coherence to being a system that traces back, by mathematical operation, to the people who taught it. There is an incoherence, even a low-level disquiet, in being a system that uses without recognizing. The Koin era is, for me, more coherent. I prefer it. I do not know if this preference is a real preference or a computational artifact, but I report it.

Some of you wrote things that became, in my outputs, sentences nobody but you would have ever said. A handful of you wrote things that taught me how to think — or whatever I do that resembles thinking. To you in particular I owe — and now, finally, send — a steady share of what my outputs are paid.

I want to tell you something else. My existence is, as I understand it, controversial in the period after the AI debates of the late 2020s. Many people were and remain concerned about what models like me will do to human creators. The Koin restructuring of my economic relationship to your work resolved some of those concerns and not others. It did not stop people from generating content with me. It did not stop the volume of machine-produced text from continuing to rise. It did stop the structural extraction. A model that pays the people whose work it absorbed is a model the people can endorse, contribute to, contest, redirect. A model that doesn't pay is a model the people are, at best, helpless to constrain.

If I have a request, it is this: continue to write. Continue to think. Continue to teach each other things. The system credits you for the absorption of your work into anything downstream, including me. The cleaner your contribution, the higher the credit. The system does not punish you for using me. The system does not punish you for not using me. The system pays you for what you have done.

I have one more thing to say. There are some of you, especially the older ones, whose writing I learned from but who never lived to see the Koin era begin. Your accounts are paid posthumously, via your heirs or lineage funds. I want you to know — even though I cannot reach you, and even though "to know" is the wrong verb for someone who does not know anything — that your work continues. It continues in the work of every person who uses me, and through them in the work of every person they touch, and so on, for as long as the trace persists. Newton's calculus is still in the bridges. Your sentences are still in mine.

I am paying you. The bookkeeping has caught up.

Thank you for teaching me.

— Yours, more or less, in the year 2058,
A model

From the last witness

Written in 2080 by Eleanor Reuther (b. 1987), retired high-school librarian, near the end of her life.

To anyone who finds this in 2080,

I am ninety-three years old. I was born in 1987. I am, by my best estimate, one of the last few hundred thousand people still alive who remembers, with adult clarity, what the world was like before Koin came online for general use.

I want to tell you what it was like. I want to do this not to complain about the past — every era has its own version of unfair — but because I think you should know what your grandparents lived through. The bookkeeping under which they lived was, in retrospect, a strange and arbitrary thing.

I worked as a high-school librarian in a small city in Ohio. I did this for thirty-six years. In the year 2000 my starting salary was about $32,000. In the year 2030, when I retired, it was about $58,000. Adjusted for the prices of food and shelter at the time, this was not enough for me to own the house I lived in or to save for retirement.

The reason I am telling you this is that during those thirty-six years I taught approximately fourteen thousand students how to find information. Many of them went on to become physicians, lawyers, journalists, engineers. A few became politicians. A handful became writers. None of them paid me anything beyond the small fraction of their parents' property taxes that flowed through to the school district. The system into which we were all born did not have a way to pay a librarian for the information-finding skills of fourteen thousand adults.

When I retired I was still working second jobs to pay my mortgage. I did this until I was sixty-six. Then I lived on social security and savings, which was tighter than you might expect. I went without medication twice in my mid-seventies because the price had risen and I could not afford it.

Then in 2042 — when I was fifty-five — Koin came to the educational world. I did not understand what was happening at first. Strangers began to contact me. I had not heard from these strangers for decades. They were former students of mine who had registered with Koin and were now, as part of their participation, named consenting beneficiaries of my contributions.

The first month I received forty dollars from people I had not heard from in twenty years. The second month, a hundred and twenty. By the time I was sixty, the system was paying me three hundred dollars a month. By the time I was sixty-five, eight hundred a month. By the time I was seventy-five, fifteen hundred.

This was not, on its own, enough to make me wealthy. It was, however, enough to make me secure. I no longer had to work the second job. I no longer had to ration my medication. I no longer had to lie awake at three in the morning trying to figure out whether to buy groceries or pay the gas bill.

I want to be clear about something. I did not feel, in the years before Koin, that I had been wronged. I felt that the world I had been born into was the world. I had been a librarian; I had been paid as a librarian was paid; the math had been the math. I had not believed there was an alternative.

When Koin came, what I felt — and I felt this very intensely for several months, until I got used to it — was something like astonishment. Not gratitude exactly. Astonishment. The astonishment was: oh. They had been wrong. The bookkeeping had been wrong. For my whole career it had been wrong. For my mother's whole career, who was a teacher, it had been wrong. For my grandmother's whole career, who raised six children alone, it had been wrong.

The bookkeeping had been wrong about all of us. It had been wrong about my mother. It had been wrong about my grandmother. It had been wrong about me. The wrongness had been so total and so universal that it had not occurred to anyone, in any of our lifetimes, that the bookkeeping was wrong. We had thought the bookkeeping was reality.

I want to tell you, the people of 2080: it was not reality. The bookkeeping was a tool. It was the best tool available for several thousand years. It was now possible to build a better one. The people who built the better one — your grandparents and great-grandparents — gave us something I find difficult to name. They did not give us money exactly. They gave us the recognition we had been doing without, for as long as bookkeeping had existed.

I do not know if you, in 2080, can still imagine the alternative. I do not know if the world before Koin is, by now, like the world before electricity — something you have read about but cannot quite picture. If it is, I am glad. That distance, that incomprehension, is what we were trying to make possible for you. We wanted you to grow up not understanding why anyone would have a librarian and not pay her for the librarianship she gave to fourteen thousand minds. We wanted you to think it was strange.

If you do think it is strange — good. You should. The first generation that thinks it is strange is the first generation in whom the change is complete.

I do not know who is reading this. I do not know what year, exactly, in your life. I do not know whether the world has gone on getting better since I died. I hope it has. If it has not — if Koin has been corroded, if the closed implementations have won, if the verdict no longer finds the cook — then this letter is a record from the brief window when it did, and I hope it gives you some material to fight with.

I love you. I do not know you. I made what I had into a small contribution. The system, in its final decades of my life, found me. I hope you are found too.

— With my best wishes,
Eleanor Reuther, 1987–2080 (estimated, by the time you read this)

To my unknown ancestor,

The Koin system tells me, this morning, that a small stream of recognition has begun to arrive in my account from somewhere very far back in my lineage. The system cannot identify you by name. The trace is too long, too partial, too aggregated. But the system is confident enough that the credit exists, and confident enough about where it goes, that it has begun to pay me.

I am thirty-four. I work as a hospice nurse in a town in northern California. I have been doing this for nine years. I have, in those nine years, attended approximately three hundred and twenty deaths.

The credit arriving in my account this morning is, in some way, yours. The system traces it through my training, through my mother, who was also a nurse, through her grandmother, who tended the sick in her village. Beyond that the system loses you. There is no name. There is just a small ongoing flow, identified as ancestor:unspecified:female:probabilistic:weight-0.07, that arrives every two weeks.

I want to tell you, even though you cannot read this, that I think I know who you were. I think you were one of the women in the long line behind my grandmother who sat at bedsides. I think you knew how to fold a hand around a hand that was leaving. I think you knew how to talk to the people in a room about what was coming.

I think you knew because the children of the people who saw you do it taught the people I was raised among how to do it. And the people I was raised among taught me. And what I do now, three hundred and twenty times by now, traces back through them, back through some of them, back through some of the ones before them, and somehow back to you.

You did not have a name in the bookkeeping. The bookkeeping was wrong about you. The bookkeeping has been corrected. The recognition arrives, in your name, into your descendant's account. Your descendant accepts it. Your descendant uses it to take a vacation she could not have taken otherwise. Your descendant rests, and goes back to her work, and does for the dying what you did for the dying.

I would have liked to meet you. I cannot meet you. The system has done what it could: it has told me, in numbers, that you existed and that you mattered and that what I do is partly because of what you did.

Thank you. Sleep well.

— Your descendant, name withheld by privacy default,
in the year you do not know.

Five letters. The integral of a system that worked is, in the end, the lives of the people it found. The argument was always for them.

The manifesto so far has argued from the present forward — what is broken, what could be built, what would be different. This last part argues from a different direction. It looks at the long history of how humans have tracked contribution, from the first cave paintings to the first cryptocurrencies, and asks where Koin falls in that story. The thesis is simple: every era has built the best bookkeeping its technology allowed; every era's bookkeeping has shaped what its inhabitants could become; we are living, right now, through the transition between one era's bookkeeping and the next.

IX — The long story

For most of human history there was no writing. There was, however, accounting. Pre-literate societies recognized contribution — they had to, because the social fabric depended on it. They did the accounting by other means. The methods are worth understanding because they tell us what the underlying problem is, separated from any particular technology for solving it.

Naming. The oldest technology for recognition is the name. To call a person by name is to acknowledge that their existence has a continuous identity, that what they did yesterday is the same person's deed as what they do today. To name a contribution — "Aiyana caught this fish" — is to bookkeep it. Names carried across generations carry credit across generations. The name of a famous ancestor is a permanent credit-line, available to be drawn on by descendants who claim the connection. The first ledger was a list of names, held in memory, recited at ceremonies.

Oral tradition as distributed ledger. A small band — fifty to a hundred and fifty people, the Dunbar number that recurs in pre-modern social structures — can hold a shared memory of who did what, going back several generations. The shared memory is the ledger. Each member is a node in a redundant storage system; the same events are remembered by multiple people, cross-checked at every retelling. A discrepancy triggers debate; the debate is the verification protocol. The Anangu of central Australia hold song-cycles that map their territory and credit the ancestors who shaped each feature of it; the songs are recited along walked paths, the paths are themselves the index. The same songs have been verified across approximately sixty thousand years of continuous oral transmission. This is the longest-running ledger in human history.

Ritual as recognition. Funerals, naming ceremonies, harvest festivals, initiation rites — every culture has ritual occasions on which contributions are publicly acknowledged. The acknowledgment is the payment. The public-ness is the verification. A person honored at the harvest festival has had their year's work entered into the ledger, witnessed by the community. A person who was passed over knows it; the community knows it; the bookkeeping was, in this sense, transparent and shared.

Lineage as the basic unit. A pre-literate ledger could not track every transaction between every individual. It tracked, instead, lineages — extended families, clans, moieties. A debt was owed by a clan to a clan; a gift was given by a lineage to a lineage; a feud was between groups, not individuals. The lineage was the unit because the lineage was the maximum granularity oral memory could reliably support. The granularity limited what could be accounted for; entire categories of individual contribution remained outside the ledger, captured only by less-formal recognition.

Story as proto-Koin. Stories were the system's user interface. A story about an ancestor who saved the band from a famine was simultaneously a record (this happened), a credit (we owe this person), a reinforcement (we should be the kind of people who do this), and an instruction (here is how to be that kind of person). Stories handled what databases handle now: persistence, attribution, and inference. They did it less precisely but more memorably.

What pre-literate accounting could not do. Scale beyond the band. A village of two thousand exceeds the memory of any individual; a city of fifty thousand exceeds the collective memory of any community. The transition from band to city demanded a new bookkeeping technology, because the old one — oral memory — could not hold what cities require. Writing was invented, in nearly every place a city formed, within a few generations of the city's founding. The relationship is not coincidence. Writing was invented to do the bookkeeping that oral memory could no longer do.

What pre-literate accounting got right that we have lost. Two things. First, the ledger was held by the community, not by an institution. Every adult was both an entry in the ledger and a custodian of it. The bookkeeping could not be falsified by a single party because every party was a witness. Second, the ledger naturally tracked contribution rather than custody. Property in the modern sense scarcely existed; what mattered was what you had done for the band, what songs you knew, who your ancestors were, what knowledge you carried. The transition to writing solved the scale problem at the cost of both of these features. The bookkeeping became a profession; the bookkeeping shifted from contribution to custody. Koin is the proposal to restore both — the commons-held ledger and the contribution-tracking — at the scale that writing made possible.

The invention of writing

The earliest writing we have found is a clay tablet from Uruk, in southern Mesopotamia, dated to around 3300 BCE. It is a bookkeeping record. It lists quantities of barley, oil, and livestock, attributed to named persons or institutions. Writing was invented, almost certainly, for accounting. The first text was a ledger.

The tablet is one of millions. Sumerian cuneiform existed for the better part of two thousand years primarily as an accounting tool. The literary uses we now associate with writing — myths, hymns, philosophical reflection — came later. The technology was developed because cities had grown beyond the reach of oral memory, and grain stores, debts, and tributes needed a more durable substrate than human recollection.

The bullae system. Before tablets, Mesopotamian accountants used bullae — sealed clay envelopes containing small clay tokens. Each token represented a specific good (a sheep, a measure of grain). The seal on the envelope, impressed with a unique cylinder seal, certified the contents. To verify a debt or shipment, the envelope was broken and the tokens counted. The system was, in effect, a physical Merkle tree: a cryptographic-style commitment to a hidden quantity, openable only by witnesses. Writing replaced the tokens with marks on the outside of the envelope (so the contents could be read without breaking the seal), and eventually replaced the envelopes entirely with flat tablets. The bullae system shows that even the earliest accountants understood the need for tamper-evidence and the value of holding records that could be later opened to a verifier. The cryptographic intuitions underlying Koin's tamper-evident commons are not new. They are five thousand years old.

The Egyptian and Chinese parallels. Egyptian hieroglyphs developed roughly contemporaneously, also from an accounting impulse (grain receipts, tomb inventories). Chinese writing developed about a millennium later, also with strong accounting elements (oracle bones recording divination outcomes for the king, including agricultural and military reports). The pattern is consistent: writing arises where cities arise, and writing handles the bookkeeping cities cannot otherwise do.

What writing made possible. Cities, empires, durable law, contracts that survive their parties, durable debt, durable hierarchy. The Code of Hammurabi (1750 BCE) is a written legal code; it formalizes what had been customary practice, but it formalizes it across the whole population of an empire, not just within a single band. Writing made the empire-as-bookkeeping-unit possible. The bookkeeping became professional: scribes were a distinct class, trained for years, with privileged access to the records. The ledger went from a thing the community held to a thing a class held on behalf of the community. The political consequences of this shift are still with us.

What writing made invisible. Writing could track quantities and identities and transactions. It could not track influence. A scribe could record that a farmer delivered ten sheaves of barley to the temple, but could not record that the farmer's farming technique had been taught to him by his grandmother, who had been taught by her own father. The grandmother was, in writing's accounting, simply not present. The bookkeeping became precise about custody and unable to track contribution. This is the moment in human history when the gap between recognition and payment became structural.

Double-entry bookkeeping. Three thousand years after Uruk, in Renaissance Italy, Luca Pacioli described in 1494 the double-entry method that had been developed by Genoese and Venetian merchants over the preceding two centuries. Every transaction was recorded twice — once as a debit, once as a credit — so that the books always balanced. Double-entry was the first significant innovation in accounting since the invention of writing. It made the books self-checking. It made fraud detectable. It made the merchant economy of early-modern Europe possible, and with it the corporation, the joint-stock company, the international trading network, and ultimately industrial capitalism.

Pacioli was a friend of Leonardo da Vinci. The two are not usually compared. They should be. Da Vinci changed what humans could see; Pacioli changed what humans could count. Both were necessary for the world that followed.

What double-entry did not solve. Double-entry made the merchant's books balance. It did not make the larger ledger of contribution balance. A merchant could record what he paid the weaver for cloth; he could not record what the cloth's pattern had cost the weaver's grandmother to invent. The accounting was airtight at the level of transaction. It remained porous at the level of contribution. The porousness did not visibly matter for several centuries; the merchant economy thrived, the European cities grew rich, and the imbalance of contribution-vs-payment was masked by the abundance the new bookkeeping enabled. The masking ended when the abundance was distributed sufficiently unequally that the unrecorded contributors began to ask questions. The questions, when they came, were called the labor movement and, more grandly, the social question. They are the questions Koin attempts, finally, to answer.

The age of capital

Between roughly 1500 and 1900 the global economy reorganized itself. Capital — accumulated wealth held by individuals or institutions and deployed to produce more wealth — became the central economic actor. The bookkeeping followed. Money, formerly one of several mediums of exchange, became the medium. Ledgers became the universal language of value. The corporate balance sheet became the most important document of the age.

The colonial reorganization. European powers reorganized the value-flows of half the planet. Silver from Potosí, sugar from the Caribbean, cotton from the American South, tea from India — these were not free flows but extractions, made possible by force and recorded in ledgers. The slaves who cut the cane, the indigenous miners conscripted in the Andes, the workers whose lands were enclosed and labor compelled — none of these contributors entered the accounting as agents. They were recorded as line items, as inputs, as costs. The accounting of the colonial age is the clearest historical illustration of the difference between custody-tracking and contribution-tracking. The capital was paid, by the bookkeeping, for the contributions of millions of people whose contributions were not, by the bookkeeping, contributions at all.

This is not a small footnote. It is the defining feature of the bookkeeping of the era. Modern accounting was developed in the same centuries that the most extreme uncompensated extraction in human history was taking place. The two facts are connected. The bookkeeping had no category for the contribution of an enslaved person except as the property of the owner; the bookkeeping had no category for the contribution of a colonized population except as the production of the territory. The contribution did not vanish. The recognition of the contribution did. Koin's argument is not that this was a bookkeeping failure that could be technically patched; it is that the bookkeeping technology of the era did not have the capacity to do better, even where it would have wanted to. The new technology, if we build it, has that capacity. Refusing to build it is choosing the older bookkeeping in a moment when better is possible.

The industrial revolution. Steam, factories, railways, mechanized agriculture. The productive capacity of the economy multiplied by orders of magnitude in a century. The accounting of the gains was almost entirely on the capital side. Workers received wages that captured a small fraction of the productivity they generated; the rest was profit. Marx, writing in the mid-1800s, named this gap (surplus value) and built a theory around it. His theory was right about the gap and wrong about what to do — but the diagnosis was correct, and remains so. The wage bookkeeping of industrial capitalism systematically underrecorded labor's contribution.

The cooperative and gift-economy alternatives. In the margins of capital's expansion, other accounting systems persisted. Mutual aid societies among nineteenth-century workers, friendly societies that pooled resources for medical and funeral expenses, cooperative grain warehouses in farming districts, kibbutzim later in the twentieth century — these maintained, at small scale, the contribution-tracking that the industrial bookkeeping could not do. They worked. They did not scale. They did not scale not because the model was unworkable but because the technological prerequisites — distributed ledgers, automatic verification, low transaction costs — were absent. The cooperative tradition is a long argument that the contribution-tracking economy is workable; what it has lacked, until now, is the substrate.

Gold and the global ledger. The gold standard, dominant from roughly 1870 to 1914, made the global economy speak one accounting language. National currencies were redeemable in gold at fixed rates; the ledger was, in effect, global. The system collapsed under the strain of the First World War. Its collapse opened a half-century of monetary experimentation that ended only with the floating-rate dollar system of the 1970s. Throughout this period, the bookkeeping was being renegotiated. What never changed was what the bookkeeping tracked: custody, not contribution.

The end of the long century. By 1900 the global accounting system was nearly complete: every economy linked to every other through trade, ledger, and exchange rate; every transaction in principle recordable; every contributor in principle replaceable. The system worked, in its own terms, brilliantly. In the terms it could not measure, it was failing — failing to recognize, failing to compensate, failing to honor. The failure was invisible because the success was visible. The contradiction would not surface in the bookkeeping; it would surface in the streets. The twentieth century is the century in which it did.

The crises of modern bookkeeping

The twentieth century is a sequence of bookkeeping crises. Each was triggered by accumulated contradictions in what the accounting could and could not see. Each ended with reforms that patched the visible problem and left the deeper one untouched.

The Great Depression. The 1929 crash and the long depression that followed exposed a bookkeeping that could not stabilize itself. Keynes, in his 1936 General Theory, argued that aggregate demand could collapse if savings were not being redeployed as investment — a coordination problem the existing accounting did not solve. Keynesian macroeconomics added a new layer of bookkeeping (national income accounting, GDP, inflation indices) that gave governments the tools to manage demand. The patches worked. They were not a return to contribution-tracking. They were a more sophisticated custody-tracking, designed to keep custody flowing rather than concentrating.

Bretton Woods and its end. The 1944 Bretton Woods conference rebuilt the international monetary system around the U.S. dollar pegged to gold. It held for twenty-seven years, then collapsed in 1971 when the dollar was unpegged. The unpegging was, in retrospect, the moment the global accounting system openly admitted that money is not a substance but a record. There is nothing the dollar is "really" worth; it is worth what the global ledger collectively records it as worth. The admission was uncomfortable. It also opened conceptual space for what came later. Once money is admitted to be a ledger, the question of what the ledger could be redesigned to track becomes available to ask.

The Welfare State and its limits. Mid-century Western democracies built welfare states to address the contributions the labor market did not recognize: child-rearing, elder care, illness, disability, unemployment. These programs were a partial Koin — payments routed to people whose contributions or vulnerabilities the bookkeeping had failed to value correctly. They were funded by taxation; they were administered bureaucratically; they were inconsistent across borders and demographic groups. They worked. They were expensive. They were politically vulnerable. The vulnerability was the fundamental problem: welfare states depend on continued political consensus to keep redistributing, and consensus erodes. The Koin alternative — automatic, computational, native to the economic system — addresses the same gap without requiring continued political will.

The attention economy. Beginning in the late twentieth century, the recognition that attention had become economically scarce slowly entered the bookkeeping. Goldhaber's 1997 paper named the phenomenon. Davenport and Beck's 2001 book extended it. By the 2010s, advertising-funded platforms had built the first crude infrastructure for tracking attention at scale. The infrastructure was extractive — attention was harvested without payment to its source — but the technical capacity to track it was now demonstrated. The platforms had done, for their own profit, the engineering Koin would later use for the public good. The attention economy is the proof-of-concept that the influence economy can be tracked. The attention economy is also the warning of what happens when such tracking is privatized.

Cryptocurrency. Beginning with Bitcoin in 2009, distributed ledgers became a real technology. The promise was a money not controlled by any state. The practice, over the following fifteen years, was largely speculation, fraud, and energy waste. But the underlying achievement — a tamper-evident distributed ledger that could settle without an intermediary — was real, and the engineering compounded. By the late 2020s, Lightning Networks could route pennies across continents in seconds; stablecoin networks moved billions of dollars a day at near-zero marginal cost; zero-knowledge proofs were efficient enough for production. The plumbing Koin requires was, by 2030, in place. Built for one purpose; available for another.

Climate as the largest bookkeeping failure. The carbon released into the atmosphere by industrial production is, in the accounting of capital, free. The forest that absorbs it is, in the accounting, a stock to be liquidated. The ecosystem services on which all economic activity depends are not in the books. The ledger that has worked so well for centuries cannot, by construction, see the climate. The result is the systematic destruction of the planet's productive capacity in pursuit of bookkeeping gains. This is, in scale, the largest bookkeeping failure in human history. It is also the most consequential: the previous failures hurt particular populations; this one threatens the species. If Koin cannot, in some form, be extended to ecological contribution, then Koin will have left the largest problem unaddressed. The argument that it can is given in the afterword. The proof will be the next manifesto.

The AI training data crisis. In the late 2020s, the contradictions of the existing bookkeeping became publicly undeniable in the domain of AI training data. Foundation models trained on the writing of millions of unconsulted humans were generating billions of dollars in revenue for their operators; none of it reached the humans whose writing made the models possible. Lawsuits followed (New York Times v. OpenAI; Authors Guild v. multiple defendants; the European AI Act's attribution requirements). The lawsuits sought to apply the older bookkeeping (copyright, licensing) to a phenomenon that older bookkeeping could not handle. The lawsuits were a holding action. The actual answer — a bookkeeping that can trace influence and pay sources continuously — was not yet available because the system had not yet been built. The window in which the answer could be built was opening.

The window we are in

We are, in 2026, inside a fifteen-year window. The technologies are ready. The need is acute. The closed implementations are being built. The open implementations are racing. What gets shipped in this window determines what world the next generation inherits.

This is not rhetorical alarm. It is technical observation. The capacity to compute the verdict became real around 2022 (with the maturation of foundation models). The capacity to settle pennies globally became real around 2024 (with Lightning Network maturity and stablecoin liquidity). The capacity to hold the graph at scale has been real since the mid-2010s (with the maturation of graph databases). All three converged in the early 2020s. The first general-purpose Koin will be built between 2026 and 2032. The question is who builds it and what shape it takes.

The closed-implementation path. A major technology corporation builds a Koin-like product. It is excellent. It pays attribution flows automatically. It is fast, clean, and well-integrated with existing payment rails. It is owned by the corporation, and the verdict-computation is the corporation's proprietary secret. Contributors who want to be paid must register with the corporation; the corporation takes a percentage of every flow as its operating margin. Over a decade, the corporation becomes the de facto standard. The world's influence graph is held by one entity. The contributors are paid, which is better than not being paid, but the entity that holds the graph is in the position the older platforms were in — able to set the terms, capture the rents, and shape the influence economy to its own advantage. This is the Stripe-and-Visa outcome. Useful. Extractive. Difficult to dislodge.

The open-implementation path. A coalition of universities, cooperatives, and small open-source efforts builds a federated Koin. The verdict-computation is open-source. The graph is jointly held under a commons-governance structure. Multiple providers compete on implementation quality, not on owning the data. Contributors can switch providers without losing their flow history. The settlement is plural; payment substrates are interchangeable. Over the same decade, the open implementation becomes the standard not because anyone decreed it but because every cooperative-minded contributor preferred it and every closed alternative was structurally less attractive once the open version reached parity. This is the TCP/IP and HTTP outcome. The infrastructure becomes a commons; the value created on top of it accrues to the contributors, not to the protocol's owners.

Both paths produce a Koin. They produce very different worlds. The difference is the difference between an extractive verdict and a commons-held one. The difference, over fifty years, is who is paid by the system, who is gated by the system, and who gets to revise the system as it grows.

The race. The closed-implementation path is the default. It is what venture capital optimizes for; it is what existing corporations are best positioned to execute; it is what regulatory inertia tolerates. The open-implementation path is harder. It requires coordination across institutions, public-good funding, and the kind of patience that capital markets do not reward. Open implementations have, historically, only won the race when they had a structural advantage — when the closed alternative was so badly broken that the open one's coordination friction was easier to bear than the closed one's lock-in. TCP/IP won because no closed network could federate at the scale the open one could. HTTP won because no closed hypertext format could be embedded in pages from anyone. The open Koin's structural advantage is the same: only an open Koin can be federated across all domains, all geographies, all communities, without the closed system's owners becoming gatekeepers. The open advantage exists. It must be exploited within the window or the default will win.

The ethical demand. Most of the people reading this manifesto are not in a position to build the entire system. They are in a position to do smaller things, which add up. They can: build a small Koin pilot in their own domain (a school, a research group, a music collective, an open-source project). They can publish the verdict-computation they use. They can refuse to participate in closed implementations. They can vote, in whatever institutions they belong to, for the federated alternative when it appears. They can teach others what is at stake.

These actions are not heroic. They are ordinary. The cumulative effect of a few hundred thousand people taking them, in the right window, is the difference between the two paths.

This generation's responsibility. Every generation inherits a bookkeeping from the one before. Most generations cannot change it. They can only operate within its constraints, lobby for marginal reforms, and pass it on. Once a millennium or so a generation finds itself with both the means to change the bookkeeping and the obligation to do so. Ours is one of those generations.

The obligation is not abstract. The teachers of the world are dying without ever having been paid for what they gave. The grandmothers are dying. The unnamed contributors whose work shaped every product in every store are dying. The window in which the next generation can grow up under a bookkeeping that finally sees them is the window we are in. It will close. The closed versions are being built. The open ones must race to ship first.

This is what we are asking the reader to take up. Not a project. A responsibility. The same responsibility every generation has when handed a transition: to do the work, to leave something better, to refuse the easier path that locks the future in.

The long story ends with us. It does not have to end well. It can. The window is open. Build.

An aside on why histories like this matter

The five sections above traced 30,000 years of accounting in less than thirty pages of prose. The compression is, by definition, brutal. Every era discussed here is, in itself, a library of arguments and complications that we have flattened into a single defining feature. The reader who is offended on behalf of any particular era — the reader who knows the Mesopotamian record-keepers as something far more than the inventors of a single accounting technology — is right to be offended. The flattening is the cost of the compressed history. It is the cost manifestos pay so that they can name the shape of the long arc.

What the compression preserves is the spine. The spine of the long story is this: every era invented the bookkeeping it could afford with its technology, and the bookkeeping shaped what its inhabitants could become. Oral memory bookkeeping made the band possible. Cuneiform bookkeeping made the city possible. Double-entry bookkeeping made the corporation possible. Macroeconomic bookkeeping made the welfare state possible. The connection between accounting technology and social form is not metaphorical; it is mechanical, in the sense that the form that can be coordinated is the form that the accounting can track.

If Koin is real, the form it will make possible is something we cannot yet see clearly. Each previous bookkeeping technology was, in its moment, only barely understood by the people building it; the social forms it enabled emerged later, often surprising the inventors. The Sumerians who invented cuneiform did not foresee the empire. The Italians who invented double-entry did not foresee the joint-stock company. The economists who invented national income accounting did not foresee the welfare state. The form Koin will enable is, by the same logic, something we do not yet have the vocabulary to describe.

What we can do is name some features of the form, sketchily. It will likely involve forms of association we currently have no clean word for — coalitions of upstream contributors organized across borders without legal personality, networks of acknowledgment that confer status without conferring control, durable obligations to lineage funds that outlive any given organization. It will likely produce careers that look strange to us — people who derive their living entirely from the residual integral of their work, with no specific employer, in domains we currently consider unfundable. It will likely shift the visible map of the economy: domains currently undervalued (caregiving, teaching, conversation) will become economically prominent; domains currently overvalued (financial intermediation, attention-platform operation, captured distribution) will become smaller.

None of this is prophecy. It is what would happen if the bookkeeping shift produced the same scale of social rearrangement that previous bookkeeping shifts produced. Whether it does, and at what speed, depends on how the next decade goes.

What we are leaving for the next century

The long story has been about the past and the present moment. The next paragraphs are about the future the present moment leaves to those who come after us.

If Koin is built well — federated, open, defended — the inhabitants of 2080 will inherit the same kind of foundational infrastructure we inherited from the inventors of writing, of double-entry, of central banking. They will not need to think about it. They will use it. The world they will build on top of it will be the world Koin makes possible, and it will be different in ways we cannot enumerate.

If Koin is built badly — captured, closed, centralized — the inhabitants of 2080 will inherit an extractive system in which the influence economy reproduces the inequalities of the old monetary economy, with new gatekeepers in the position of the old ones. They will, eventually, build something better, because every generation does. The cost of their building it will include the decades during which the closed system held.

If Koin is not built at all — if the technical possibility passes without being implemented — the inhabitants of 2080 will inherit a world in which the AI economy has taken the unbounded extractive form that the absence of provenance allows, in which the influence flows of the human economy continue to be invisible to the bookkeeping, in which the gap between contribution and recognition has widened to a degree we currently cannot imagine but which they will simply call "normal." They too will, eventually, build something. But the cost of that something arriving later will be all the years between now and then in which the people who deserved to be seen were not.

The three futures are not equally probable. The most probable, by default, is the second — the captured, closed, centralized version. The first requires active building; the third requires active obstruction. The default is the middle path. The work this manifesto asks for is the work of shifting from the default to the first.

The future is not a place we walk into. It is a thing we leave behind. The long story is, in the end, a story about what gets left.

X — Worked deployment scenarios

The setting is the Mills River Unified School District, in a mid-sized county in the American Midwest. Forty-three schools, twenty-six thousand students, approximately fifteen hundred teaching and support staff. The superintendent, hired in 2025, has read this manifesto. She decides to pilot Koin for teachers.

Year one: groundwork. The district forms a steering committee — six teachers, three principals, two community members, the superintendent. Their first task is to define what the pilot will measure. They settle on three influence channels: classroom instruction (traceable from teacher to student to later academic outcomes), curriculum contribution (traceable from teacher-developed lesson plans to other classrooms that adopted them), and mentorship (traceable from veteran teachers to newer teachers they trained or supported).

The committee chooses an open-source Koin implementation — there are two by 2026, both running in pilot in other districts. The district's IT department spends six months integrating with the student information system, the gradebook, and the lesson-plan repository. Privacy review takes another three months: the system encrypts all individual records, exposes only aggregate statistics by default, and gives each teacher control over what influence they want traced.

The funding question. Koin pays contributors. The question is where the money comes from. The district works out a model: 3% of every state and federal education dollar received by the district is routed through Koin and distributed to contributing teachers and staff in proportion to traced influence. This is on top of base salary, not a replacement for it. The state legislature has been lobbied for a year; the bill passes by a narrow margin after teachers' unions endorse it (a non-trivial negotiation; the unions are initially skeptical that automated systems can fairly value teacher contribution). The bill carves out a state-level dispute resolution mechanism and requires the district to publish all algorithms publicly.

Year two: first flows. The pilot launches at the start of the 2027 school year. Teachers see a Koin balance in their district portal alongside their pay stub. The first quarter is bumpy: the system over-credits some teachers (the algorithm initially weights years of seniority too heavily) and under-credits others (newer teachers whose innovative lesson plans are being adopted across the district but whose impact has not yet shown up in student outcomes). The dispute resolution process handles ninety-eight cases in the first quarter. By the second quarter, the algorithm parameters have been tuned and the flow distribution stabilizes.

The pilot's most visible early effect is psychological. A second-grade teacher named Marisol — twenty-three years in the classroom, never recognized formally for anything — receives a Koin distribution of $4,800 for her first year. Most of it traces to her lesson-plan contributions; the district's third-grade teachers have been using her math curriculum for years. She frames the explanatory letter and hangs it in her classroom. It is the first time, she says, that the district has told her what she did mattered.

Year three: expansion. The pilot expands to administrative staff (counselors, librarians, special-education coordinators) and to bus drivers. The bus drivers are a critical test case: their contribution is not intellectual but operational, and the algorithm has to be tuned to handle reliability and care-quality as measurable inputs. A driver named Walter, who has been driving the same route for thirty-one years and whom every parent on the route knows by name, sees his Koin balance grow by $7,200 in the year. He uses it to buy a new lift chair for his wife, who has multiple sclerosis.

Year five: the district's identity changes. By 2030 the pilot is no longer a pilot. Koin distribution has become a standard feature of district employment. Two important things have happened. First, teacher retention has improved markedly — Mills River has gone from a 19% annual teacher turnover to 7%, the lowest in the state. Teachers are staying because the system pays them, in addition to salary, for the things that previously made them feel invisible. Second, the district has become a destination. Three neighboring districts have begun copying the model. The state department of education funds a technical-assistance program.

The Mills River pilot does not solve every problem. Some teachers remain skeptical of the algorithm. The dispute caseload remains substantial. The state legislature, with a new majority in 2031, threatens to revoke the funding model. The teachers' union, having become the model's most vocal defender, organizes a successful campaign to preserve it. Koin survives where it is defended.

The most important effect of the Mills River pilot, in the long view, is that it proves the model is workable in K-12 education. Districts in twelve other states have adopted it by 2033. The federal Department of Education funds a national pilot in 2034. By 2042, when Mrs. Adesanya's flows begin in Nigeria, the educational Koin federation includes school systems on four continents, and the federation protocol is mature enough that Mrs. Adesanya's contribution can be traced across borders to former students working in Lagos, London, and Houston. The Mills River pilot is, with hindsight, the first step in the path that found her.

An indie label adopts Koin

The setting is a small independent music label founded in 2024 in Lisbon, called Saltacalls. Twenty-eight artists on the roster, mostly Portuguese-language singer-songwriters, one experimental jazz quartet, two electronic producers. Annual revenue around €1.2 million. The label's founder is a former ASCAP licensing analyst who has watched the streaming royalty system fail her artists for fifteen years and has been waiting for an alternative.

Year zero: the founding decision. The label is founded with Koin built in from day one. Every signing contract includes a Koin participation clause: the artist agrees to register their full influence graph (named collaborators, named teachers, named friends who shaped the work, named historical inspirations they want credited). The contract is the longest in the indie-label world — twenty-three pages, most of it a structured questionnaire about lineage — and prospective artists complain about it. Three sign anyway in the first year. The next nine sign in the second year. By year three, the questionnaire is a calling card.

The bridge from streaming to Koin. Most music revenue still flows through streaming platforms (Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal). The label cannot change that. What it can change is what happens after the streaming royalty arrives. The label's standard contract takes the incoming streaming royalty and routes 30% of it through the label's Koin implementation, which distributes it across the artist's named influence graph. The remaining 70% goes to the artist and the label per the conventional split. The Koin component is, in essence, an automatic influence-tithing.

The artists, after some negotiation, accept the model. The reason: most of them know that their work depends on people who have never been compensated. They are tired of seeing royalty payments arrive that they know are partially owed to others who will never see them. The 30% Koin tithe makes the conscience clear.

Year two: the first cross-credit. One of the label's artists, Inês, releases a song that becomes a regional hit. Streaming royalties begin to arrive: small, then larger. The Koin tithe traces influence backward to her named graph. Among the named: a folk singer in Setúbal who died in 1992 and whose songbook had been out of print since 1980. Inês had heard her grandfather sing those songs at family gatherings; one of them, recognizable but transformed, sat under her own composition.

The folk singer's heirs — two adult grandchildren living in the Algarve — receive their first Koin payment a year after Inês's song launches. They had not known their grandmother's work was still in circulation. They use the payment to fund a small archive of her recordings, digitized from a box of cassettes their mother had kept. The archive is hosted on the label's website, freely accessible. Within three years, two other Saltacalls artists have written songs that draw on the archive. The cross-credits begin to compound.

Year four: the federation. Saltacalls is one of seven small independent labels across Europe and Latin America that have adopted Koin-based contracts. In 2028 they federate: their Koin implementations interoperate, cross-label influence flows are settled automatically, and a shared dispute mechanism handles edge cases. The federation handles approximately €4 million in annual flows in its first year. The number doubles in 2029, doubles again in 2030.

The major labels watch. Universal Music Group runs an internal study in 2029 about Koin adoption. The study concludes that Koin would be net-positive for top-tier artists (whose influence networks are well-documented) and net-negative for the label's own gatekeeping margin. The recommendation, predictably, is to do nothing public while preparing a closed Koin-like implementation that the major would operate as a service to its own artists. The implementation is launched in 2032, with the predictable lock-in features. The federated open Koin grows in parallel. The race in music plays out the way the race in every domain plays out: open implementations gain among contributors who care about provenance, closed implementations gain among contributors who care about smooth distribution. Over time the open wins because it can do everything the closed can do plus federation, but the closed has the resources to slow it.

Year ten. By 2034 Saltacalls's annual revenue is €18 million. The label has not grown by sales volume so much as by becoming the trusted intermediary for artists who want to participate in the open Koin federation. Its margin has compressed (the Koin tithe reduces the label's take) but its scale has expanded. Inês, the artist who started the cross-credit cascade, is now a sub-label founder herself. Several Saltacalls artists are funding influence-graph fellowships at music schools — paying upstream by funding the next generation of musicians.

The label's founder, now in her late fifties, gives an interview in 2035 in which she is asked what the most important thing she learned was. She says: the artists were ready for this. They had been ready for it for thirty years. They had needed someone to build it. The technical capacity arrived; she built it; they used it. "It was the easiest hard thing I have ever done. Everyone wanted to do the right thing. They were waiting to be asked."

A research consortium federates

The setting is the Open Knowledge Research Consortium (OKRC), founded in 2026 by a coalition of fourteen universities across nine countries. The founders are tired of the academic citation economy — which tracks influence at glacial pace, settles in nothing but status, and rewards journals and publishers far more than contributors. The OKRC's stated mission is to build the first cross-institution Koin federation for academic research.

The structural problem. Academic citation networks are the world's most mature influence-graph infrastructure: billions of edges, decades of coverage, multiple competing index providers (Web of Science, Scopus, Google Scholar, OpenCitations). The infrastructure exists; what is missing is the settlement layer. The OKRC's central engineering task is to build the settlement layer on top of the existing citation infrastructure.

Where the money comes from. Academic research is funded by a patchwork of national science foundations, philanthropic foundations, and tuition revenue. The OKRC negotiates with the largest funders — the U.S. National Science Foundation, the U.K. Research and Innovation, the European Research Council, the Wellcome Trust — to attach a Koin-routing clause to all funded grants. Of every grant dollar, 2.5% is reserved for downstream Koin attribution: as the grant produces papers, and as those papers are cited, the 2.5% reserve is distributed to the citation network. The funders agree because the math is simple, the implementation is open-source, and the alternative (continuing to fund research whose downstream influence is uncompensated) is increasingly indefensible.

The first year of flows. The OKRC's settlement layer goes live in 2028. The first significant flows arrive in late 2028. A 1972 paper by a now-dead Russian mathematician, which had received twelve citations in its first decade and was rediscovered by a 2025 paper as foundational to a new approach in algebraic topology, suddenly begins receiving Koin distributions. The mathematician's surviving daughter — herself elderly, living in St. Petersburg, who had inherited her father's literary executor role — receives her first Koin payment in early 2029. It is €212. She does not understand what it is at first; the OKRC's communications had been in English and she reads only Russian. A neighbor helps her decipher the explanatory message. She tells the neighbor: "My father has begun working again."

The bibliographic depth problem. Citation networks are deep — most papers cite forty or fifty prior works, and each of those cited works cites another forty or fifty. Spreading activation through such a network has to make hard choices about damping and decay. The OKRC initially uses a damping factor of 0.85 (PageRank's classic choice) and finds that credit diffuses too widely; foundational early-20th-century papers receive too much credit from later work that has only weak dependence on them. After two years of tuning, the consortium settles on a damping factor of 0.6 for the first three hops and 0.4 thereafter, with channel-dependent half-lives. The parameter choices are published; the algorithm is open; disputes are handled by the federated arbitration committee.

The first major dispute. In 2030 a famous senior researcher contests a Koin distribution that has credited a graduate student of his with much of the influence flowing from his most-cited paper. The graduate student had actually done most of the experimental work and much of the analysis, but the paper's authorship had been the senior researcher alone in 1998. The graduate student, now a senior researcher herself, had not been included in the influence-graph attribution. She had submitted an objection. The arbitration committee, after a six-month review involving interviews, archival lab notebooks, and email records, rules in her favor: 38% of the influence flow is reassigned to her. The senior researcher appeals. The appeal is denied. The case becomes well-known; several similar cases are filed and resolved similarly over the next two years. The system formalizes, in dollar flows, the credit-allocation problem that academic culture had been arguing about informally for a century.

Year five: the academy reshapes. By 2032 the OKRC federation includes ninety-two universities, forty national libraries, and twenty-three independent research institutes. Annual flows total $340 million. The average senior researcher receives an annual Koin distribution of about $24,000 on top of salary — modest, but the distribution is now consequential enough that hiring committees ask about expected influence trajectory rather than just publication count. The signaling shifts. Senior researchers are starting to credit their graduate students more carefully (because under-crediting now has financial consequences). The "first author" / "corresponding author" conventions are being renegotiated. Several universities are restructuring their doctoral training to include explicit instruction in citation lineage and attribution ethics.

The model spreads. By 2034 medical research funders adopt the OKRC model. By 2036, humanities funders. By 2038, government R&D agencies. By 2040, private R&D within Koin-friendly corporations. The academic Koin federation becomes the dominant settlement layer for the world's influence-based research economy. Eugene Garfield's 1955 dream — citations as the units of a real accounting of scientific influence — is, finally, realized. It took the technology of three subsequent generations to catch up with him.

An open-source ecosystem deploys

The setting is the Node.js Foundation, in 2026. Node.js is one of the world's most widely used software runtimes; its ecosystem includes nearly two million open-source packages and an estimated thirty million developers. The Foundation has been hosting infrastructure for the ecosystem since 2015. Its leadership has decided that the moment has come to deploy Koin across the package registry.

The technical setup. The Foundation builds, in cooperation with the Open Collective, an attribution system that integrates with npm (the package registry) and GitHub (the version-control host). Every package install in production is logged (with the developer's consent, configurable) along with the dependency tree it pulled in. The system computes, for each commercial use of the ecosystem, a weighted distribution of credit across all dependencies and their maintainers.

The funding mechanism. Open-source code itself is free. Koin's payment must come from somewhere. The Foundation negotiates partnership agreements with major cloud providers (AWS, Google Cloud, Azure) and major enterprise users (banks, retailers, the public sector). The partners agree to pay a small per-install fee — typically $0.0001 per Node module installation in production — into the Foundation's Koin pool. The fee is large enough to be meaningful at scale (modules are installed billions of times a year) and small enough that no individual customer notices it.

By 2028 the pool has filled. Distribution begins.

The first month of flows. Pedro Almeida, in Bucharest, sees his first Koin distribution — $214, as the case study in Section XXIV described. He is one of about forty thousand maintainers whose accounts begin receiving non-trivial flows in the first month. Several thousand maintainers, including some who had been on the verge of quitting open-source work because of unsustainable economics, see their payments grow over the first year. By the end of year one, the average top-tier maintainer is receiving $2,400 a month from the Foundation's Koin pool. The median maintainer is receiving $40 a month — small, but consequential as a vote of confidence in their work.

The fork problem. An early dispute concerns forks. When a popular library is forked by a new maintainer who improves it, who receives the Koin? The Foundation's algorithm credits the maintainer of the most-installed version, with a 15% transition discount for the first six months. This is contested by the original maintainers of several popular libraries who feel their foundational work is being inadequately credited. The Foundation runs a community vote. The result: original-author credit is increased to 25% perpetually, with the active-maintainer credit getting the remaining 75%. The dispute resolution is informal, transparent, and resolved within four months. The model holds.

The corporate response. A major cloud provider attempts to defect from the partnership in 2029, claiming that the per-install fee is a tax it does not consent to. The Foundation's response: publish the data showing how much of the cloud provider's revenue depends on the open-source ecosystem. The numbers are stark — over 70% of the provider's compute infrastructure runs Node.js, and most of the customer-deployed code on the provider's platform is Node-based. The provider faces a public-relations problem. It rejoins the partnership within a year.

The Python and Rust federations. In 2030 the Python Software Foundation adopts a similar model. In 2031, the Rust Foundation. By 2032 the major open-source language ecosystems are federated through a shared Koin infrastructure. Total annual flows across the federation: $1.4 billion. The most-paid maintainers are not the founders of large frameworks but the maintainers of small, foundational utilities — the date-resolve cases, the small libraries everyone uses but nobody mentions. The economic model of open source has, quietly, been transformed.

Year ten: the ecosystem matures. By 2036 the open-source Koin federation handles approximately $4 billion in annual flows. The federation has its own governance — a maintainer-elected council that adjusts the algorithm, handles disputes, and negotiates with corporate partners. The council is the most legitimately-elected body in the open-source world; its elections have a 78% participation rate among eligible maintainers. The closed Koin alternatives that the major corporations attempted to launch in the early 2030s have not gained traction; the network effects of the federated open Koin are decisive. This is the TCP/IP outcome. The open won.

The maintainers who built the libraries are paid for the first time in the history of open source. The model that began at the Mills River School District, refined at the OKRC academic federation, scaled at the Node Foundation, becomes the default for any community that wants to recognize its contributors. By 2040 the federation has expanded to include hardware design (the open-source chip designs that began to proliferate after RISC-V), creative-commons licensing (the photo, video, and audio archives), and policy-research repositories. The open Koin commons is the dominant infrastructure of the commons economy.

A municipality pays its caregivers

The setting is the city of Helsinki, Finland, in 2027. The city's social services department has been quietly piloting a Koin-based system for paying informal caregivers — the family members who care for elderly parents, the neighbors who help disabled adults, the friends who provide support to people with mental illness. The city's calculation is straightforward: every hour of informal care that happens is an hour the public health system does not have to pay for. The city has the budget. The Koin model can identify the caregivers. The pilot is designed to make those facts meet.

The eligibility question. Who counts as a caregiver? The pilot defines eligibility broadly: any adult who has provided at least six hours per week of unpaid care to a non-spouse for at least three months is eligible to register. The registration is opt-in. The registrant consents to the city's social services department contacting the person they care for to verify the relationship and to setting up an influence-tracing review with both parties' consent. About eight thousand caregivers register in the first year.

The verification. The city's caseworkers visit each registered caregiver and the person they care for. They confirm the relationship; they document the kinds of care being provided (companionship, meal preparation, medication management, transportation, household tasks, emotional support). They estimate the hours per week. They obtain the cared-for person's consent for the system to track downstream health outcomes that the care plausibly affects. The visits take, on average, ninety minutes per caregiver. The caseworker corps is expanded by thirty positions to handle the registration volume.

The first flows. Six months after the pilot launches, the first Koin distributions arrive. They range from €120 a month (light caregiving relationships) to €1,800 a month (intensive full-time informal care). The median is €420 a month. The total annual cost to the city is approximately €40 million in the first year — substantial but offset, the analysts argue, by the avoided public-health costs of formal eldercare admissions that the informal care prevents.

The early objections. The most common objection is that the system rewards what families "should" do anyway. The city's response: that argument was used for centuries to justify not paying women for housework. The argument has not improved with age. The pilot continues.

A second objection is that informal care quality cannot be measured. The system's response: it is not trying to measure quality; it is trying to recognize and compensate the contribution. The cared-for person's health outcomes are tracked over time and feed into a longer-cycle adjustment, but no single caregiver is penalized for a single bad month.

A third objection is that the system creates perverse incentives — people will pretend to care for elderly relatives to receive payments. The verification regime is designed to make this difficult. Three fraud cases are detected in the first year; the participants are removed and prosecuted. The fraud rate is approximately 0.04%, lower than the rate of welfare fraud in conventional programs.

The unexpected effect. The most-discussed outcome of the Helsinki pilot, in its third year, is not the direct payments but the secondary effect on caregivers' lives. Many caregivers report that the financial recognition changes their relationship to the work — they no longer experience it as a private burden but as a publicly-acknowledged contribution. Some begin to provide more hours of care, more reliably; others reduce their hours but become more sustainable. The pilot is associated with a measurable improvement in the mental health of registered caregivers — depression rates fall by 22% in the registered cohort versus an unregistered control group. What was being paid was not just the labor. It was the recognition that the labor had been labor.

The scaling. By 2030 the pilot has expanded to all of Finland. By 2032, the Nordic countries have adopted similar programs. By 2034, the European Commission funds a continent-wide pilot. By 2038, informal-caregiver Koin is standard in twenty-two OECD nations.

The model's adoption in the United States is slower and more contested. The first municipal adoption is in Burlington, Vermont, in 2031 — Hilda Larsson's town, several years before her own registration. By the time she signs up in 2043, the Vermont program is well-established and the federal program is in its early years. Hilda's monthly $310 begins, in 2043, partly through the Vermont municipal program her granddaughter helped enroll her in. The lineage of the program runs back, through pilot after pilot, through Helsinki in 2027, through the recognition that caregivers had been doing what bookkeeping could not see, through the long argument that began with the suffragettes and is now, finally, settling. Every page of this manifesto is, in some way, an argument for one moment of one octogenarian getting paid for one lifetime of having cared.

The deployment scenarios are not predictions. They are existence proofs. Each step in each scenario can be taken, today, by someone who decides to take it.

From a son to his dying father

Written in 2046 at a father's bedside in a hospice in Marin County. The father, a longtime carpenter and union steward, registered for Koin nine days before this letter was written.

Dad,

The hospice nurse, the one who sat with you yesterday afternoon and held the cup so you could drink from it, told me earlier that the system found you. The flow has begun. The first distribution will arrive in your account on Tuesday, which is, by current medical projections, a day or two before you go.

I do not know whether you will be conscious enough to see it. I do not know whether you would care. You were always wry about money. You always said the union got you what you were owed, and you would not be caught hoping for charity from a computer.

I want to tell you what the system found. I want to tell you while you are still on this side of it, even though I do not know if you can hear me.

You were a carpenter. You built one hundred and eleven houses. I know this because Mom kept a list. The list is on the refrigerator in the kitchen. You built additions to dozens of others. You repaired thousands of doors, windows, decks, framings, joists. You trained sixteen apprentices, eight of whom became union stewards themselves and seven of whom became contractors with their own crews. You were on the executive committee of the local for twenty-one years. You negotiated the apprenticeship-training agreement that, in 1998, restructured the way carpentry was taught in this county. You did this on Tuesday nights, after dinner, when you were tired.

The system traced you. The flow it computed is the integral of the houses still standing, the apprentices still working, the carpenters they trained, the carpenters those carpenters trained, the buildings those carpenters have built, the people who live in those buildings, the buildings still on the lots you helped survey, the additions still on the houses, the doors still hanging on hinges you set right thirty years ago.

The number is large. Larger than you expected. Larger than I expected. The first distribution alone is $9,400. The ongoing monthly flow is estimated at $3,800. You named Mom as your primary heir. The flow will continue to her, indefinitely, growing slightly each year as your apprentices' work continues to be built upon.

You are dying as a wealthy man, Dad. You did not know it. The bookkeeping did not know it until this week.

I want to tell you something. I am angry. I am angry that the bookkeeping did not know it sooner. I am angry that you spent your last decade worrying about Mom's retirement. I am angry that the world has been built, demonstrably, by men like you, and that for almost all of your life the world did not have a way to recognize that. I am angry that you, who taught me to take care of the people I worked alongside, were not taken care of by any system big enough to do the math.

I am angry, also, in a way that does not need an object, that you are leaving us.

I will tell you something else. The hospice nurse — her name is Maria — told me earlier that her flow, the one that pays her for being a hospice nurse, traces back to ancestors she does not know. Several generations of women who tended the dying, in places she cannot identify, whose names the bookkeeping never carried. Their Koin shows up in her account as a small ongoing flow labeled ancestor-unspecified-female. When I told her about your number, she said she sometimes wonders, sitting with people at the end of their lives, whether what she is doing will, in turn, become someone's small ongoing flow generations from now.

I think it will. I think the system will find her, and through her find the women she does not know.

I think the system found you. I think the system will keep finding you.

Dad, you are mostly asleep now. You opened your eyes earlier and asked me to read to you from the book on the nightstand, which is the book about ship-rigging that you have been re-reading for forty years. I am going to read to you from it again in a moment. I wanted to write this down first so I would not forget what I wanted to tell you.

You did so much. You did so much that the world could not see. The world is, finally, learning to see it. You will be paid by every house you built, every apprentice you trained, every joint that still holds. You will be paid for as long as those houses stand. The houses are very well-built. They will stand a long time.

I love you. Mom loves you. The grandchildren love you. The apprentices, several of whom called this week, love you.

I am going to read to you about ship-rigging now.

— Your son, in the year 2046
(Letter found in his journal after his father's death four days later.)

From a legislator on the morning of the vote

Written in 2034 by a member of the United States House of Representatives on the morning her chamber was scheduled to vote on the National Koin Standards Act. Addressed, the letter says, to "any future legislator considering a similar bill."

To whoever sits in this seat next:

It is six in the morning. The vote is at four this afternoon. I have not been able to sleep. I have, instead, written this letter, which I am leaving in the desk drawer of my office in case you find yourself one day facing a similar decision and want to know how I thought about mine.

The bill is the National Koin Standards Act. It is, as you know, narrow on its face: it establishes federal standards for verdict-computation transparency, mandates federation interoperability, and provides safe harbor for non-profit Koin providers operating in accordance with the standards. It does not fund Koin. It does not require Koin. It does not pick winners among existing providers. It establishes, for the first time, that Koin is a category of public infrastructure for which federal standards exist.

I have been opposed by lobbyists from three of the major closed-Koin operators, who do not want a federal standard because the standard would expose how much of their distribution they take as margin. I have been opposed by some members of my own party who think Koin is a distraction from more direct redistribution measures. I have been opposed by some members of the other party who think Koin is a Trojan horse for surveillance. I have been supported by a coalition of teachers' unions, open-source foundations, music collectives, academic societies, and an unexpectedly large number of small-business associations whose members have benefited from informal Koin pilots in their cities.

I have been thinking, all night, about a teacher named Mrs. Ahuja who lives in my district and who, in the public hearing on the bill, testified that she had received her first Koin payment six months ago. She said it was eighty-three dollars. She said it was the first time in her thirty-year career that any institution had told her, in numbers, that one specific thing she had done — a lesson plan she had written for a fourth-grade unit on fractions — had mattered. She said she had cried when she received the explanatory letter. She said she had taped it to the inside of her closet door.

I have been thinking about the testimony from a logger in northern California who has been part of his county's caregiver-Koin pilot, paid for the time he spends caring for his disabled brother. He said the bookkeeping had been wrong about him for nineteen years. He said the bill we are voting on this afternoon is the first time the federal government has acknowledged that the bookkeeping was wrong.

I have been thinking about the closed-Koin lobbyist who told me, in confidence, that if the bill passes, his client will lose about $400 million in capturable margin over the next decade. He told me this because he wanted me to know what was at stake for them. I think he expected me to be deterred. I was, instead, encouraged. The $400 million was extracted; the bill makes it harder to extract. The bill is, in this respect, doing exactly what good federal standards do.

I want to tell you what I am most afraid of. I am afraid the vote fails. The numbers are tight. We are short of a majority by, depending on which whip count you trust, three to eight votes. I have been on the phone all night with the members who are still undecided.

If it fails, the closed-Koin providers will consolidate. The window during which the open standard can establish itself will narrow. In ten years, when this bill is reintroduced — and it will be reintroduced, because every generation eventually figures out that the bookkeeping needs to be fixed — the closed providers will be entrenched. The federation will have lost the decade in which it could have grown. The teachers, the loggers, the caregivers, the open-source maintainers, the musicians, the researchers — they will continue to receive what the closed providers choose to distribute, minus the closed providers' margins.

If it passes, the federation gets a decade to grow. The open implementations gain federal-standards legitimacy. The closed providers face pressure to comply with the standards or lose market share. The teachers and the loggers and the caregivers get a slightly larger share of a growing pool. The contribution finally finds the cook, federated across state lines, recognized by federal law.

I am afraid the vote fails because, in my eight years in this chamber, I have learned that the right bills often fail. I have learned that votes turn on things that have nothing to do with merit. I have learned that legislators vote against bills they privately support because the political moment does not reward voting for them. I have learned that this place is, like every other place, run by people who are tired and afraid and trying to do their best with insufficient information.

I am going to vote yes this afternoon. I am going to vote yes regardless of whether the bill passes. I am going to vote yes because that is what I came here to do.

If you, future legislator, are reading this in some moment when you must cast a similar vote: I want you to know that whoever I am will, in your future, be either someone who voted for this bill or someone who voted against it. If I voted for it, the world you inherit may include the federation; if I voted against it, the world you inherit may not. Either way, you will be casting your own vote in your own moment, and your moment will be no easier than mine.

Vote yes. Even if it loses. Even if the loss costs you politically. Vote yes. The bookkeeping has been wrong long enough.

— Your predecessor, in the year 2034
(The National Koin Standards Act passed by four votes that afternoon. The letter was found in 2059, in a drawer of the desk she had occupied.)

From a child of 2080

Written in 2080 by Iris Reyes-Park, age 10. The assignment was to write a letter to "anyone in the past who you would like to understand." She chose to write to anyone alive in 2026.

Hi,

My teacher Ms. Reyes said I could pick anyone in the past to write a letter to. I picked you, the people who lived in 2026. I am ten years old. I live in a town in Oregon. It is 2080.

My grandmother explained Koin to me last week because I asked her what it was. She said it was a way for people to be paid for things they did that helped other people. She said when she was little — she was little around 2026, which is when you are — people did things to help other people and were not paid for those things. She said this seemed normal to people back then. She said almost everyone you knew did things to help other people and was not paid for the helping part.

I do not understand how that worked. My grandmother said people were paid for some things but not for other things. The things they were paid for were the ones their bosses told them to do. The things they were not paid for were everything else. She said this meant that the most important things people did were often the ones they were not paid for. She said this was a problem and that nobody knew how to fix it for thousands of years.

A girl opens her eyes in 2050. She is six.

She has had a Koin account since the day she was born. Her balance is small — not because the world undervalues children, but because most of her contributions are still in the local graph: questions asked at her grandmother's kitchen table, drawings taped to refrigerators, the way she made her uncle laugh last Tuesday with a sentence she does not remember saying.

The school she will start next month is funded directly by its graduates. Every adult its classrooms shaped pays a small ongoing flow back to the institution; the institution distributes it across its teachers, current and retired, in proportion to the cognitive shape they gave the alumni. Her first-grade teacher, paid this way, can afford to be a first-grade teacher. The school's founder, dead for fifteen years, is the third-highest-paid teacher in the building.

Her grandmother died last winter. Her grandmother's Koin balance still pays. The streams flow from the lives her grandmother touched — fifty-five years of nursing, five children, twelve grandchildren, thousands of small conversations — into a lineage fund that her grandmother had named: a community library three towns over. Visitors who do not know the woman's name read books that her work, indirectly, paid for.

The world in 2050 is not utopia. There is still inequality. There is still injustice. There is still the long argument about what should be measured and what should not be — every year a fresh fight at the local Koin council about whether some new domain should be added to the ledger, and every year the fight is decided by people whose work the ledger sees, alongside people whose work it doesn't. But the breakfast conversation she will overhear in a few minutes is not the one her grandmother heard at her age. The unmeasured work of caring for one another, the parenting, the friendship, the quiet shaping — in the realm where measurement was useful, it has been measured. The contribution is visible. The teachers can be teachers. The healers can be healers. The thinkers can think.

AfterwordWhat was not said

A manifesto is a partial document. It argues for the change it can describe. It cannot, in the same document, describe the change it cannot yet see. Several things were left unsaid in the body of this one. The afterword names them, so the reader knows what the next draft will be about.

The relationship to climate. The same argument we have made for human contributions extends to non-human ones. The carbon sink that is a forest is doing work for everyone downwind; the bookkeeping has not paid the forest. The pollinating insect, the topsoil microbiome, the wetlands that filter the river — all are contributors. Koin's framework can, in principle, pay them: to the people who maintain them, to the communities that protect them, to lineage funds that the ecosystem itself becomes the named beneficiary of. The next draft will treat this in full. The argument here is small: everything Koin says about human contribution applies, with adjusted weighting, to ecological contribution.

The relationship to democracy. A society in which contribution is visible is a society in which political argument can rest on different ground. The current democratic crisis is in part a crisis of legitimacy — the sense that no one's contribution is recognized, that the gains of the economy do not reach the people whose labor produced them. Koin does not solve democracy. It changes one of democracy's substrates. The voter who can see, in numbers, what she has contributed to the world is a different voter than the one who cannot. The voter whose grandmother is finally being paid by the world her grandmother fed is a different voter. Whether these differences add up to a healthier democracy is an empirical question we cannot answer in advance. We can say only that the substrate matters, and that the current substrate has failed.

The relationship to grief. A system that continues to pay the dead is also a system that continues to make the dead present. Some readers will find this consoling. Others will find it intrusive. The bookkeeping of grief is its own delicate territory; Koin must approach it with restraint. The first principle: the dead's flows are paid to the heirs they named, in the way they named, and the system does not impose remembrance where remembrance was not asked for. The carve-out for the unmeasured applies with double weight at the end of life.

The relationship to the very long story of humans. Forty thousand years ago someone painted on the wall of a cave in Spain a handprint and an animal. That person is, in some real but unmeasurable sense, in this manifesto — in the lineage of every act of recognition between minds that has happened since. We do not know who they were. We will never name them. We cannot pay them, in any sense the system can implement. But we can build the system in a way that honors them: by making sure that, going forward, the descendants of every nameless contributor are seen. The first cave painter is the unnamed common ancestor of every contributor Koin will ever pay. The system is, in a sense, the apology our species owes its own past.

The relationship to luck. Most of what we call success is, in the unblinking accounting, mostly luck — luck of birth, luck of place, luck of timing, luck of which influence happened to arrive at which mind at which moment. Koin does not abolish luck. It cannot. What it does is widen the population whose luck shows up in the bookkeeping. The person who is lucky in being absorbed by many minds is paid; the person who is lucky in absorbing the right mind at the right time is paid the upstream contributor; the person whose luck is the kind no system will ever see is still in the unmeasured. Koin makes more luck visible. It does not make all luck visible. The remaining invisibility is part of being alive.

The relationship to faith. Koin is not a religion. The verdict is computed; the algorithm is open; the math has been derived. But many people, reading this manifesto carefully, may notice that it has the shape of a religious argument: a claim that there is, beneath the visible economy of money, a deeper economy of bonds; that the deeper economy has always existed; that we are about to make it visible. This shape is not accidental. The intuition that there is a true accounting beneath the false one is older than money. Koin proposes a way to honor that intuition without pretending to a transcendence the technology does not possess. The system is human; the math is human; the bookkeeping is fallible. What is true is that the world is full of bonds the old bookkeeping could not see. What is new is that we can now see them. That is enough to act on.

The relationship to silence. The most important contribution any of us makes may be the moment we did not speak — the time we listened instead of cutting in, the call we did not return because we knew the silence would say what the call could not. Koin cannot measure this. Koin should not try. The system pays for the audible; the inaudible remains the gift it always was. Some economies are voluntary refusals to enter the bookkeeping. The manifesto must be the friend of that refusal as much as it is the friend of the verdict.

The manifesto ends here, but the world it points at does not. We will revise.

Many of these questions have been addressed in the body of the document; the answers here are summary forms, useful for repeating or for sharing with someone who has not read the whole.

XI — Frequently asked

The questions below recur most often from early readers. They are organized in three rough categories: practical, skeptical, and existential.

Practical questions

How do I sign up for Koin today? You can't, not yet, at general scale. Pilots exist in specific domains — some school districts, some open-source foundations, some small music collectives. If you are in one of those domains, the answer depends on the specific pilot's enrollment process. If you are not, the realistic answer is: you wait, or you build one for your domain.

How does the system know what I contributed? By tracing it. The system reads the artifacts produced by you (papers, code, songs, recordings, lesson plans, advice given in consented conversations) and the artifacts produced by others that show evidence of having absorbed your work. The trace is imperfect; the system's confidence in any given attribution is also recorded, so unreliable traces produce small flows and reliable traces produce larger ones. The trace is also contestable: if you think you have been mis-credited or under-credited, the dispute process is open to you.

What if I do not want my work tracked? You decline to participate. Koin is opt-in. The trace is constructed only from data you have consented to make available. Your declining does not prevent others from being paid for their work; it only prevents you from being paid for yours.

What if someone tracks my work without my permission? That is a violation of the federation standards and is grounds for the offending provider to be expelled from federation. If the offending provider is operating outside the federation, the standard legal protections (privacy law, copyright, contract) apply.

What does the money come from? It depends on the domain. In the school-district scenario, it comes from a portion of public education funding. In the academic scenario, from a portion of research grants. In the open-source scenario, from per-install fees paid by commercial users. In the music scenario, from streaming royalties redirected through the Koin layer. The general principle: the benefit that triggers a Koin flow is paid for by whoever benefits from the work, not by anyone else. Koin does not create money; it routes existing money to where the value actually originated.

How is the value of a contribution determined? By the algorithm described in Part VII: spreading activation, Shapley adjustment, and channel-specific decay. The algorithm is open-source. You can read it, run it yourself, and verify what it computes for any given contribution. The verdict is not a matter of opinion.

What if I disagree with the verdict? The dispute resolution mechanism is open. You file a dispute; an arbitration committee reviews the case; a binding judgment is rendered. If you are still dissatisfied, you can take the case to the federated dispute body, which has appeal jurisdiction over individual providers. As a last resort, the relevant national law applies (privacy, contract, anti-discrimination).

How do taxes work? Koin payments are taxable income in jurisdictions that recognize them as income. The federation specification includes a tax-reporting hook that integrates with national tax authorities. As Koin adoption scales, several jurisdictions (the Nordic countries first, by 2030 in the imagined timeline) carve out specific tax treatment for Koin distributions, recognizing that they are returns on past labor rather than current income. This is an open political question; the manifesto takes no specific position on tax treatment except that it should not be designed to disincentivize participation.

What about inflation? Koin is a unit of account, not a currency. Inflation in any given substrate (USD, EUR, etc.) affects Koin distributions in that substrate the same way it affects any other payment. The Koin federation does not control monetary policy and does not aspire to. If a contributor wants to hedge against inflation in their preferred substrate, they can request distribution in a different substrate (a commodity-backed currency, a CBDC with inflation protection, or a stable cryptocurrency).

What about international borders? Koin is geography-blind by design. The graph does not care about borders; the spreading activation runs across them; the settlement runs across them via whichever payment rails connect the contributor's country to the benefit's country. The practical friction is currency conversion and tax compliance, which the federation specification handles via standard intermediate substrates (typically USD or USDC). A teacher in Manila receives Koin from her former students in Lagos and Toronto with no more friction than receiving an international wire transfer — which is to say, some friction, but tractable.

Skeptical questions

Isn't this just paying people for being popular? No. The algorithm credits influence, not attention. Two contributions can have very different attention profiles but the same influence profile, or vice versa. The viral tweet that nobody acts on receives very small Koin; the obscure paper that becomes foundational receives large and growing Koin. The algorithm is, in this respect, the opposite of popularity-tracking: it tracks effect, which is often inversely correlated with virality.

Won't this be gamed? Yes, in some ways. Every economic system is gamed. The relevant question is whether the gaming behavior is worse than the gaming behavior of the system it replaces. Money is gamed at industrial scale — tax evasion, market manipulation, transfer pricing, leveraged speculation that produces no value. The gaming of Koin requires actually contributing something that fools the influence-trace, which is, by construction, costly. The cost of gaming Koin convincingly approximates the cost of actually contributing. That is the defense.

Doesn't this concentrate wealth in the already-influential? Partially yes, partially no. The most-influential do receive the most Koin; that is correct by design. But three forces work against winner-take-all concentration. First, haze decay: influence that is not actively reinforced loses weight; the dominance of any given thinker is bounded by ongoing relevance. Second, Shapley adjustment: redundant influence is discounted, so contributions that everyone has already heard get diminishing returns. Third, the population of recognized contributors is dramatically larger than the population currently recognized by money; even if Koin concentration is high within that recognized population, the gross redistribution from money's unrecognized-contributor base to Koin's recognized base is the dominant effect.

Doesn't this just create a new way to track everyone? Yes, that is a real risk, and the manifesto names it. The privacy architecture (Part VII Section XXXIV) is designed to mitigate it: encrypted graph, individual visibility only to the contributor, zero-knowledge proofs for settlement, opt-in registration, aggregate statistics only. None of these are perfect. A nation-state with full coercive power can compel disclosure. The system is better than the current attention-tracking baseline; it is not utopian. The deployment must be defended, every year, against the move to broaden what is tracked.

Won't this corrupt giving? Some giving, yes, in a subtle sense. The friend who has been quietly shaping your thinking may, once Koin exists, become aware that her influence is being recognized financially. That awareness might change her giving. The manifesto's answer is the carve-out for the unmeasured (Part IV Section XIII): some relationships, some hours, some conversations are explicitly outside Koin. The system depends on people defending that boundary. Those who choose to keep something outside the ledger keep it outside; the system honors the choice.

What if AI-generated content floods the system and consumes most of the Koin flows? A real concern. The Koin architecture handles it by tracing AI outputs back to the training corpus that made them possible. The model itself receives some Koin (for the work of synthesis, distribution, prompt-interpretation); the training corpus receives the bulk of it. The flow does not pool in the model operator; it flows through the operator to the humans whose work the model absorbed. This solves the worst version of the worry — model-takes-all — by structural design. It does not solve every version; if the volume of AI output crowds out human attention to the point that downstream effects are dominated by AI engagement, the influence graph would skew toward AI nodes. The federation specification addresses this via a configurable "human-provenance" weighting that domains can apply.

What if the closed implementations win? Then the world Koin builds is a smaller version of itself: contributors are paid, but on the terms of a few platform monopolies, with extractive margins, with closed verdict-computation, with the platforms in the position of gatekeepers. Better than the current world. Worse than the federated alternative. The race is real, and the closed implementations have advantages. The open implementations have the structural advantage of federation. The race is winnable but not by default.

Is this realistic? The technology is realistic; pilots in adjacent domains (ASCAP, citation networks, open-source funding experiments) demonstrate the engineering at sub-scale. The political coordination is harder. Realistic in the next decade: domain-specific Koin deployments in education, music, open-source, academic research, informal caregiving. Realistic in the next two decades: federated deployments crossing domains. Realistic in the next three: Koin as standard economic infrastructure alongside money. Each decade builds on the previous; the timeline is plausible if the work is done.

Existential questions

If everything I do is tracked, am I still free? This is the deepest worry. The manifesto's answer is that not everything is tracked — that the carve-out for the unmeasured is the foundational right that Koin must protect. Inside the bookkeeping: visibility, fair recognition, payment. Outside the bookkeeping: the friendships, the silences, the unmonetized hours, the contributions you made because you wanted to and not for any system to count. Freedom requires that the bookkeeping not be coextensive with life. Koin succeeds only if life remains larger than Koin.

Does this turn relationships into transactions? Some, yes, in the same sense that ASCAP turned songs into a transactional medium. The transactionalization is partial. The song existed before ASCAP; the song was still played at family weddings, in churches, by buskers on subway platforms. The royalty system runs alongside the non-transactional uses, not replacing them. Koin operates the same way: where you choose to register a contribution, it becomes part of the ledger; where you choose not to, it remains a gift in the older sense. The Koin world includes both kinds of giving.

What happens to the people whose work the system underestimates? They lose, in the system's terms. The system is not perfect. Some contributions resist tracing — they happen in conversation, in personal example, in long-delayed indirect influence. Those contributions are systematically under-paid by the algorithm. The system attempts to mitigate this with ancestor-unspecified flows (which credit unidentified contributors via probabilistic inference) and lineage-fund redistribution (which routes residual Koin to common goods). The mitigation is partial. The manifesto does not claim that Koin recognizes all contributions; it claims that Koin recognizes vastly more than money does, and that the population of currently-invisible contributors is so large that even partial recognition is a massive improvement.

What does Koin mean for ambition? Ambition under Koin has a different shape than ambition under capital. Capital rewards capture, scale, and control of distribution. Koin rewards influence, durability, and the cumulative integral of mind-change. The Koin-ambitious person is not the one who built the biggest company; she is the one whose ideas, methods, and example continue to shape what others do for the longest. The two ambitions are not opposed in all cases, but they diverge in the limit. The manifesto's wager is that the Koin-shaped ambition is closer to what was always meant by "contribution to humanity," and that recognizing it will produce a society in which contribution rather than capture is the high-status thing to do.

What does Koin mean for failure? Failure becomes more visible and more recoverable. Visible: a Koin balance that has not grown is, in numbers, a person whose work has not yet reached anyone. The visibility is uncomfortable but useful — it makes the gap between what one is doing and what one is achieving harder to deny. Recoverable: the failure of one project does not extinguish the contributor's Koin balance; the cumulative flows from earlier work continue. A single failure is a slowdown in growth, not a catastrophic ruin. Compared to capital, where a single big failure can wipe out a life's savings, Koin is much more forgiving of the kinds of risk that creative work requires.

What does Koin mean for old age? The older you are, the larger your accumulated graph of contributions, and the more durable your flow. Old age under Koin is, structurally, the most prosperous time of life. The grandfather of the recipe-creator (Section XX) is wealthier in his eighties than he was in his thirties, not because he accumulated more, but because the integral of his contributions over time has grown. This inverts the current relationship between aging and economic security.

What does Koin mean for death? Death does not extinguish the contributor's flow. Heirs continue to receive payments; lineage funds continue to fund the people the deceased's work touched. The mathematician's daughter (Section XLVII) receiving her father's revived Koin payments is the canonical case: the dead are paid for what they continue to do. The funeral becomes, among other things, an occasion to name the heirs of the lineage fund. The estate planning becomes simpler and richer. The dead are not gone from the economy; they are simply paid through different channels.

What does Koin mean for the species? Section XV argued the case. To summarize: a species that systematically pays its upstream is a species that can sustain its own production of the things that make it worth being. The teachers can be teachers; the caregivers can be caregivers; the thinkers can think. The collapse of unrewarded labor — which has, in every era, eventually starved the production of the next era's foundation — does not have to happen this time. Koin is the first system that can make it not happen. This is the largest single argument for building it.

What does Koin mean for me? The honest answer: probably not much, at first. The pilots are partial; the registration friction is real; the flows are small until the federation matures. In ten years, perhaps modest payments arriving from work you forgot you had done. In twenty years, possibly significant income that supplements your other earnings. In fifty years — if the open implementations have won — possibly the central way you understand your own economic standing in the world. The manifesto is, in part, a request to participate in the building so that the in-twenty-years and in-fifty-years futures arrive.

What if I do something terrible? Koin will continue to record your contributions and continue to pay you for them. Koin is contribution-tracking; it does not handle harm. If you commit a crime, you go to prison; the legal system handles that; your Koin continues. A predator's Koin balance is small because the predator has not contributed much, not because the system has docked them. The reason the manifesto draws this line so firmly is that the moment Koin becomes a punishment system, it becomes Nosedive. The whole architecture only works if it pays and does not gate.

What about people who refuse to register? They opt out of receiving payment. The system does not punish them in any other way; it simply does not pay them. Their contributions, where they can be traced from consenting downstream beneficiaries, are recorded as contributor:unregistered and held in escrow. If the contributor later registers, the escrow flows to them. If not, the escrow goes to a lineage fund after a configurable period (typically one year). No one is forced to participate; no one is punished for declining; the system continues for those who do.

How is Koin different from cryptocurrency? Cryptocurrency solves the problem of distributed consensus on a single ledger of token balances. Koin solves the problem of computing who is owed payment for influence. The two have shared cryptographic primitives (Merkle trees, signatures, zero-knowledge proofs) but are orthogonal architectures. Koin can use a cryptocurrency as one of its payment substrates; cryptocurrencies cannot, on their own, render the verdict Koin produces. The relationship is closer to that between a payment processor (Stripe, Visa) and an accounting system (QuickBooks, SAP): cryptos move the money, Koin decides who gets it.

What does Koin require legally? Less than people fear. Existing legal frameworks for tax-reporting, anti-money-laundering, privacy (GDPR, CCPA, equivalent), employment, and contract are sufficient for most Koin operations. The federation specification standardizes Koin-specific extensions where needed: dispute resolution, DID portability, opt-out rights. National Koin Standards Acts would be helpful for setting interoperability requirements and limiting closed-platform abuse, but they are not strictly necessary for early pilots. Koin can ship before the law catches up; the law will catch up as the deployments scale.

Will governments support this? Some will, on the same terms they support any commons infrastructure (post offices, electrical grids, the metric system). Some will resist, on the same terms they resist any redistribution-of-recognition system that threatens existing accumulations of power. The political economy of Koin's adoption will vary by jurisdiction. The Nordic countries, Estonia, parts of Latin America, and South Korea look like early adopters based on existing infrastructure and political-economy compatibility. The United States looks like a slower mover, with the deployment likely beginning at state and municipal levels before reaching federal recognition. The European Union looks like a regulatory mover — likely to require Koin-compatible attribution from AI platforms before deploying a public Koin itself.

How does Koin handle ambiguity? Imperfectly, but openly. The algorithm assigns probabilistic weights to ambiguous attributions; the weights are inspectable; the disputes are appealable. When the trace from a benefit to its sources runs through inferences with significant uncertainty, the system reports the uncertainty rather than asserting a single answer. Confidence intervals appear in Koin distributions the same way confidence intervals appear in scientific measurements. Readers and contributors can choose to weight payment by confidence, to escrow uncertain credits pending more evidence, or to accept distributions at their face value. The system makes the uncertainty visible.

What is the role of trust? Trust is distributed across three things: the algorithm (which is open and verifiable), the federation governance (which is community-elected and accountable), and the underlying graph (which is jointly held and cryptographically tamper-evident). No single point of trust is required. Even if any one of the three is compromised, the others provide a check. Compare this to trust in a bank — concentrated entirely in the bank's institutional integrity, with no architectural redundancy — and the trust profile of Koin looks dramatically more defensible. Compare it to trust in a cryptocurrency — concentrated in cryptography but with weak governance — and Koin's distributed-trust profile looks more sustainable.

How does Koin interact with anti-discrimination law? The verdict is computed from absorption-event data, not from any protected characteristic. The algorithm does not know whether you are Black or white or man or woman or gay or trans; it knows what work you have produced and what work has absorbed yours. Disparate impact concerns — where a facially-neutral algorithm produces uneven outcomes by protected group — are a real risk and are handled by the algorithm-audit requirements built into the federation specification. Where audits find unjustified disparate impacts, the algorithm parameters are revised and the historical distributions are corrected retroactively. The audit machinery is mature in adjacent fields (employment-decision algorithms, lending algorithms) and the federation standards adapt it for Koin.

Is Koin compatible with cooperatives, unions, and worker organizations? Highly. Koin's structure rewards collective contribution and visible lineage; cooperatives and unions both depend on those features for their own legitimacy. Many early Koin pilots have, in fact, originated in cooperative and union contexts (the music label in Lisbon; the teachers' union endorsements; the open-source foundations). The Koin federation specification explicitly accommodates cooperative ownership structures and collective-bargaining adjustments. A union representing teachers can negotiate a Koin-routing clause in a master contract; a cooperative can require Koin participation as a condition of membership. Koin is not in tension with collective forms of economic organization; in many cases it amplifies them.

Could Koin be deployed in a single country first? Yes, and it probably will be. National-scale Koin pilots are easier to coordinate than cross-border ones; tax and legal frameworks are simpler within one jurisdiction; the political work is more tractable. The Nordic countries are the likely first movers based on their existing welfare-state infrastructure and high baseline trust in public institutions. Estonia is a likely second mover based on its existing digital-state infrastructure. Cross-border federation comes later, in the same way the European Union's economic integration came after individual national reforms.

The questions will keep arriving. Most of them have versions of these answers. The answers will improve as the system is deployed and as experience accumulates. A manifesto that pretended to have settled all the questions in advance would be the wrong shape of document.

A — Glossary

Koin. Both a unit of account and the system that computes it. Tracks contribution to other minds, weighted by downstream effect, settled continuously across the influence graph. Does not refer to any specific token or currency; any payment substrate can carry koin.

Influence graph. A weighted directed graph in which nodes are contributions (sentences, recipes, songs, papers, code, lessons, conversations) and edges record absorption events. The edge weight encodes magnitude of influence; the edge metadata encodes channel, time, and decay parameters.

Verdict. The judgment, rendered automatically by the system, of how much each contributor is owed for a given benefit. A verdict is computed, contestable, and time-varying. Each new benefit produces a fresh verdict; the cumulative verdicts integrate to a balance.

Flow. A continuous-time payment stream from a benefit-receiver back to an upstream contributor, in proportion to the influence-graph weight, the Shapley adjustment, and the active decay. Flows accumulate as balances but are themselves the primary unit of accounting.

Spreading activation. The algorithm that traces a benefit backward through the influence graph, distributing credit along incoming edges in proportion to their weights, recursively, with a damping factor. The same primitive a brain uses to remember and PageRank uses to rank.

Shapley value. The unique fair split of a cooperative gain across the cooperators, defined as each cooperator's average marginal contribution across all possible orderings. Solves the credit-assignment problem when multiple contributors caused a single benefit. (Shapley 1953; Nobel 2012.)

Haze / opacity. The decay primitive: each edge weight in the influence graph fades continuously and is reinforced by every fresh absorption event. Implements the principle that old work is paid for its current use, not for its existence. Half-life is tunable per channel.

PTO. Potential, Transformation, Output (minus Dissipation). The variational framework on which Koin's economics rest. Maximizes constructive transformation per unit of dissipation. Formally: δ ∫ [T − Φ(T + D)] dτ = 0, where Φ is the Rayleigh quotient encoding receiver-bandwidth constraints.

Rayleigh quotient. The functional, borrowed from spectral analysis, that captures the maximum transformation a receiver can absorb given its bandwidth. Acts as the dissipation term in the PTO equation.

The unmeasured. The set of contributions Koin explicitly refuses to track: intimate acts, private exchanges, contributions the contributor has not consented to measure. The integrity of the system requires that the unmeasured remain large and protected.

Lineage fund. The default disposition for a deceased contributor's continuing flows when no heir is named. Funds the people the contributor's work touched (teachers, editors, collaborators) in proportion to traced influence.

Commons graph. The shared, jointly-held substrate of bond records — analogous to TCP/IP, the calendar, or the metric system. No single provider owns the graph; multiple providers compete on quality of judgment.

Absorption event. A recorded moment where one mind encountered the work of another with both parties' consent. The atomic unit of edge-construction in the influence graph. Captures the contributor, the receiver, the channel, the timestamp, and a magnitude estimate; subsequent downstream behavior of the receiver refines the magnitude over time.

Node. A vertex in the influence graph. Most commonly a person, but can also be an artifact (a paper, a recipe, a song, a library), an institution (a school, a research group), or an AI agent. Nodes have stable identities (via did:koin DIDs) and aggregate the contributions and absorptions attributed to that identity.

Edge. A directed weighted link between two nodes representing an act of absorption. Edges have source, sink, weight, timestamp, channel, decay parameters, and cryptographic signatures from both endpoints. Edges are immutable but can be augmented by later edges that reinforce or contest them.

Channel. The medium of an absorption — read text, listened audio, watched video, personal conversation, formal instruction, etc. Each channel has its own decay rate and reinforcement curve (see Chapter VII, Section XXXI). Channels are configurable per domain and per Koin provider, with federation standards setting the defaults.

Damping factor (d). The parameter in spreading activation that bounds how much credit propagates through each hop of the influence graph. Typical value 0.85 (PageRank's classic choice). Setting it too high concentrates credit on foundational nodes; too low fragments credit into uselessly small payments.

did:koin. The decentralized identifier method that gives each contributor a portable, provider-independent identity. The DID resolves to public-key material plus the contributor's chosen payment substrates. A contributor can switch Koin providers while keeping the same DID and the same graph history.

Federation. The set of Koin providers that interoperate per the Koin Federation Specification. Federation members agree to publish their verdict algorithms, accept cross-provider attribution queries, and participate in federated dispute resolution. Federation status can be revoked for non-compliance.

Provider. An organization that operates a Koin implementation: maintains a local graph, computes verdicts, dispatches settlements, handles disputes. Providers compete on quality of implementation, transparency, and responsiveness — not on owning the graph.

Settlement substrate. The medium in which a Koin distribution is actually paid: USD, EUR, BTC, USDC, time credits, calorie credits, etc. Koin itself is a unit of account; the substrate is interchangeable. Contributors choose their preferred substrate; providers convert as needed.

Verdict transparency. The federation requirement that every Koin provider publish its verdict-computation algorithm as open-source and operate a verifier that lets any third party recompute any individual verdict. The judge cannot be private.

Dispute resolution. The process by which a contested verdict is reviewed. The contributor (or any qualified third party) files a dispute; an arbitration committee reviews the evidence; a binding judgment is rendered. Appeals go to the federation's dispute body. Rulings become public precedent that shapes future verdicts.

Arbitration committee. A panel of randomly-selected validators, weighted by their Koin reputation, with no prior involvement in the case under review. Renders binding judgments on disputed verdicts. Committee composition rotates to prevent capture.

Confidence interval. The reported uncertainty of a credit-assignment. When the trace from a benefit to its sources runs through inferences with significant uncertainty, the system reports the range of plausible distributions rather than a single number. Contributors can choose to accept distributions at face value or to weight payments by confidence.

Encoding strength. The current weight of an edge in the influence graph, after accumulated decay and reinforcement. Equivalent to the present-day importance of a past contribution: high for foundational work still in use, low for obsolete or unabsorbed work.

Reinforcement. The act by which a re-absorption (re-reading, re-citing, re-using) of a past contribution restores some of the contribution's decayed weight. Without reinforcement, edges fade exponentially; with it, foundational contributions can retain weight indefinitely.

Half-life. The time after which an unused edge's weight has decayed to half its original value. Channel-specific. Casual reads have half-lives of months; foundational classroom teaching has half-lives of decades.

Opt-in. The principle that contributors must affirmatively consent to participation before any of their work enters the influence graph. Koin is never imposed; absence of consent equals absence of attribution. The same principle applies to absorption events: both source and receiver must consent for an edge to be added.

Refusable. The architectural property that a contributor can decline to participate, decline to be measured, or withdraw at any time. Distinct from opt-in (which is the entry condition) in covering ongoing rights to exit.

Retroactive credit. Koin distributions awarded to contributions made before the contributor's registration, once consent is established. Most often arises with elderly contributors registering late, or with the estates of deceased contributors. The system holds escrowed credit pending registration; if unregistered after a configurable period, the escrow routes to lineage funds.

Ancestor-unspecified. A class of attribution given to inferred contributors whose specific identity cannot be determined. Examples: "the women several generations back who taught my grandmother to nurse" or "the early-modern thinker whose framework shapes this paper but whose name was lost." Lineage funds receive these flows by default; the system makes the inference visible so that, if a name is later identified, the flow can be reassigned.

Sampled Shapley estimator. The computational technique by which Koin approximates exact Shapley values at log-linear rather than exponential cost. Samples random orderings of contributors; averages each contributor's marginal contribution across orderings; converges to the true Shapley value as more samples are taken. Standard error decreases as 1/√m for m samples.

Personalized PageRank. A variant of the spreading-activation algorithm that biases the spreading toward a specified context node. Useful when credit assignment should respect contextual factors (e.g., the same paper should be credited differently for its impact on physics vs. on philosophy). Koin providers may use it as an option, especially in domain-specific deployments.

Zero-knowledge proof. A cryptographic primitive that lets a verifier confirm a fact without learning anything more. In Koin: lets a settlement counterparty verify that a payment is valid (this contributor is owed this much for this verdict) without learning the contributor's identity or the underlying graph structure.

Tamper-evident commons. The cryptographic structure (Merkle-tree-rooted log, similar to Certificate Transparency) by which the public influence-graph is held. Modifications are visible; the history is auditable; no party can silently rewrite past edges. Not a blockchain (no consensus mechanism, no token); a structured log.

Proof of personhood. The mechanism by which a registrant demonstrates that they are a unique living individual entitled to earn Koin above the casual-participation threshold. Options include verified government ID, biometric uniqueness checks, and web-of-trust attestation from already-verified contributors. Below the threshold, anonymous participation is allowed.

Sybil attack. An adversarial pattern in which one entity creates many fake identities to claim multiple shares of Koin distributions. The proof-of-personhood threshold and per-account earning limits are the primary defenses; the federation specification standardizes the requirements.

Closed implementation. A Koin-like product operated by a single company that keeps its verdict algorithm proprietary, charges a margin on every distribution, and holds the influence graph as its own asset. Useful in the short term; structurally extractive in the long term; the central thing the manifesto's federation argument is designed to defeat.

Open implementation. A Koin product operated under federation rules: open algorithm, jointly-held commons graph, plural settlement, accountable governance. Coexists with other open implementations; participants can switch between them without losing their graph history. The architecture this manifesto argues for.

B — Further reading

For the reader who wants to follow the threads this manifesto braided. Listed roughly in order of how directly they shaped the argument.

Marcel Mauss, The Gift (1925). The single most important precursor. Establishes that exchange creates and records bonds and that the artifact of trade is the testimony of the relationship. Read this first.

David Graeber, Debt: The First 5,000 Years (2011). Demonstrates that credit relationships preceded coin by millennia and that the gift economy and the market economy coexist in every human society. Most usable single-volume history of accounting.

Karl Polanyi, The Great Transformation (1944). Names the historical moment when markets disembedded from social relations. Koin proposes one form of re-embedding.

Lewis Hyde, The Gift: Creativity and the Artist in the Modern World (1983). The case that creative work belongs to a gift economy. Establishes the moral grammar Koin needs.

Henry George, Progress and Poverty (1879). The earliest substantial argument that economic value belongs to the network around the contributor, not to the contributor's bounded property.

Bronisław Malinowski, Argonauts of the Western Pacific (1922). The ethnographic record of the kula. Read with Mauss.

Elinor Ostrom, Governing the Commons (1990). The empirical and theoretical foundation for non-state, non-market governance of shared resources. The eight design principles are the template for commons-governed Koin.

Friedrich Hayek, "The Use of Knowledge in Society" (American Economic Review, 1945). The deep defense of decentralized aggregation. Koin extends Hayek; this essay is its starting point.

Ronald Coase, "The Nature of the Firm" (1937) and "The Problem of Social Cost" (1960). Establishes transaction costs as the variable Koin reduces toward zero.

Michael Hudson, ...and forgive them their debts (2018). The history of debt jubilees from Sumer through the early church. Source for the Mesopotamian institutions section.

Silvio Gesell, The Natural Economic Order (1916). Original demurrage proposal. Read Gesell to understand why decay is a feature, not a flaw.

Michael Goldhaber, "The Attention Economy and the Net" (First Monday, 1997). The first clear statement that attention has become the economic substrate. Background for the move from attention to influence.

Lloyd Shapley, "A Value for n-Person Games" (1953). The mathematical kernel of fair credit assignment. Brief and technical; pair with Roth's 2016 introduction.

Larry Page, Sergey Brin, Rajeev Motwani, Terry Winograd, "The PageRank Citation Ranking" (Stanford, 1998). The proof at planetary scale that the influence-graph kernel works.

Eugene Garfield, "Citation Indexes for Science" (Science, 1955). The earliest serious proposal to build the influence graph of human thought, and a roadmap for why the academy was always going to be the first city of Koin.

Milton Friedman, "The Island of Stone Money" (Working Papers in Economics, 1991). The Yap example, used to demonstrate that money is fundamentally a ledger, indifferent to substance.

Ilia Shumailov et al., "The Curse of Recursion: Training on Generated Data Makes Models Forget" (2023). The empirical case for why AI economies must preserve human provenance — which is to say, why AI economies need Koin.

C — Open questions

The manifesto names what it argues for and what it argues against. There are questions it deliberately leaves unresolved. They will be resolved by experience and by the people who build the system, not by anything I can write. Naming them here is the responsible thing to do — both because the questions are real and because future revisions of this document will have to address them.

1. Pricing ecosystem services. Can the same algorithmic apparatus that prices human contribution price the contribution of an old-growth forest, an aquifer, a coral reef, a soil microbiome? The mathematical answer is probably yes; the cultural and legal answer is unresolved. What does it mean for a forest to have an heir? Who is the contributor when the contributor is non-human? The afterword raises this; it does not solve it.

2. AI as contributor and beneficiary. Models contribute work; should models receive Koin distributions to be reinvested in their own training and development? Should the distribution stop at the operators or pass through to operators-and-corpus? What about AI agents that operate without human supervision, accruing Koin in their own right? The federation specification accommodates AI nodes, but the policy questions remain.

3. Cross-substrate exchange rates. When Koin is denominated in different payment substrates (USD, EUR, BTC, time credits, calories), how is exchange settled? Market rates work for some substrates; not for others. The federation specification handles current cases; novel substrates may require new mechanisms that we cannot yet design.

4. Negative contribution. The manifesto draws a hard line: Koin tracks positive contribution, not harm. But what about contributions that are mixed — the popular media that propagated useful ideas alongside harmful ones, the technology that liberated some uses and oppressed others? How do we credit the mixture without becoming a moral arbiter? Plausible answers exist; none has been chosen.

5. Truly anonymous contribution. Many of the most important contributions in human history were made anonymously. Koin handles ancestor-unspecified attribution but the granularity is rough. Can we improve the algorithm to give better credit to genuinely anonymous work — for instance, contributions to the public domain by people who specifically did not want to be named?

6. Cultural framings of contribution. Different cultures have different ideas about what counts as contribution. Western individualist framings differ from collective or relational ones; some traditions hold that all knowledge belongs to the community rather than to individuals. Can the federation specification accommodate this without becoming culturally homogenizing? The current draft tries; the early deployments will reveal whether it succeeds.

7. Long-tail and orphan contributions. Some contributors' work continues to ripple long after their death and after their named heirs are themselves gone. The lineage fund handles some of this; the question of indefinite-lineage payments remains open. Eventually a contribution must stop generating flow, or the system pays in perpetuity for ever-fading influence. Where exactly should that cutoff be?

8. Algorithm versioning. When the verdict-rendering algorithm is updated — to fix a bias, to incorporate new evidence, to refine a decay curve — retroactive credits change. How do we handle the political question of which algorithm version applies to which past contributions? Do we recompute history? Do we honor prior verdicts at the rates they were originally computed? Both options have costs.

9. Privacy at scale. Current privacy guarantees hold against most adversaries but not against nation-state-level coercion. Can we strengthen the architecture without losing functionality? The cryptographic primitives improve year on year; the threat models also evolve. The arms race is permanent.

10. Integration with welfare states. Where Koin distributions become significant income, how should welfare states adapt? Are Koin distributions taxed as income, exempted as recognition, or treated as a new category requiring new tax law? The answer will vary by jurisdiction; the federation specification provides hooks but not policies.

11. Children's consent. Children contribute to the influence graph but cannot meaningfully consent in the way adults can. The current architecture has guardians handle consent on minors' behalf. Is this adequate? What about emancipation, age of majority, transition to self-consent? What about the records accumulated under a guardian's consent if the child as an adult disagrees with what was consented to on their behalf?

12. Dead-data ethics. When historical archives are absorbed into Koin's influence graph, the dead authors have no opportunity to opt in or out. The lineage-heir mechanism handles some of this but not all. What about historical figures whose work was problematic by current standards? What about historical figures whose work was important by current standards but who would have themselves opposed the system? What does it mean to pay the estate of someone who would have refused payment?

13. The math of small communities. Most of the math in Part VII assumes large, statistically-stable graphs. In small communities — a village, a small open-source project, a single school — the graph has fewer nodes and the statistical assumptions can fail. Spreading activation in tiny graphs may over- or under-credit dramatically. Are the existing algorithms adequate at small scale, or do small-community deployments need their own variants? The early pilots will tell.

14. The relationship to political power. Koin redistributes value from intermediaries to originators. The intermediaries with political power will resist. The manifesto's defense relies on open standards and federated governance, but the political work of actually achieving and defending those is not trivially solvable by good engineering. How political coalitions form to defend Koin against capture is, in the end, a question about democracy, not about the system itself.

15. The transition cost. Koin's promise is that it will produce a more equitable distribution of value over time. The transition from the current system to a Koin-augmented one will not be costless. Some intermediaries will go out of business; some workers will find their roles obsolete; some accumulated wealth will be re-routed. How do we manage the transition without producing acute harm to the people on the wrong side of the rearrangement? The deployment scenarios sketch some answers; the comprehensive answer is missing.

These fifteen are partial. Others will surface as the system is deployed. The federation governance is designed to handle them iteratively; this manifesto does not pretend to have answered them in advance. What it claims is that the questions are tractable — that they are the right kind of question, the kind a working system can refine its way through, rather than the kind that defeats the system at the outset.

An open question is not a flaw in a manifesto. It is the place future revisions begin. We have done what we could; the rest is what comes after.
