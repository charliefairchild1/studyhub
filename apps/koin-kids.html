Koin for kids · v1.0

The Story of a Better Way to Count What Matters

A guide to Koin, written for people ten years old and older. Or for anyone who wants the short, clear version.

1.The thing money doesn't see

Imagine your grandmother taught you to read when you were five years old. She sat with you every afternoon for a whole year. She showed you how letters made words. She was patient. She didn't get paid for any of it.

Now imagine you grow up. You become a doctor. You help people. You earn money for your work. Your grandmother is happy for you.

Here is a question. Did your grandmother contribute to the doctor you became? Of course she did. Without her, you wouldn't have learned to read at five. You would have learned later, with someone else, and maybe you would have become a different doctor, or maybe not a doctor at all.

Now here is the strange part. The world has no way to pay her for what she did. Money knows your salary. Money knows the bill the patient paid the hospital. Money does not know that your grandmother taught you to read. Money cannot see her.

This isn't just about your grandmother. Money cannot see most of the people who helped you become who you are. It cannot see the teacher who explained fractions when you were eight. It cannot see the friend who told you it was okay to be sad. It cannot see the librarian who picked the book that changed how you thought. None of these people get paid for what they did, because money doesn't know how to count it.

Money has been like this for five thousand years. We've been very used to it for so long that most of us never noticed it was strange.

Money sees who held the contract at the moment of payment. It does not see who actually made the value that was being paid for.

2.What we couldn't do before

People have been noticing this problem for a long time. Why didn't anyone fix it?

Because it was technically impossible. To pay your grandmother for teaching you to read, you'd have to know that her teaching helped you, and you'd have to know how much, and you'd have to be able to send her a tiny payment whenever you used what she taught you. Doing this for one grandmother is hard. Doing it for everyone — for every grandmother, every teacher, every friend who ever helped anyone — is impossible.

For most of history, the technology to track all those connections didn't exist. We had to pick the best simple thing instead. We picked money. Money tracks the simple thing — who has the dollar bill — and ignores everything else. It's the best honest answer we could give when the question was too hard for us to answer well.

But the question isn't too hard for us anymore. The technology to answer it just arrived.

3.Three new things that change everything

In the last few years, three new technologies all became possible at the same time. Each one was important on its own. Together, they make the impossible thing possible.

Computers that can read and understand. Modern AI can read a book and figure out which other books it learned from. It can read a paper and identify the older papers it built on. It can listen to a song and recognize the older songs that shaped its melody. This used to take a historian working for years. Now it takes a computer working for seconds.

Computers that can hold huge maps of connections. Imagine a giant map of every person in the world, with lines connecting people who influenced each other. The map has billions of dots and trillions of lines. Until recently, computers couldn't hold a map that big. Now they can.

Ways to send tiny amounts of money around the world. If you wanted to send your grandmother three cents from her share of your salary every month, you couldn't. The cost of sending the three cents would be more than three cents. Now, with new payment systems, you can send three cents to anyone in the world in less than a second, for less than a thousandth of a cent.

Put these three together, and you have something new. You can know what helped what, you can store that knowledge at the scale of the whole world, and you can send tiny payments to anyone, anywhere. The technical things that needed to exist before we could fix money exist now.

4.What Koin does

Koin is a name for the system that uses these three new technologies together. Koin doesn't replace money. Koin sits on top of money and adds something money was missing.

Here's the basic idea. When someone gets paid for something — for a book, a song, a doctor's visit, a meal — Koin uses the new technology to figure out who helped make that thing possible. It traces backward through the connections. It finds the teachers, the friends, the inspirations, the helpers. It splits a small part of the payment among all of them.

So when your doctor gets paid, a tiny part of that payment goes to your grandmother for teaching you to read, which made it possible for you to study medicine. Another tiny part goes to the teacher who taught the medical school class that taught you how to diagnose. Another tiny part goes to the doctor whose research paper your treatment was based on. Another tiny part goes to that doctor's teacher. And so on, going backward through everyone who, in some way, made your doctor possible.

Each tiny payment is small. But your grandmother gets these tiny payments from everyone she helped, and everyone they helped, all the time. Add up all the tiny payments and you get a real income. An income that pays your grandmother for the actual work she did, even though the work happened decades ago and no one wrote it on a contract.

5.A story about a recipe

Helena's tiradito

Helena was a cook in Lima, Peru. She had been cooking for twenty-three years, ten hours a day, for not much money. She loved cooking but she was tired. Her younger daughter was about to start university and Helena didn't know how she would pay for it.

One day in December, Helena wrote down a recipe. It was for tiradito — a Peruvian dish made of raw fish — with mango and a special sauce she had been working on for years. She submitted the recipe to a new system that used Koin.

In this system, kitchens around Latin America could read Helena's recipe and cook it. Every time a kitchen sold a plate of Helena's tiradito, a tiny payment went back to Helena. Three cents, sometimes seven, sometimes ten. By itself, three cents is nothing. But thousands of plates were sold every week.

The first month, Helena got twelve dollars. The second month, thirty-eight dollars. The third month, seventy-five dollars. By the end of the first year, the recipe was earning her more than her job. By the end of the second year, she quit her job and only worked when she felt like it.

Helena's daughter graduated without debt. Helena bought her mother a better house. The recipe is still being cooked tonight, in kitchens across South America, and Helena, now sixty-six years old, is still being paid for the meal she wrote down on a piece of paper one afternoon in 2031.

And here's the part that might surprise you. Helena named her prep cook, a man named Eladio, as a co-author of the recipe. He had helped her perfect it — he said the sauce needed less acid and more salt, and he was right. Eladio got 8% of Helena's earnings for the rest of his life. When he died, his daughter received that 8%. His grandson became a chef partly because of the money Helena's tiradito sent his family. And the grandson's own work, now, is sending tiny payments back through that chain, back to Helena.

The tiradito Helena wrote down one afternoon turned into a river of small payments that keeps feeding the people who helped her, and the people they helped, and so on, for as long as the recipe keeps being cooked.

6.What this would mean for you

You are a kid right now. Maybe you've never thought about earning a living. But you will, someday. And the world you grow into is going to be different depending on whether systems like Koin exist.

If Koin exists, here are some things that might be true for you:

Your contributions count from the start. The moment you do something that helps someone else — even something small — the system can notice it. The question you asked your teacher that helped another student understand the lesson. The joke you told that made your cousin laugh and feel better about a hard day. The story you wrote that one friend really loved. All of these are tiny contributions. In a Koin world, they leave traces in the system, and the traces can grow into real income over time.

You can be paid for the impact of your work, not just the work itself. If you write a song at fifteen and it becomes a song that other songwriters draw from for the next thirty years, you keep getting paid for thirty years. You don't have to keep performing it. You don't have to keep promoting it. The system pays you based on what your song actually did in the world.

You don't have to be famous to be paid. Today, most people who contribute to the world's value are invisible. They make things, do things, help people, and the bookkeeping never sees them. In a Koin world, you can be a quiet person who does important work and still be paid for it.

The people who helped you can finally be paid. Your grandmother. Your favorite teacher. The friend who taught you how to think about hard problems. The person who gave you the book that changed your life. All of them, in a Koin world, get small payments from the work you do.

7.The hard questions

You're old enough to think about hard questions. Let me share a few that adults are still arguing about.

Will this be like a video game where everyone gets a score and people are mean to each other about their scores? The worry is that Koin will turn into a system where everyone is constantly comparing how much they've contributed, and people with low scores will be looked down on. The defense is that Koin pays you — it doesn't decide who you're allowed to talk to or where you're allowed to go. The score is just an income. Your friends are still your friends. Your value as a person isn't your Koin balance. The system has to keep that separation, and people have to defend it. If Koin ever starts being used to decide who deserves things other than payment, the system has gone wrong and people need to fix it.

What if the computer makes mistakes about who deserves what? It will. Computers always make mistakes. The system has built-in ways to fix mistakes: anyone can challenge a decision, a small group of people reviews it, the decision can be reversed. The system gets better over time as it learns from its mistakes. It will never be perfect; it will be much, much better than what we have now.

What about people who can't be tracked? This is a real problem. Some of the most important work in the world happens privately, between two people, in conversations no one writes down. A friend who comforts another friend. A mother who raises a child. A friend who helps another friend with a problem. The system can't fully trace these things. It tries to handle them — there is a concept called "ancestor unspecified" where the system credits people even when their specific names can't be recovered — but it's imperfect. Some important contributions will remain invisible. The hope is that most of the previously invisible contributions become visible, even if not all of them.

What about people who do bad things? Koin pays people for the help they give to others. It doesn't punish people for hurting others. A person who hurts people doesn't get negative Koin; they just have a small Koin balance because they haven't contributed much. Punishing bad behavior is the job of the legal system, not Koin. The reason Koin doesn't punish is that the moment it does, it becomes a different kind of system, one that decides who's allowed to do things. That would be bad. Koin pays. It doesn't gate.

8.Why you might want to learn more

Koin doesn't fully exist yet. There are small pilots — in schools, in music labels, in caregiving programs — but the big, world-changing version is still being built. The next ten years will decide whether it gets built in a way that helps everyone, or whether companies build closed versions that mostly help themselves.

This matters for you in a specific way. The people who are building Koin right now are mostly adults. The people who will inherit it are kids. The decisions being made now will affect what your adult life looks like.

If Koin gets built well — open, fair, owned by everyone — your generation will grow up in a world where contribution is finally counted. Your future grandmother (when you become a grandparent someday) will be paid by the work of the children she helps raise. Your teachers will be paid by the careers their students build. Your own work, whatever you decide to do, will be paid for what it actually does.

If Koin gets built badly — closed, controlled by a few companies — your generation will grow up in a slightly improved version of the current world. Better than nothing, but not as good as it could have been.

If Koin doesn't get built at all — if everyone decides it's too hard or too risky — your generation will grow up in mostly the current world. The bookkeeping will still be wrong. The teachers and grandmothers and friends who help shape the world will still be invisible to the system that decides who gets paid.

Your generation has more say in this than you might think. The kids who grow up understanding what's at stake will be the adults who, in twenty years, are running the institutions that make Koin work or fail. The earlier you understand it, the better your generation will steer the outcome.

9.Three things you can do

You're young. Most of the work of building Koin will be done by adults. But there are three things you can do, even now, that matter.

Be the kind of person who notices invisible contribution. When you see someone helping someone else — your teacher staying late, your bus driver going out of their way, your friend's parent listening to you talk about your day — notice it. Say thank you. Tell other people about it. The first thing Koin needs is people who already pay attention to the contributions that the bookkeeping can't see. You don't need any technology for that. You just need to look.

Ask questions when things don't make sense. When you hear that a teacher makes less money than someone who plays games on the internet, ask why. When you hear that the people who built the internet — the open-source programmers — aren't paid by the companies that use their code, ask why. The questions aren't rude; they're how we figure out what to fix. Adults who can't answer them honestly are part of the problem. Adults who can answer them honestly are the ones building the future.

Learn things. Make things. Help people. All three of these will leave traces in the world, even if you can't see them now. The traces are going to matter — to you, to the people you helped, to the people they help. The system that finally pays for all of this is being built. Your contributions will count, whether you can see them counting now or not.

You don't have to wait until you're an adult to make the world bigger. You make it bigger every time you help someone. Koin is the system that, when it works, will finally notice.

If you want to read more about Koin, there is a longer book for adults called The Ledger of Minds: A Manifesto for Koin. It is hard in places. The math is hard. The history is long. But the ideas are the same as the ones in this short version. You can read it now or you can read it in five years. The argument will still be here.
