How to read

Why are some languages "disconnected"?
Seven different language families are shown. Each family has its own root (marked ›) and no demonstrated common ancestor to the others. Indo-European is the biggest; Uralic (Finnish, Hungarian) is a separate family that ended up geographically inside Europe; Sinitic, Austronesian, Semitic, Turkic, and Japonic are all linguistically independent. Their separation is the historical fact, not a rendering bug.
Label styles when you pick a meaning
Solid green — the word is attested/recorded directly for that language.
Faded italic with ~ — the word is inherited from the nearest ancestor that has a record. Useful as a plausibility estimate when specific vocab is missing.

Compound words

How words actually form
High-frequency pairs get lexicalized — "break + fast" → "breakfast", "good + bye" (God be with you) → "goodbye", "you + me" → "us". Pick two meanings and a base language below to see how the compound is built, then apply sound shifts to see it erode into a single unrecognizable unit. How it works

Pick a starting language above, then press ▶ Play (or Step). Each generation, eligible words undergo one real phonological rule — intervocalic voicing, lenition, palatalization before front vowels, etc. Click any word to see which rule produced it and why (articulatory reason).

Words that accumulate enough drift spawn daughter languages (new colors). Neighboring languages sometimes borrow each other's words (dashed green lines). Watch for real historical patterns: Grimm's Law, Great Vowel Shift, palatalization, final devoicing.
