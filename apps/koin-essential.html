Money is the wrong
instrument. We finally
have the right one.

A thirty-page distillation of the case that the economy is, before anything else, a verdict on what each person contributed — and that the technology to render that verdict honestly just arrived.

The argument in one page

An economy is a verdict on who contributed what. Money has rendered this verdict for five thousand years using one signal: who held the contract at the moment of exchange. That signal is a poor stand-in for actual contribution. Teachers, caregivers, friends, mentors, the dead, the originators of recipes, the writers of forgotten papers — none are seen by money. The bookkeeper is not a judge.

Three technologies just arrived at once: models that can read influence, graphs that can hold provenance at scale, settlement rails that can move pennies globally. Together they make the verdict computable. We can now trace, with reasonable fidelity, what work moved which mind, and route a fraction of any downstream benefit back to the source.

Koin is the proposal that we do this — automatically, openly, jointly held, non-extractive, refusable. Not a token. Not a blockchain. A graph of bonds, queried by spreading activation, settled by any payment rail. Nobody pays into Koin; the world simply notes, in the ledger, where value originated, and routes it home.

The system pays. It does not gate. The unmeasured is preserved. The window to build it openly, before someone closes it, is now.

I — The judge problemAn economy is, before anything else, a verdict.

Every dollar that changes hands is a small judgment: this person contributed something worth that much. Every paycheck is a verdict. Every market price is a verdict. The aggregate of all those verdicts — that is what we mean by the economy.

For all of history, we have rendered those verdicts with the crudest possible instrument: money. Money does not see contribution; it sees custody. It records who held the contract at the moment of exchange, not who produced the value being exchanged. When you pay for a book, the money goes to the publisher, to the platform, to the printer. A sliver reaches the author. Nothing reaches the librarian who shaped the author at fifteen, the friend whose sentence became the central argument, the dead philosopher whose framework gave the book its shape. Money cannot see them. It was never designed to.

The wild proxies have produced a world where a teacher who shaped a thousand minds earns less than a consultant who reshuffled the same money between two corporations. A nurse who held a dying patient's hand earns less than the man who traded the hospital's debt. A philosopher whose framework underwrites half a century of policy earns less than the marketer of an energy drink. These are not anomalies to be patched. They are the system functioning exactly as designed — because the system was never designed to judge contribution. It was designed to clear transactions.

We never had a judge. We had a bookkeeper. We confused them because we had no choice.

What does someone actually contribute? Sometimes a thing — a meal, a chair, a roof. Sometimes a service — a haircut, a diagnosis, an hour of patience. Sometimes a piece of attention — a glance that caught a falling child, an answer to a stranger's question. Sometimes an idea — a recipe, a method, a sentence that lodged in a friend's head and became, ten years later, a company.

The substrate of the economy is not money. It is the universe of these contributions, most of which are invisible because we have never had instruments fine enough to see them. Money is what condensed out of the substrate when the substrate became too complicated to track by memory. We have been mistaking the condensation for the thing.

The most invisible contributions are the upstream ones — the teaching, the influence, the framework, the example. They produce nothing the bookkeeper can see; they produce only changed minds. And changed minds are where every other contribution comes from.

Why was this allowed to continue for five thousand years? Because it was technologically impossible to do better. To judge a contribution accurately you have to trace its effects — through the people it touched, the work they did, the people they touched, recursively. Until very recently, this was a labor only history could perform, and only in retrospect, and only for the people history happened to remember. Every contribution that fell outside history's narrow attention was lost. We fell back on proxies, knowing they were rough, having no choice. The mismeasurement was structural.

II — Why we can measure it nowThe shift has happened.

Three technologies just became possible at once. Each was independently impossible thirty years ago. Together they are the apparatus that makes Koin computable.

Models that read influence. Modern foundation models can ingest a text, a corpus, a recorded conversation, and infer with reasonable fidelity what shaped it — what writers it echoes, what arguments it inherits, what frameworks it presupposes. The same models that worry copyright lawyers are the instruments that make Koin's verdict possible. They can do, in seconds, the source-tracing labor that a historian could only do in years and only for famous works.

Graphs that hold provenance at scale. Modern graph databases can hold billions of nodes and tens of billions of edges, traversed in milliseconds. Spreading activation runs on such graphs and finds upstream contributors for an arbitrary benefit in real time. The infrastructure is no longer the bottleneck.

Settlement rails that route pennies globally. Stripe Connect, Wise, Lightning Network, Visa Direct, central-bank digital currencies — all of these can route a fraction of a cent to a contributor on the other side of the world in seconds, without a bank deciding to allow it. Micropayments, a fantasy in the 1990s, are an engineering detail now.

What used to take a historian working for a decade can now be done in the runtime of a request. The judgment is no longer guesswork. It can be computed, from the evidence, with the same rigor we now apply to weather and protein folding.

Koin is the proposal that we use this capability for what it was always for: render an honest verdict on what each person contributed, and let the economy clear on the basis of that verdict instead of the proxies we used while we waited.

The window has a closing date. By the early 2030s, several major platforms will have launched closed Koin-like products. By the mid-2030s, those products will have established defaults, vendor lock-in, and network effects that newer entrants — open or closed — will struggle to overcome. The federated open Koin must either be built and at scale before then, or the world will receive a centralized version of the same idea, which will produce most of the gains for most contributors but capture significant margin and gatekeeping power for the platform operators. The window is roughly four to seven years.

III — What Koin isA graph, not a chain. A flow, not a score. An apparatus, not an authority.

Koin is a graph, not a chain. The record of who-influenced-whom is held in a weighted directed graph of contributions and receivers. Each edge has a magnitude (how much one moved the other), a timestamp, a channel (read, listened, watched, conversed), and a decay (older influence fades unless reinforced).

Koin is a flow, not a score. Your "balance" is not a number that summarizes you. It is the sum of the small streams arriving from every contribution you have made that is still doing work in the world. When you contribute, you open a stream. When the contribution is forgotten or superseded, the stream slows and stops. The integral of your streams is what we call your wealth — but the instrument is the flows, not the integral.

Koin is an apparatus, not an authority. There is no Koin Bank deciding what each contribution is worth. The verdict is rendered by the graph itself: the magnitude of an edge is computed from the evidence — what the receiver did next, what their work credited, what the chain of effect looks like downstream. The judgment is automatic and contestable, the way a scientific measurement is automatic and contestable.

To be precise about what Koin is not:

It is not Patreon. Patreon pays the producer of a stream. Koin pays everyone whose work is now part of the producer's mind.

It is not royalties. Royalties pay the named author. Koin pays the unnamed teacher who taught the author to read.

It is not universal basic income. UBI is a redistribution from production to existence. Koin is a redistribution from downstream to upstream — from those who captured value to those who created it.

It is not a wealth tax. A wealth tax extracts by political decree. Koin redistributes by computation, from the captured to the original, by tracing what actually moved the world.

It is not a token. Koin is a unit of account, not a currency. You can pay koin in dollars, euros, bitcoin, time, calories — anything that can be metered. The substance does not matter. The accounting does.

It is not a blockchain. Blockchains solved a different problem (distributed consensus on a single ledger). Koin needs a different primitive (efficient backward-traversal of a richly-weighted graph). The relevant data structure is the influence graph, queried by spreading activation, the same primitive a brain uses to remember.

You are paid by every mind your mind made better. You pay every mind that ever made yours better. The economy is the integral.

IV — How the verdict is madeThe math, in plain language.

The system that decides how much each contributor is paid works in six steps. The full mathematics — Lagrangians, Shapley values, spreading activation, decay functions — is in the long version. The plain-language description is here.

Step one: the system needs evidence. Nothing enters the Koin ledger without an absorption event — a moment where one mind demonstrably encountered the work of another. The events are recorded: who read what, who watched what, who cited what, who acknowledged what. The recording is opt-in; the source and the receiver both consent before any edge is added to the graph. If there is no consented evidence of absorption, there is no Koin flow.

Step two: the system builds an influence graph. Every consented absorption becomes a directed edge with a weight. The weight encodes how much the receiver was moved — measured by what they did afterward, what they cited, what their downstream work shows traces of. A casual read produces a small weight; a foundational shaping produces a large weight.

Step three: when a benefit happens, the system traces it backward. A benefit is a moment of value-creation: a book is sold, a song is streamed, a lesson causes a student outcome, a paper is cited. When the benefit happens, the system starts at the node where the benefit landed and propagates credit backward through the incoming edges of the influence graph. The propagation is recursive: credit reaches the immediate upstream contributor, then their upstream contributors, then theirs, and so on, with the amount diminishing at each hop. This is the algorithm the brain uses to remember (spreading activation) and the algorithm Google uses to rank pages (PageRank). Same primitive, different domain.

Step four: the system corrects for redundancy. If two upstream contributors independently provided the same insight, neither should be credited as if they were the sole source. The Shapley adjustment — a Nobel-Prize-winning piece of game theory from 1953 — computes each contributor's marginal value: how much of the benefit was uniquely caused by their presence. Each contributor is paid for what their presence added that no one else's presence would have added.

Step five: the system applies decay. Old contributions that nobody is currently absorbing weigh less than new contributions that are being absorbed widely. Old work is paid for its current use, not for its existence. Foundational work that continues to be absorbed retains weight indefinitely; genuinely obsolete work fades.

Step six: the system pays. The computed credit is settled in whatever payment substrate the contributor has registered — dollars, euros, bitcoin, anything metered. The settlement is atomic: the same instant the benefit is recorded, the credit is computed and the payment is dispatched. The contributor sees the new flow in their account, with an explanatory record of what triggered it and how the amount was calculated.

An example. A reader pays $20 for a book. The system identifies the book; identifies its bibliographic chain; identifies the editor; identifies the writing teacher the author named in acknowledgments; identifies the three friends who shaped the manuscript; identifies the production chain. Spreading activation traces credit backward, Shapley adjusts for redundancy, decay accounts for age. The result, computed in milliseconds when the purchase clears: $8.40 to the author, $3.10 across the bibliography, $2.60 to the editor, $1.40 to the writing teacher from twenty years ago, $0.95 across the three friends, $1.20 to the production chain, $0.85 to the publisher, $1.10 to the bookstore, $0.40 to the lineage commons. Total: $20. Every penny allocated; no platform captures a structural margin; the upstream contributors finally visible to the bookkeeping.

The judgment is automatic, traceable, contestable, and continuously revisable. It does not depend on anyone's good will. It depends on the graph being honest and the algorithm being open.

V — Why you can trust it (and why it's hard to steal)

Trust in Koin is not a leap of faith. It is a verifiable property of an open system.

The algorithm is open. The code that turns "this work was absorbed" into "this much Koin flows" is open-source. You can read it. You can run it yourself on the same inputs and verify that it produces the same outputs. You can compare it to alternative algorithms. The federation specification mandates this; any provider that closes their algorithm loses federation status and the trust that comes with it. The verdict cannot be made in secret.

The graph is auditable. Every edge in the influence graph has a source, a destination, a weight, a timestamp, a channel, and cryptographic signatures from the consenting parties. Edges cannot be created without consent; edges cannot be silently modified after the fact. The graph is, in effect, a tamper-evident log of consented influence events.

Disputes are resolvable. If you think you have been mis-credited, you file a dispute. An arbitration committee reviews the evidence and renders a binding judgment, which can be appealed. The whole process is transparent; the rulings become public precedent that adjusts how future verdicts are made. The system gets better over time because the disputes teach the algorithm.

No one owns the graph. The federation specification requires that the underlying influence graph be jointly held under commons governance — like the calendar, like the metric system, like TCP/IP. Multiple providers compete on quality of judgment, not on owning the data. A contributor can switch providers and bring their graph history with them. This prevents the lock-in that has historically captured every centralized ledger.

Now consider a different question entirely. Money has a property that is so familiar most people forget it is a property: it can be stolen. A pickpocket takes your cash; a phishing email drains your bank account; a stolen credit card buys goods until the bank notices. The reason money is stealable is that money is a bearer token of value. Once someone has the token, they have the value.

Koin does not have this property. There is no stored pile of koin. Your "balance" is the running integral of evidence about what you have contributed, computed continuously from the influence graph. Past flows have already been paid out; future flows have not yet been computed. There is no warehouse to break into.

To "steal" your koin, an attacker would have to compromise your identity (which the cryptographic architecture defends against), falsify the influence graph (which the tamper-evident signing prevents), or redirect your flows (which time-locks and multisig make hard). Compare to a bank account: one good phishing email and your savings go to Belarus. Koin has no Belarus-wiring equivalent because the "balance" is not a transferable thing; it is the output of a public computation. You cannot take what isn't there to be taken.

And there is a deeper point. A system that removes the structural possibility of theft does not just reduce crime statistics; it dissolves a moral dilemma humans have lived with for as long as there have been ledgers. The current bookkeeping puts theft in front of every person, every day, as a continuously available temptation. Most decline; some do not; the rest of us pay the cost in locks, alarms, alarms-on-the-locks, insurance, suspicion of strangers, and the cognitive overhead of constant low-grade vigilance.

Koin changes the structural payoff. The thing that was tempting becomes architecturally unavailable. People in a Koin world will be more honest not because they are better people but because the situation no longer rewards dishonesty. This is the same kind of structural moral improvement that locks (which dissolve some theft without requiring more virtue), vaccines (which dissolve some disease without requiring more immunity), and seatbelts (which dissolve some death without requiring better driving) produced. You do not have to make people better. You have to build a world in which "being worse" no longer has a payoff.

Money is stealable because money is a thing held. Koin is mostly not stealable because koin is a thing computed. The architecture of the verdict changes the architecture of what can be done to the verdict.

VI — Nobody paysThe video-game insight, and what the trade leaves behind.

In a well-designed video game, you gain. Other players do not lose. You level up, find loot, complete a quest — the world's total has gone up, and nobody had to give anything back. This is so natural inside the game that no one questions it. Outside the game, we have inherited the opposite intuition: every dollar in your pocket came from someone else's pocket. The pie is fixed. You are taking. Both intuitions are partial truths, and the video-game one is closer to the actual physics.

When a chef writes a recipe and it cooks a thousand meals, no recipe-pool was depleted. When a teacher's framework shapes a thousand students, no framework-pool was depleted. The supply of ideas, of teaching, of attention, of care — these are not finite reservoirs that can be drained. Money, the instrument, is fixed-pool. Contribution, the thing money was trying to track, is not. Money inherited zero-sum semantics from the time when wealth meant grain and grain was finite. The semantics never updated.

Koin updates them. The koin that flows to the chef when her recipe cooks a meal is not subtracted from the diner — the diner has the meal. It is not subtracted from the kitchen worker — the worker is paid. It is recognition that the world's stock of fed people has gone up by one, and that the originating idea is the reason. Nobody pays. The world simply notes, in the ledger, where the increase came from.

This isn't speculation. Imagine two villages, before money. Each finds, in its own territory, a small object that exists nowhere else — a particular stone, a colored shell, an alloy only their soil holds. When the villages meet and trade, each gives the other its unique object. After the exchange, both villages possess something the other made possible. A third village, arriving later, sees that the first two have each other's tokens — and knows, without being told, that the two have met, traded, bonded. The artifact of trade is the record of the bond. Obsidian found a thousand miles from the volcano it came out of, amber from the Baltic in Egypt, Pacific shells in the Mississippi Valley — every archaeological trade route was first inferred from a foreign object showing up in a local context. The objects were testimony.

Koin is that ledger, scaled to billions of contributions and updated continuously. When your idea moves my mind, the flow between us is not a tax I pay; it is the marker that we traded. The flow is the relationship made visible. The economy is not a substance being moved around. It is a graph of bonds being recorded.

One last concern, the most familiar one. Anyone who has seen the Black Mirror episode about social ratings will worry: isn't this just Nosedive? The worry is reasonable. The differences are precise.

Nosedive measures impression: did the encounter please me. Koin measures effect: did the work actually move what came after. Nosedive compresses a person into one number. Koin does not compress — there is a flow per contribution, per receiver, decayed by time and channel. Nosedive gates access — a low rating means you cannot rent a car. Koin only pays. A low Koin balance means your ideas are not reaching people; it does not mean someone is permitted to refuse to serve you. Nosedive is symmetric: anyone can rate anyone. Koin is causal: only people who actually absorbed your work can pay you. Nosedive is mandatory. Koin is refusable. And Koin tracks contribution, not harm. A predator does not get a negative balance; he simply has a low one because he contributed little. Harm is the territory of justice, not economics. The moment a contribution ledger becomes a permission ledger it has become Nosedive, regardless of what the numbers measure.

The point is not to measure everything. It is to stop pretending we are measuring nothing.

VII — What disappears, what appears

Rent extraction disappears. The platform that owns the distribution can no longer take 70% of the price for the act of distribution. Under Koin, distribution is one contribution among thousands, weighted by its actual impact on the receiver.

Gatekeeping disappears. The publisher, the agent, the algorithm — these are intermediaries between idea and mind. Their value, where real, is paid. Their leverage, where extractive, evaporates. They cannot capture the value of the idea any more than the postal worker can capture the value of the letter.

Pseudonymous parasitism disappears. The aggregator that scrapes a thousand minds and repackages their thinking under its own banner can no longer be compensated as the originator. The compensation flows past it, to the minds whose thinking it aggregated.

And in their place, what appears:

The teacher gets paid for the rest of her life, by the world her students went on to build.

The friend whose remark sparked the company gets paid by the company.

The mathematician whose paper was forgotten for fifty years gets paid the instant the paper is re-discovered and put to use.

The translator whose translation made the philosopher legible to a new continent gets paid for every reader the philosopher reaches in that continent.

The grandmother who taught a generation of children to read is paid by the work that literacy made possible — quietly, anonymously, for the rest of her life, and after.

The dead get paid into the work of their descendants. Where the descendants are biological, this is inheritance. Where they are intellectual, this is the academy as it has always wished it could be.

Children are pre-positioned in Koin. Every account is opened at birth. The child has no balance for years — but the moment her first idea reaches another mind, the system credits her. She does not have to wait for adulthood to be an economic participant. She was one the day she was conscious enough to influence.

The dead continue to be paid. Their accounts do not close. The flow continues to their named heirs or to a lineage fund — a small endowment that supports the people their work touched. The dead, in Koin, are richer than they were in life, and their wealth continues to circulate.

But the most important thing that appears is not on the ledger at all. Koin must preserve the unmeasured. The friend you sit with in the dark. The hour given to a dying parent without account. The line in the journal no one reads. The kindness shown to a stranger that may have prevented something terrible from happening, which neither of you will ever know. These do not enter Koin. They cannot. Some resist measurement because no graph could honestly capture them. Others should not be measured even if they could — because to measure them would corrupt them. A Koin world in which everything is on the ledger is a Koin world that has failed.

The promise of Koin is not that the ledger covers everything. It is that the ledger covers what money was trying and failing to cover, and leaves the rest alone.

VIII — The recipeA worked example.

To see what an accurate verdict looks like, take the smallest case where it is actually achievable today.

A chef writes down a recipe. Under the old economy, three things can happen to it. She can cook it herself — selling one plate at a time, capped by her own hands and time. She can sell the rights to a restaurant or a packaged-food company — a single check, after which she watches the dish become someone else's revenue. She can publish it in a book — a 10% royalty on each copy of the book, but no royalty at all on the millions of dinners cooked from the page.

All three are bad verdicts. The recipe did the work — every plate is a re-running of her idea — but the verdict her income reflects has almost nothing to do with how many of those plates the world ate.

Now imagine the recipe is submitted to a system. An AI-driven kitchen reads it and executes it: a worker preps measured ingredients into labeled bowls; the machine combines, heats, times, finishes, packages. The chef does not have to be present. The recipe runs anywhere a kitchen runs it. And every unit sold pays a royalty — small, automatic, continuous — back to the chef. Her income is no longer bounded by her stove or her time; it is bounded by how many people her idea fed. The judgment is accurate because the trace is unambiguous: this recipe produced this plate produced this transaction. The economy gets the verdict right, mechanically, every meal.

This is the entire Koin thesis at small scale. Make the trace visible; the verdict follows. Generalize the pattern: the lesson plan that produced the student's grade. The diagnostic protocol that produced the patient's recovery. The proof technique that produced the engineer's design. The argument that produced the voter's choice. The melody that produced the album that paid for the singer's house — and the unnamed nursery rhyme inside the melody that paid for nothing.

Every artifact in the world is a recipe being run. Koin is the apparatus that lets the verdict find the cook.

IX — The lineageWe are not the first to see this.

This idea is old. We are not the first to see that money fails to track contribution. The synthesis is new; the sightings are not.

Henry George (1879) saw that the value of land comes from the community around it, not from the owner — and proposed that the rents extracted should flow back to the community. He was eighty percent of the way to Koin, applied to one asset class.

Marcel Mauss (1925) studied the gift economies of the Pacific and showed that gifts created bonds — that the object of exchange carried obligation, identity, history. He saw the artifact-of-trade-is-the-record-of-bond pattern in cultures that had never abandoned it.

Karl Polanyi (1944) traced the "disembedding" of the economy from social relations — the historical moment when markets became autonomous from the obligations they used to be part of. Koin proposes one form of re-embedding.

Lewis Hyde (1983) argued that creative work belongs to a gift economy that runs underneath the commercial one. Koin operationalizes Hyde's distinction.

Ronald Coase (1937) argued that the size of firms is determined by transaction costs. Koin drives transaction costs for influence attribution toward zero.

Elinor Ostrom (1990) showed that the commons is not doomed — human communities have managed shared resources for centuries without privatization or state control. Her eight design principles map almost one-to-one to non-extractive Koin governance.

Citation networks in the academy have been a primitive Koin since the seventeenth century — unpaid, unsettled, but tracking influence across generations of thought, surviving the death of every participant.

PageRank (Brin & Page, 1998) applied spreading activation to the web at planetary scale and proved that the mathematical kernel of Koin can be computed efficiently for billions of nodes.

ASCAP (1914) and the music royalty system have been a working, century-old proof that the influence-trace-to-royalty pattern operates at country scale. Add a richer trace, add more substrates, and you have Koin.

Each of these saw a piece. Each ran into the wall of the technologies of their time. The synthesis was not available because the apparatus was not available. The apparatus is here now. Koin is the synthesis these earlier sightings have been waiting for.

X — Objections, consideredThe hardest pushbacks, answered.

A manifesto that ducks its objections is propaganda. The strongest arguments against Koin deserve direct answers.

"This is surveillance." The graph is, by definition, a record of what moved you. But Koin is opt-in, the data is encrypted, and the verdict can be computed without revealing identity through zero-knowledge proofs. The right comparison is not "Koin vs nothing" but "Koin vs the current attention-harvesting baseline." Koin gives the contributor a payment in exchange for visibility that ad networks already harvest for free.

"It will be gamed." Any system of value can be gamed. The relevant question is whether the gaming is worse than what it replaces. Money is gamed at industrial scale — tax evasion, market manipulation, transfer pricing, leveraged speculation that produces no value. Citation networks are gamed (citation rings, predatory journals); PageRank was gamed (link farms); in every case the response was iterative improvement, not abandonment. Koin's specific defense: the verdict is keyed to verifiable downstream effect, which is harder to fake than a star rating because the receiver's later behavior either does or does not show absorption. The cost of gaming Koin convincingly is roughly the cost of actually contributing.

"It corrupts giving." This worry comes from the gift-economy tradition — that putting a number on a gift destroys what makes it a gift. The objection is sound but mis-aimed. Koin's carve-out for the unmeasured is the answer: the system handles what was already commercial-adjacent (writing, music, teaching, advice, software) and leaves the genuinely intimate alone. The hour you give a friend in the dark is not a Koin event unless you decide to make it one — and the system has no way of finding out about it unless you tell it.

"It widens inequality. Popular voices get most of the koin." The most serious objection. Three answers. First, even unequal Koin is more equal than current capital — because current capital is captured almost entirely by intermediaries (platforms, publishers, employers) rather than originators. Returning value to originators, even unequally, redistributes from the very-rich corporate few to the moderately-rich influential many. Second, the haze (decay) primitive prevents perpetual concentration: contributions that stop being absorbed stop generating flow. Third, the Shapley adjustment penalizes redundancy — a contribution everyone has heard reaches diminishing returns; a contribution that uniquely shapes a small but consequential audience can outpay a viral one. The math fights the winner-take-all dynamic.

"Royalties don't pay much." Existing royalty systems pay little because the rates are negotiated against captured distribution channels. A streamed song pays about $0.003; a book author gets 10% of cover. Both are artifacts of bargaining power asymmetries. Koin's structural change is removing the platform's leverage. When distribution is one weighted edge among many — not the gatekeeper — the verdict redistributes the full value of the contribution, not the leftover after rent.

"It will become Nosedive." The defense is institutional, not technical. Koin pays; it does not gate. The moment a Koin balance starts gating anything, that is the moment Koin has become something else and must be opposed. The same vigilance citizens needed to keep credit scores from determining medical care is what they will need to keep Koin balances from determining anything other than what arrives in the contributor's account.

"Won't it be coopted?" Three defenses: the graph must be commons (no single entity owns it), the verdict computation must be open (the judge cannot be private), the settlement must be plural (any payment rail can implement it). The race is real — closed implementations will be built — and they will lose only if the federated open version ships first and federates faster. This is the same race TCP/IP and HTTP won. It is winnable. It is not automatic.

The objections do not refute the proposal. They name the things we must defend if the proposal is to work.

XI — The askWhat we are asking you to do.

Build with Koin, even at small scale.

If you teach: talk to your district about a small pilot. If you write code: register your library with an attribution-aware platform. If you write or make music: document your influence graph in your acknowledgments. If you research: cite more carefully and argue for the federated standards. If you organize: draft model legislation. If you have capital: fund commons infrastructure rather than closed alternatives. If you have nothing in particular to offer: read carefully, argue with this document, be the kind of person who already, before the bookkeeping has caught up, sees what the bookkeeping is going to see.

Make it open. Make it standard. Refuse to own the graph.

The first system to do this honestly — and to do it without trying to own the ledger — is the system the world is going to choose. Not because anyone decreed it, but because once a worker is offered a system where the value she creates flows back to her, she will not voluntarily return to a system where it does not. Once a chef is offered a recipe royalty that pays her every meal, she will not voluntarily return to a one-time check. Once a teacher is offered compensation that scales with the lives she changed, she will not voluntarily return to a salary that does not see her students. Once an AI's training corpus is paid, the closed-corpus models that did not pay will be morally and competitively obsolete.

The window for shipping the open version before the closed ones consolidate is four to seven years. The window for shaping what gets built is now.

This is not a project. It is a responsibility. The same responsibility every generation has when handed a transition: to do the work, to leave something better, to refuse the easier path that locks the future in.

Humans are good at producing more than they consume — but only when the produce-more is paid for. When the produce-more is invisible to the economic system, humans stop producing it. We are smart enough to figure out that the system does not see us; we adapt accordingly. Centuries of unrewarded teachers, caregivers, parents, mentors, friends, librarians, midwives, mediators have, generation by generation, withdrawn or rationed their contributions. The cost is not measured in dollars; it is measured in the meals that did not get cooked, the conversations that did not happen, the children who were not taught, the questions that were not asked. A species that systematically fails to pay its upstream is a species that systematically dries up its source.

Koin is the first general-purpose system in human history that can pay the upstream. Not the only system. Not the perfect system. But the first that can do it computationally, at scale, with the fidelity that makes participation rational for the upstream contributor.

If we build it, we get a species that can finally afford its own teachers, its own caregivers, its own thinkers, its own conscience. If we don't — or if we let it be built closed — we get an extension of the current world, where every increment of technology widens the gap between custody and contribution and the species starves in the surplus.

The math is on our side. The history is on our side. The technology just arrived.
